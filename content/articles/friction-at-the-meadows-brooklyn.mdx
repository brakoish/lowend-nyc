# Friction at The Meadows Brooklyn

**Bushwick — March 2026**

Loving drum & bass in New York means accepting a certain loneliness. You'll be in London, or Bristol, or some random warehouse in Berlin, and the BPM will hit 174 and the whole room locks into this synchronized urgency that just doesn't happen at house or techno tempos. Then you fly back to JFK and remember: oh right, we don't really do this here.

We do, sometimes. And when Friction plays The Meadows this month, it's one of those nights where a UK heavyweight actually brings their A-game to Brooklyn instead of phoning it in for a festival slot.

## Who He Actually Is

Ed Keeley has been Friction for over twenty years. If you know DnB, you know why that matters. If you don't: he's not some circuit-riding DJ playing the hits. He's a cornerstone of the genre, the kind of artist whose early productions still get played today because they hold up.

Brighton teenager in the late 90s, cutting teeth on the local rave scene. By the early 2000s he was making records that actually moved things forward — "Light Speed," "Believe," "Jazzfreak." Tracks that balanced DnB's darker neurofunk side with something more musical without going soft. That balance is harder than it looks.

But Shogun Audio is the real story. Friction started the label in 2004 and ran it for two decades, releasing records by Rockwell, Icicle, SpectraSoul, Technimatic — basically the artists who defined how DnB sounded in the 2010s. Shogun had a vision: technical, melodic but not cheesy, underground but ambitious. Most labels don't last five years. Shogun stayed relevant for twenty.

Friction's own records followed that arc. His 2012 album *Led Astray* is still a high-water mark for vocal DnB — "Someone" with Arlissa, "Long Gone Memory" with Banks. Emotional stuff that still hits on a dancefloor. His remixes for Chase & Status, London Grammar, whoever — they all showed range without losing identity.

Then there was the BBC Radio 1 show. Years of breaking new records, platforming underground producers, connecting the genre's history to whatever was coming next. When Radio 1 started killing specialist shows, losing Friction hurt. It was one of the last places you could hear DnB presented properly to a mainstream audience.

## What You're Actually Getting

If you've never seen him: three decks, sometimes four. Fast mixing that never feels rushed. The ability to drop from liquid funk sweetness into neurofunk darkness without losing the thread. A record bag that spans decades but doesn't feel like a history lesson.

Friction plays the full spectrum. Liquid moments with soulful vocals and that specific melancholy DnB does better than anything else. Jump-up sections that get hands in the air. Stripped-back rollers that work the floor with precision. Dark neurofunk that sounds futuristic even when it's been around for years.

What ties it together is selection. Twenty-plus years of digging and playing. He knows what works, what doesn't, and when. His timing is sharp. Drops land when they should. Breakdowns breathe. Energy builds and releases in waves that feel inevitable even when they surprise you.

## The Room Matters

The Meadows isn't the biggest venue — maybe mid-hundreds capacity — but it's become the most reliable spot for DnB, dubstep, and UK-derived sounds in NYC. Bushwick has basically become the default home for bass music here, and this place is a big reason why.

The sound system is the thing. They invested early in proper bass weight. It's a long, narrow room that keeps the crowd close to the DJ, creates intimacy even when packed, channels energy toward the floor instead of letting it dissipate.

I've seen DnB struggle in bigger venues. There's something about the genre that works better when the room is full but not overwhelming, when you can see the DJ's hands, when the bass hits your chest directly. The Meadows gets this. They've been booking consistent DnB for years, building a crowd that knows what they're there for.

Bushwick itself matters. This isn't Manhattan clubland or bottle-service Williamsburg. It's still Brooklyn. The crowd at The Meadows is mixed — expat Brits missing London, Americans who found DnB through gaming or YouTube, old ravers who've been following this since the 90s. One of the few places in the city where drum & bass feels like a community instead of a curiosity.

## Why This Show Actually Matters

DnB has always had a weird relationship with America. Born in the UK, refined in London and Bristol, and despite decades of trying, it's never broken through to mainstream American consciousness the way house or techno has. You hear DnB influences in pop production, movie trailers, video games — but the actual scene stays underground, scattered pockets of true believers.

NYC's DnB history is especially patchy. We had moments — Direct Drive parties in the early 2000s, various one-offs over the years — but consistent infrastructure has been hard to maintain. Venues close. Promoters burn out or move on. The crowd exists but it's fragmented.

That's why nights like this matter. When someone of Friction's stature comes through, it's not just a gig. It's validation. A reminder that the genre has history, weight, artists who've dedicated their lives to this sound and can still command a room anywhere in the world. A rallying point for a community that doesn't always get to feel like one.

Friction specifically matters because he bridges eras. Not a legacy act playing old hits for nostalgia. Still releasing music, still involved with Shogun, still engaged with where the genre's going. His sets connect classic DnB to new productions pushing it forward. For a scene that sometimes feels stuck between reverence for the past and anxiety about the future, that connection matters.

## The Prediction

I'm expecting a masterclass. Friction doesn't play New York often, and when he does, he brings everything. The technical skill — multi-deck mixing, precise EQ work, weaving between subgenres without jarring transitions. But more than that, the intangible thing that separates good DJs from great ones: the sense that someone in the room actually gives a shit about where this night is going.

The Meadows will be packed. It always is for DnB nights, and Friction is the biggest name they've booked in a while. The sound system will get pushed. The crowd will be knowledgeable, responsive, ready for whatever he throws at them. And somewhere around 2 AM, probably during a devastating drop or perfectly timed vocal, there will be one of those moments where the whole room feels like a single thing.

That's why we go out. That's why we put up with the inconsistent infrastructure and the late nights and the fact that our favorite genre will never get the mainstream recognition it deserves.

Because when it works — when the DJ is right, the room is right, the sound is right — drum & bass delivers something no other genre can. Speed, weight, and musicality combining into genuine euphoria. Friction has been creating those moments for twenty years. He's very good at it.

If you're even slightly curious about drum & bass, this is the night to show up. If you're already a convert, you don't need me to tell you. See you in the dance.

**Rating: 4.5/5** — Essential for DnB heads, highly recommended for the curious. One of the genre's foundational artists in a room that understands what he's bringing.
