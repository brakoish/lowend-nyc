# KSHMR at Marquee: Big Room Royalty Descends on NYC's Most Notorious Nightclub

**Chelsea, Manhattan — March 28, 2025**

There's a particular type of energy that only exists when a producer who's spent the last decade dominating festival main stages steps into a nightclub. The scale changes. The intimacy shifts. What works for 50,000 people at Tomorrowland has to be recalibrated for a room that holds a fraction of that. Some artists shrink in that transition. Others adapt. KSHMR, I'm betting, will thrive.

He's at Marquee on March 28th, and honestly, it's about time.

## From The Cataracs to Kashmir

Niles Hollowell-Dhar's origin story is well-documented but worth repeating because it explains so much about his sound. Before he was KSHMR, he was Cyrano, one half of The Cataracs, the Bay Area production duo behind "Like a G6" and a string of mid-2000s hip-hop hits. He co-wrote for Selena Gomez. He understood pop structure, hook writing, the mechanics of what makes a track stick in someone's head.

Then in 2014, he disappeared. Or rather, he reappeared. Anonymous at first, releasing big room tracks on Spinnin' Records with no face attached. The music was immediate: "Tsunami," "Stampede," "Megalodon" — festival weapons built on massive synth stabs, Indian-influenced melodic hooks, and drops that seemed scientifically engineered to trigger serotonin release. When Tiësto outed him at Ultra 2015, the mystery dissolved but the momentum only accelerated.

The name KSHMR (pronounced "Kashmir") comes from his father's heritage — Kashmiri Pandit, emigrated from India. That connection isn't just biographical window dressing. It's woven into the DNA of his music. Tracks like "Jammu," "Kashmir," "Delhi," and "Bazaar" wear their South Asian influences openly, blending Bollywood melodicism with EDM's bombastic architecture. In a genre often criticized for cultural homogeneity, KSHMR's willingness to foreground his roots has always set him apart.

## The Sound: Cinematic and Unapologetic

Let's be clear about what KSHMR does because the critical establishment sometimes gets sniffy about it. This is big room house. It's future bass. It's progressive electro designed for peak-time moments when the crowd needs to feel like the roof might actually come off. It's not subtle. It's not meant for headphone contemplation. It's body music, built for rooms full of people who want to feel something massive together.

But within that framework, KSHMR operates with more sophistication than his detractors give him credit for. His arrangements are intricate, layered with orchestral elements, vocal chops, and melodic counterpoints that reveal themselves on repeated listens. He's a producer's producer — his Splice packs are among the platform's most downloaded, his tutorials and templates have educated a generation of bedroom producers.

The discography is extensive: "Secrets" with Tiësto, "Karate" with R3HAB, "Power" with Hardwell, "The Spook" with Bassjackers, "No Regrets" with Krewella and Yves V. His 2021 album *Harmonica Andromeda* showed range beyond the festival formula, and 2023's *KARAM* took a sharp left turn into Indian hip-hop, collaborating with Desi artists like KR$NA, Raftaar, and Seedhe Maut. The throughline is ambition — KSHMR refuses to stay in his lane, even when his lane has been extremely successful.

DJ Mag has consistently ranked him in their Top 100, peaking at #11 in 2021. He's played Coachella, Tomorrowland, EDC, Ultra, Sunburn — the global circuit's biggest stages. And through it all, he's maintained a connection to his craft that keeps him relevant even as big room's dominance has waxed and waned.

## The Room: Marquee Reborn

Marquee is one of those NYC institutions that everyone has an opinion about. Opened in 2003 in the heart of Chelsea's club district, it's been through multiple iterations, ownership changes, and cultural moments. For years it was synonymous with bottle service, celebrity sightings, and the excesses of Manhattan nightlife at its most visible.

But here's the thing: Marquee has also consistently booked serious electronic music. Through all the scene changes, the rise and fall of various micro-genres, they've maintained a commitment to big-room dance music that few Manhattan clubs can match. When big room was at its peak, Marquee was ground zero. When it supposedly "died," Marquee kept programming it anyway.

The recent renovation changes the equation. "Top-to-bottom" is the phrase they're using, and from what I've seen, it delivers. New sound system. Redesigned interior. A refreshed aesthetic that acknowledges we're in a different era of nightlife than when Marquee first opened. The Tao Group, which operates the venue, understands that NYC clubgoers have options now — Brooklyn warehouses, underground spots in Queens, DIY venues that didn't exist two decades ago. To compete, Marquee had to evolve.

The location still matters. 289 10th Avenue, right in the heart of Chelsea. Accessible but still feeling like a destination. The room itself — reportedly around 600 capacity for the main floor — is big enough to feel like an event but small enough that you can actually see the DJ. For an artist like KSHMR, used to playing to tens of thousands, that's a constraint that can spark creativity.

## Why This Show Matters

Big room house has had a complicated few years. The genre that dominated EDM's commercial peak in the early-to-mid-2010s faced a backlash as tastes shifted toward techno, house in its various underground forms, and bass music's more experimental edges. The term itself became almost pejorative in certain circles, shorthand for formulaic, overly commercial dance music.

But here's what I've learned watching this scene: everything comes back around. The kids who discovered electronic music through big room are now older, more experienced, nostalgic for the sound that first hooked them. Meanwhile, a new generation has come up without the baggage of the EDM wars, hearing these tracks fresh. And the producers who survived the genre's decline — the ones who kept evolving, kept releasing, kept touring — have earned a kind of veteran status.

KSHMR is firmly in that category. He never abandoned big room, but he never let it define him either. The *KARAM* album proved he could operate outside the formula. His continued production education work — the tutorials, the sample packs, the mentorship — positioned him as an elder statesman even while he's still in his mid-30s. He's one of the few artists from that 2014-2016 wave who's maintained both credibility and commercial viability.

Seeing him at Marquee feels like a statement. It's a reminder that big room didn't disappear; it just found its proper context. In a room this size, with a system this powerful, surrounded by people who know every drop before it happens — that's where this music makes sense. Not as background noise at a festival, not as algorithmic playlist filler, but as a shared experience in a dark room full of strangers.

## What to Expect

If you've never seen KSHMR live, here's the rundown. Expect a high-energy set that moves through his catalog — the classics like "Secrets" and "Karate," newer material from *KARAM* and recent singles, and plenty of IDs and unreleased tracks. He's a technically proficient DJ who understands pacing, building from atmospheric openings through to peak-time anthems.

The production value will be high. KSHMR's shows are known for their visual elements — the Indian-inspired imagery, the dramatic lighting, the sense of spectacle that matches the scale of his music. Even in a club setting, he brings a festival mindset to the presentation.

And the crowd? This will be a mix of longtime fans who've been following since the early Spinnin' days, newer listeners drawn by recent releases, and the usual Marquee mix of dedicated clubgoers and bottle service tables. Navigating that energy is part of the NYC nightclub experience — find your pocket, find your people, lose yourself in the sound.

## The Verdict

I'm going. That's the bottom line. KSHMR at Marquee represents something I want to support: a globally significant artist choosing to play a proper NYC club rather than skipping the city entirely or defaulting to a festival slot. It's big room done right, in a room that's earned its place in the city's nightlife history.

Is it the most underground event happening that weekend? No. Is it going to satisfy the techno purists? Probably not. But not everything needs to be. There's a place in this city's ecosystem for unapologetic, well-crafted, emotionally resonant big room house. KSHMR has been holding that space for a decade. Marquee, newly renovated and recommitted, is the right room for it.

See you on the dancefloor. I'll be the one losing my mind when "Secrets" drops.

**Rating: 4/5** — A must-see for big room fans and a reminder that KSHMR remains one of the most consistent producers in the game. Marquee's renovation adds intrigue to an already compelling booking.
