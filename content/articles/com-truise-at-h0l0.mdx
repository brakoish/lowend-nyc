# Com Truise at H0L0: Synthwave's Synthetic Astronaut Lands in Ridgewood

**Ridgewood, Queens — April 3, 2026**

There's a particular kind of nostalgia that doesn't feel cheap. It's not the forced retro aesthetic of a vintage t-shirt at Urban Outfitters or the algorithmic 80s playlist that plays at every Brooklyn bar. It's something more specific, more considered, more genuinely transportive. Seth Haley, the upstate New York producer who records as Com Truise, has been mining that territory for over a decade now, and he's still finding gold.

When he touches down at H0L0 on April 3rd for a DJ set, it'll be a homecoming of sorts. Haley was born and raised in Oneida, in Madison County, just a few hours north of the city. He's lived in Orlando for years now, but there's something fitting about him playing Queens, in a warehouse on the Bushwick border, surrounded by the same kind of creative transplants who gravitated to his sound in the first place.

## The artist: from art director to synthetic astronaut

Before Com Truise existed, Seth Haley was an art director. He designed packaging and promotional materials, worked in the visual side of the music industry, spent his days thinking about how things look while making music at night under names like Sarin Sunday and Airliner. When he resigned from that job in 2010 to focus on music full-time, he was taking a leap that plenty of bedroom producers dream about but never actually make.

The first Com Truise release, the *Cyanide Sisters* EP, dropped that same year. Originally a free download on AMDISCS, it got picked up by Ghostly International and reissued in 2011. That label association has defined his career. Ghostly isn't just a record label; it's a stamp of aesthetic approval, a signal that an artist has been vetted by one of electronic music's most visually and sonically discerning operations. Com Truise fit right in alongside Tycho, Matthew Dear, and the rest of the Ghostly roster—artists who treat electronic music as a holistic experience, where the visual identity matters as much as the kick drum.

The name itself is a spoonerism of Tom Cruise, that quintessentially 80s movie star, and it sets the tone for everything that follows. Haley's music lives in the space between irony and sincerity, between kitsch and genuine beauty. He calls it "mid-fi synthwave slow-motion funk," and that's as accurate a description as you're going to get. It's synthesizer-heavy, obviously, built from analog and digital sources processed through Reason and Ableton. It's influenced by 80s film scores, by the neon-noir aesthetic of movies like *Blade Runner* and *Drive*, by the moment when digital technology first started to infiltrate popular music.

But it's not just pastiche. What separates Com Truise from the endless sea of retro-synth producers is the narrative ambition. Every album from *Cyanide Sisters* through 2017's *Iteration* tells the story of a space traveler, a "synthetic astronaut" named Com Truise who flees an oppressed society on some distant world. It's a concept album approach applied to instrumental electronic music, and it works because Haley commits to it fully. The music feels like it has a plot even when there are no words.

## The sound: neon-drenched and deliberately imperfect

If you've never heard Com Truise, start with "VHS Sex" or "Brokendate" from 2011's *Galactic Melt*. Those tracks established the template: thick, warbling basslines, arpeggiated synths that spiral upward like smoke, drums that hit with a deliberate, almost sluggish precision. The "slow-motion" part of his self-description is key. This isn't high-BPM workout music. It's music for late nights, for driving through city streets at 2 AM, for moments when you want to feel like you're living inside a movie montage.

*In Decay* (2012) collected unreleased material and deep cuts, showcasing the range of Haley's output. *Iteration* (2017) was supposedly the final chapter in the Com Truise saga, a conclusion to the space traveler narrative that felt like a proper send-off. Then came *Persuasion System* in 2019, a slight pivot toward more immediate, less conceptually burdened territory. The sound has evolved over time—he's incorporated the Dave Smith OB-6 into his live setup since 2017, adding new textures and possibilities—but the core identity remains intact.

What you won't get at this show is a live performance in the traditional sense. Haley is billed for a DJ set, which means he'll be selecting and mixing tracks rather than performing his own material with hardware. For some fans, this might be a disappointment. For others, it's an opportunity to see a different side of his musical personality. Com Truise DJ sets tend to range wider than his productions, touching on influences and contemporaries that inform his sound without necessarily appearing on his albums. Expect 80s synth-pop, Italo disco, early Detroit techno, and contemporary producers working in similar territory.

## The venue: H0L0's warehouse intimacy

H0L0 has been holding down the Ridgewood/Bushwick corridor since 2017, and in that time it's established itself as one of the most reliable rooms in the city for electronic music that operates outside the mainstream. The 800-capacity warehouse on Wyckoff Avenue isn't trying to be a superclub. There's no VIP section, no bottle service, no massive LED wall distracting from the actual music. What you get is proximity: to the artist, to the sound system, to the other people in the room who made the trek to Queens for something specific.

The sound system at H0L0 punches above its weight. I've heard everything from noise experiments to rolling techno in that room, and it consistently delivers clarity without harshness. For music as texturally rich as Com Truise's—where the warmth of analog synths and the crispness of digital processing need to coexist—that clarity matters. You want to hear the details: the way a filter opens up over sixteen bars, the subtle pitch drift of an oscillator, the layered pads that create the sense of vast space.

The crowd at H0L0 tends to be knowledgeable without being pretentious. This isn't a venue that draws the bridge-and-tunnel crowd looking for a generic night out. The people who make the trek to Ridgewood are there for the music, willing to engage with something unfamiliar, ready to be transported. They're the right audience for what Haley does.

## Why this show matters now

Synthwave as a genre has had an interesting trajectory. It emerged in the late 2000s as a genuine underground movement, producers discovering the emotional possibilities of 80s synthesizer sounds without the baggage of the era's excesses. It got a boost from the *Drive* soundtrack in 2011, which introduced the aesthetic to a broader audience. It spawned subgenres and offshoots: darksynth, outrun, vaporwave. It became, for a moment, almost ubiquitous.

And then the backlash came. Too much of anything breeds contempt, and synthwave started to feel like a parody of itself—endless producers cranking out identical arpeggios and neon grids, the aesthetic becoming as formulaic as the music it was supposedly reacting against. The term "synthwave" itself became almost embarrassing, associated with lazy nostalgia and algorithmic playlist fodder.

Com Truise survived that backlash because he was never really just a synthwave producer. His music has too much personality, too much narrative ambition, too much genuine craft to be dismissed as mere retro fetishism. The Ghostly International association helped—he was always positioned as an artist first, a genre practitioner second. But more importantly, the music holds up. Put on *Galactic Melt* today and it still sounds fresh, still sounds like something only Seth Haley could have made.

This H0L0 show comes at an interesting moment. Haley has been relatively quiet on the release front in recent years, though he produced the music for Coinbase's viral Super Bowl LVI ad in 2022, sampling the Beatles' cover of "Money (That's What I Want)"—a high-profile flex that introduced his sound to millions of viewers who'd never heard the name Com Truise. He's been touring consistently, hitting festivals and clubs across the US and Europe. But there's a sense that something new might be brewing, that this period of relative quiet could be leading toward the next phase of the project.

## The prediction

For this DJ set, I'm expecting a journey through Haley's influences and contemporaries, the records that shaped the Com Truise sound alongside his own productions. The tempo will likely stay in the mid-range, 110-120 BPM, that slow-motion funk zone where his music lives most comfortably. There will be moments of pure dancefloor utility, tracks that exist to make bodies move, and there will be moments of pure atmosphere, tracks that exist to create a mood, a sense of place.

The H0L0 room should suit him well. The warehouse aesthetic, the exposed brick and industrial fixtures, provides a neutral backdrop that won't compete with the music. The sound system should handle his low-end requirements without strain. And the crowd, that particular H0L0 crowd of dedicated heads and curious explorers, should give him the receptive energy that any DJ set needs to reach its potential.

If you're on the fence: go. Bring an open mind and comfortable shoes. Be prepared to hear music that treats the 1980s not as a joke or a marketing gimmick but as a genuine source of inspiration, a period when synthesizers were new and full of possibility, when the future seemed bright and strange and just a little bit dangerous. That's the world Com Truise invites you into. It's a world worth visiting.

**Rating: 4/5** — Essential for synthwave heads and Ghostly International completists. Highly recommended for anyone who wants to hear electronic music that prioritizes atmosphere and narrative over immediate gratification. One of the more interesting bookings H0L0 has made this season.
