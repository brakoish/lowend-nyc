# Covenant at Brooklyn Monarch

I still remember the first time I heard "Dead Stars." It was 2003, some warehouse in Bushwick that doesn't exist anymore, and someone dropped it at that perfect moment when the drugs are kicking in but the anxiety hasn't started yet. That bassline hit and I felt like I was levitating. That's Covenant. They've been doing this for almost 40 years and they still hit like that.

Now they're playing Brooklyn Monarch, and yeah, I'm going. You should too.

## The Band

Covenant formed in 1986 in Helsingborg, Sweden. While their countrymen were making pop music for IKEA commercials, these guys were building something darker. They took EBM's industrial aggression, mixed it with synth-pop's earworm melodies, and filtered it through Nordic existential dread. The result is songs that work on a dancefloor at 2 AM but also hit different when you're walking home alone at 5 AM questioning your life choices.

Their 1996 record *Dreams of Cryotank* put them on the map, but 1998's *Europa* made them legends. "Figurehead" is still a perfect song—melancholy without being cheesy, aggressive without being meathead industrial. 2000's *United States of Mind* gave us "Dead Stars," which somehow manages to be both a club anthem and the sound of a nervous breakdown. That's their whole thing: beauty and decay, dancing while the world burns.

They put out *The Blinding Dark* in 2023 and it's actually good. Not "good for a legacy act" good—actually good. Most bands from the 80s are coasting on nostalgia at this point. Covenant is still trying.

## The Venue

Brooklyn Monarch is in a converted industrial building on the Williamsburg waterfront. Exposed brick, high ceilings, concrete floors that vibrate when the subs hit. The sound system is serious—this isn't some bar with speakers duct-taped to the wall. It's a proper room for proper music.

It's not a Manhattan bottle service club where people go to be seen. It's a warehouse with a license. The kind of place where the music matters more than the Instagram photos. For a band like Covenant, whose whole aesthetic is industrial and massive, it's a perfect fit. The room has enough space for their lighting rigs to actually do something. The sound has room to expand and hit you in the chest.

## Why Go

New York gets a lot of electronic acts. We get the techno tourists playing the same Beatport top 10 tracks. We get EDM refugees who press play on a USB stick and wave their hands. Covenant is different. They're a real band—three people playing actual instruments, writing actual songs with verses and choruses and emotional weight.

Eskil Simonsson doesn't scream. He doesn't need to. His voice has this controlled intensity that makes you lean in. Joakim Montelius builds these huge, cinematic synth landscapes that feel like standing at the edge of something ancient and slightly dangerous. Live, they bring a physicality that most electronic acts can't touch. You can see them working. You can see the sweat.

In a city where most music is optimized for TikTok clips and algorithmic playlists, Covenant is a reminder that electronic music can be made by humans with something to say. It's not background noise. It's not content. It's art that happens to work on a dancefloor.

## What to Expect

They'll play "Dead Stars." They have to. They'll play "Figurehead" and "Call the Ships to Port." Those songs are why most of us are there. But they won't just play the hits and leave. They've got new material that holds up, and they've never been a band to rest on their legacy. Expect a mix of classics and deeper cuts, old darkness and new darkness.

Their live show has gotten more theatrical over the years. Better lighting, better staging, but not in a cheesy way. They understand atmosphere. They understand that the visual matters as much as the audio when you're trying to create a world for ninety minutes.

## The Verdict

Covenant at Brooklyn Monarch is worth your time and money. It's Swedish darkness meeting Brooklyn grit. It's three decades of electronic music history in a room that knows how to handle it. If you've ever danced with your eyes closed in a room full of strangers who get it, who understand something the daylight world doesn't, this is your night.

The show will sell out. It always does. Get tickets early, show up on time, and prepare to feel something.
