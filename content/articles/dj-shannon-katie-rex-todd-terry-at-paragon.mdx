# DJ Shannon, Katie Rex, Todd Terry at Paragon

**Lower East Side — March 2026**

Paragon announced this lineup and I stopped scrolling. DJ Shannon, Katie Rex, and Todd Terry on the same bill. Three artists who have nothing in common on paper except this: they all destroy dancefloors. A Texas techno assassin, a Philly queer firebrand, and a Bronx house legend who basically invented the sound we're still chasing. If this doesn't get you hyped, I don't know what to tell you.

## The venue

Paragon sits on the Lower East Side in that sweet spot where the neighborhood's grit hasn't been completely scrubbed away. It's not trying to be Brooklyn. It's not trying to be the next Output. It's just a proper room with a proper soundsystem and a crowd that shows up ready to work.

The LES has always been where New York nightlife gets real. While Manhattan clubs chase bottle service money and Brooklyn warehouses chase techno tourists, the Lower East Side just... dances. Paragon fits that lineage. The room is small enough to feel the bass in your chest, big enough to get lost. No gimmicks. No theme nights. Just music, loud and late.

I've had my best nights at Paragon because it doesn't try too hard. The door isn't performing exclusivity. Drinks don't cost rent. The sound punches above its weight. When promoters bring real talent through those doors, the room responds. And this lineup? This is real talent.

## DJ Shannon: Texas techno, unfiltered

Let's start with DJ Shannon because she represents where techno is right now — the kind that actually moves bodies, not just impresses other DJs.

Coming out of Texas, Shannon's sound is exactly what you'd want: hard, relentless, zero pretension. She's not making techno for the Berghain crowd. She's making techno for people who want to sweat, who want to feel something physical, who don't need a dissertation with their kick drums.

She broke through via Night Slugs and Fade to Mind, but she's always had her own voice separate from that London sound. What Shannon understood early — and what separates her from the flood of hard techno producers lately — is that aggression alone isn't enough. You need groove. You need space. You need those moments where the kick drops out, the synths breathe, then everything crashes back in.

Her sets are relentless in the best way. She plays fast and hard but never loses the thread. There's narrative to her selections, a sense that each track answers the one before it. I've watched her clear rooms and rebuild them from scratch. That confidence only comes from years of playing tough rooms and learning what actually works.

What I respect: she's stayed true to her sound even as techno blew up globally. She could've softened her edges, chased the melodic techno dollar, tried fitting into more palatable boxes for festival bookings. Instead she doubled down on the hard stuff — industrial-tinged kicks, distorted synths, tracks that sound like they're trying to break the soundsystem. That's the Shannon you're getting at Paragon. Thank god.

## Katie Rex: queer techno, loud and proud

If DJ Shannon represents techno's present, Katie Rex represents its future — or at least one future I'm invested in seeing happen.

Katie Rex is a DJ, producer, and founder of queer collective/label Club Carry. She's New York-based now but came up in Philadelphia, and you hear that Philly warehouse DNA in everything she does. This isn't polite chin-stroking techno for subgenre discussions. This is techno as liberation, as community, as space where queer bodies take up room and demand to be seen.

Her sound lives between hard techno, EBM, and industrial-tinged dance music that's been bubbling from queer undergrounds for years. She plays fast — often 140 BPM and beyond — but there's always musicality. The synths scream. The drums pound. Vocals, when they appear, are distorted, processed, made strange. It's music that refuses to be background noise.

What makes Katie Rex essential isn't just her DJ skills, though those are serious. It's her vision for what dance music can be. Club Carry isn't just a party series or label. It's intent — a commitment to centering queer and trans artists in a scene that's historically been hostile to them. When Katie plays, she's building a world track by track where people like her get to be main characters.

I've seen her sets go completely off the rails. Crowd losing their minds, energy building to fever pitches where it feels like the room might lift off. She has that rare gift of making a club feel like sanctuary and battleground simultaneously. This is music for dancing and surviving, for joy and rage, for when you need to feel completely, overwhelmingly alive.

At Paragon, with this crowd, on this bill? I expect something special. Katie Rex has been on a tear, and playing alongside Shannon and Terry will push her to bring her absolute best. Don't sleep on this set.

## Todd Terry: the legend, still teaching

And then there's Todd Terry.

If you don't know why Todd Terry matters, we need a longer conversation than I have space for. Short version: house music royalty, a true pioneer who defined New York dance music in the late 80s and early 90s, whose influence still echoes through every kick drum and chopped vocal you hear.

Todd Terry is the Bronx-born producer and DJ who took Chicago house, hip-hop breakbeat culture, and the emerging New York club scene and forged something new. His "Todd Terry Project" releases, his Royal House work, his remixes for Björk, Michael Jackson, The Rolling Stones — these are foundations everything else builds on. When people talk about "New York house," they're largely talking about what Todd Terry invented.

The "Todd Terry sound" is instantly recognizable: chopped-up vocal samples, hard-hitting drums, the way he could take a soulful house groove and make it hit like hip-hop. "Can You Party," "Weekend," "Bango (To the Batmobile)" — these aren't just classics, they're blueprints. Draw a straight line from Todd Terry's sampler to UK hardcore and jungle, to modern bass music, to how producers still approach vocal manipulation.

But here's what matters for Paragon: Todd Terry isn't a museum piece. He's still out here, week after week, playing clubs and festivals worldwide, showing the younger generation how it's done. And I mean literally showing them. A Todd Terry set in 2026 is a masterclass in selection, reading a room, understanding that the DJ's job is making people dance. Full stop.

He'll play classics, of course. You don't see Todd Terry and not hear "Can You Party" or "Jumpin'." But he'll also play records from last month, last week, digging deep into current house and finding tracks that deserve ears. His ear is still sharp. His taste is still impeccable. His technical skills — how he mixes, builds energy, knows exactly when to drop the next record — these are things you can't fake or teach. You only learn them by doing what Todd Terry has done for nearly four decades.

Seeing him on this bill feels significant. Passing the torch, or maybe recognizing the torch never needed passing because Todd Terry still holds it, still lights the way. When he steps up at Paragon, he's not just playing a set. He's representing the entire history of this music, the New York tradition that made nights like this possible.

## Why this bill works

On paper, these three shouldn't work together. DJ Shannon's hard techno, Katie Rex's queer industrialism, Todd Terry's classic house — different worlds. Different BPMs, different aesthetics, different crowds.

That's exactly why this booking is genius. Paragon isn't giving you a seamless, homogeneous night where every DJ sounds the same. They're giving you a journey. They're trusting the audience to handle variety, appreciate contrast, understand that the best nights take you places you didn't expect.

Maybe Todd Terry lays the foundation with classic house grooves reminding you where this came from. Then Katie Rex blows the roof off, pushing energy into raw, queer, unapologetic territory. DJ Shannon brings it home with techno that doesn't let up, demanding everything you have left and asking for more.

Or maybe they go different order. Maybe Todd Terry closes, showing the young guns how a legend commands a room. Maybe Katie Rex goes first, sets the tone, declares territory, makes it clear this is space where queer voices lead. The beauty of a bill this strong: any configuration works. You're getting three distinct visions, three approaches to making people move, and the tension between them makes each set hit harder.

## The prediction

I'm expecting a long night and a sore neck tomorrow. I'm expecting to hear records I've never heard and classics I can't believe I'm hearing on a soundsystem this good. I'm expecting a crowd that knows exactly what they're getting into, that shows up ready to work, that understands how rare it is to get three artists this compelling in a room this intimate.

Paragon doesn't do bills like this often. When they do, it's because they found something special. DJ Shannon, Katie Rex, and Todd Terry together isn't just a good night out. It's a statement about what dance music can be when you stop worrying about genre boundaries and start focusing on what matters: the music, the community, the collective experience of losing yourself on a dancefloor.

New York has been through a lot with its nightlife. We've lost venues, scenes, whole eras of this city's dance music history. But nights like this remind me it's not over. The spirit that made New York the birthplace of house music, that made this city essential to global dance music conversation — that spirit is still here. You just have to know where to look.

Look at Paragon. Look at this bill. Show up early, stay late, prepare to have your expectations exceeded.

**Rating: 5/5** — Essential. Three artists at the top of their games, in a room that knows how to host them. This is why we go out.
