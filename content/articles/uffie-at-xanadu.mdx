# Uffie at Xanadu: The Ed Banger Icon Returns to Remind NYC What Electro-Clash Was

**Bushwick — March 2026**

There's a particular kind of nostalgia that hits when you hear those opening bars of "Pop The Glock." That deadpan delivery, the minimal electro beat, the sheer audacity of a 17-year-old American in Paris declaring herself over a track that sounded like it was beamed in from some alternate future where hip-hop and French house had a beautiful, messy baby.

Uffie was never supposed to last. That's what they said. Another MySpace-era flash, another bloghouse curiosity, another artist whose entire career would be defined by one single and a thousand thinkpieces about internet fame. But here we are, nearly two decades later, and Anna Hartley is still standing. Still performing. Still carrying the Ed Banger torch in a world that desperately needs what she's selling.

And now she's coming to Xanadu.

## The venue: Bushwick's fever dream

Let's be real for a second. Xanadu shouldn't work. A roller rink. In Bushwick. That doubles as a nightclub. With a hand-painted maple floor and a d&b audiotechnik sound system. It's the kind of concept that sounds like it was dreamed up during a particularly ambitious acid trip, the kind of place that could easily collapse under the weight of its own quirk.

But it doesn't collapse. It soars.

The team behind Xanadu, the same minds that brought us The Turk's Inn and Sultan Room, understand something fundamental about New York nightlife right now. We're tired of the same four walls. Tired of black boxes with a booth and a bar and not much else. We want spaces that feel like experiences, venues that transport us somewhere else the moment we walk through the door.

Xanadu delivers. That 7,000 square foot rink, the custom Riedell skates, the stage curated by the Sultan Room crew, it all adds up to something genuinely unlike anything else in the city. When you're there, you're not just at a show. You're inside a concept. A vibe. A whole aesthetic universe.

And for Uffie? It's perfect.

## Uffie: the accidental icon

The story has been told a thousand times, but it bears repeating. Anna Hartley was born in Miami, raised in Hong Kong, living in Paris, when she recorded "Pop The Glock" on a whim in 2006. She wasn't trying to start a movement. She was just messing around, putting her bored-rich-girl drawl over a beat by her then-boyfriend Feadz, uploading it to MySpace because that's what you did.

Then Busy P heard it. Then Ed Banger signed her. Then the world came calling.

What followed was a whirlwind that would have destroyed most teenagers. The hype cycle of the late 2000s was brutal, a machine that chewed up young artists and spat them out before they could legally drink in America. Uffie was everywhere and nowhere at once: performing at Coachella, appearing on Justice's "Tthhee Ppaarrttyy," becoming the face of a sound that didn't really have a name yet.

Electro-clash, bloghouse, nu-rave, whatever you want to call it. Uffie was there at the beginning, before the genre tags solidified, before the thinkpieces crystallized. She was making music that felt like the internet sounded: fragmented, irreverent, global, detached and emotional all at once.

But the machine kept turning. The debut album, *Sex Dreams and Denim Jeans*, took four years to materialize, an eternity in internet time. By the time it dropped in 2010, the moment had shifted. The blogs that had championed her had moved on to dubstep, then trap, then whatever came next. Uffie became a footnote, a "remember when," a symbol of a specific cultural moment that had passed.

Except she didn't disappear. She just stopped playing the game.

## The long game

Here's what the haters missed: Uffie was always smarter than the narrative. While the internet was busy declaring her a one-hit wonder, she was living an actual life. She had a daughter. She moved between cities. She released music when she felt like it, not when the algorithm demanded it. She refused to become a nostalgia act, even as the world tried to force her into that box.

And slowly, something shifted. The artists who grew up on "Pop The Glock" started coming of age. Charli XCX, SOPHIE, the entire hyperpop movement, they all carried traces of Uffie's DNA. That deadpan vocal style, the fusion of rap and electro, the embrace of artifice as authenticity, it all came back around.

When *Sex Dreams and Denim Jeans* turned 14 last year, the reappraisals started rolling in. Pitchfork, The FADER, the same outlets that had been lukewarm in 2010, suddenly recognized what Uffie had built. The album wasn't a failed follow-up to a viral single. It was a blueprint. A document of an artist figuring herself out in real-time, messy and honest and weird in all the right ways.

And now, in 2026, Uffie is performing again. Not as a nostalgia act. Not as a "remember the 2000s" throwback. As a living, breathing artist whose influence is finally being acknowledged by the mainstream she never quite fit into.

## Why this show matters

Let's talk about what you're actually going to get at Xanadu. Uffie's live shows have always been divisive, and that's part of the point. She's not a polished pop performer. She's not going to give you choreographed dance routines and perfect vocals. What she offers is something rarer: presence. Attitude. The sense that you're watching someone who genuinely doesn't care if you get it or not.

That attitude, that Parisian cool mixed with American swagger, it's what made her stand out in 2006 and it's what makes her relevant now. In an era where every artist is a brand, every performance is content, every move is calculated for maximum engagement, Uffie's detachment feels radical. She's not trying to be your friend. She's not trying to go viral. She's just... performing. Being. Existing in the moment with a microphone and a backing track and whatever she's feeling that night.

At Xanadu, that energy is going to hit different. The venue's surrealism, the roller rink aesthetic, the sense that you've stepped out of normal Brooklyn and into some alternate dimension, it all amplifies what Uffie brings. This isn't going to be a show you watch passively. It's going to be an experience you step into, a world you inhabit for a few hours.

And the music? Expect "Pop The Glock," obviously. Expect "MCs Can Kiss" and "ADD SUV" and the other singles that defined her early run. But also expect deeper cuts, newer material, the songs that don't have a million Spotify plays but mean something to the people who've been following her all along.

## The Ed Banger connection

We can't talk about Uffie without talking about Ed Banger Records. The label, founded by Busy P in 2003, defined a sound and an aesthetic that still reverberates through electronic music today. Justice, SebastiAn, Mr. Oizo, Breakbot, the roster reads like a who's-who of French electronic music's most innovative voices.

Uffie was always the outlier in that crew. The American. The rapper. The one who didn't quite fit the template. But that's exactly why she mattered. Ed Banger wasn't just about French house revivalism. It was about attitude. About taking the past and warping it into something new. About looking good and sounding better and not giving a damn what anyone thought.

That ethos is alive and well in 2026, even if the original Ed Banger wave has crested. The label's influence is everywhere: in the current wave of French house revivalists, in the hyperpop artists sampling the same sounds Uffie was working with, in the general sense that electronic music should be fun, stylish, and slightly dangerous.

When Uffie steps onto that Xanadu stage, she's not just representing herself. She's carrying the torch for a whole era. A whole aesthetic. A whole way of thinking about music and culture that valued attitude over polish, vibe over virality, authenticity over algorithm.

## The prediction

I'm not going to tell you this is going to be a perfect show. Uffie doesn't do perfect. She does real. She does messy. She does memorable.

What I can tell you is that Xanadu is the right room for her. The venue's commitment to experience over efficiency, to vibe over volume, to creating something genuinely unique in a city that's seen it all, it aligns perfectly with what Uffie represents.

This isn't going to be a DJ set. This isn't going to be a karaoke performance over backing tracks. This is going to be Uffie, in a room built for spectacle, reminding everyone why her name still matters after all these years.

If you were there in 2006, if you remember the MySpace days and the bloghouse explosion and the feeling that the internet was going to change everything about how music worked, this is your chance to reconnect with that energy. Not as nostalgia, but as living history.

If you weren't there, if you discovered Uffie through Charli XCX interviews or hyperpop Spotify playlists or TikTok videos using "Pop The Glock" as a soundtrack, this is your chance to see where it all started. To understand why an entire generation of artists still name-check her as an influence.

Either way, get to Bushwick. Skate if you want to. Don't if you don't. But be there. Because artists like Uffie don't come around often, and venues like Xanadu don't last forever. The combination of the two? That's something special.

**Rating: 4/5** — Essential for Ed Banger heads, electro-clash nostalgists, and anyone who appreciates artists who refuse to play by the rules. The Xanadu setting elevates what could be a standard performance into something genuinely memorable.
