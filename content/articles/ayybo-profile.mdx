# AYYBO: From Anaheim Bedrooms to Knockdown Center

Knockdown Center, Queens / March 13, 2026 / Gray Area

Some artists spend years chasing the algorithm. AYYBO built his following by yelling "Goddamn, baby" into a microphone and meaning it.

The Anaheim-born producer plays Knockdown Center this Friday, and if you've been anywhere near bass house in the last three years, you already know the name. "Hypnosis" has 11 million Spotify plays. Pitbull remixed it. John Summit, Dom Dolla, The Martinez Brothers, and Pete Tong all rinse his tracks. He's got 2.4 million monthly listeners and a label, GDB Records, that he runs entirely on his own terms.

Not bad for someone who started out DJing lunch breaks at his high school.

## The come-up: from drumline to Deadbeats

AYYBO's real name is Aaron Bonnema, and his path to Knockdown Center runs through the unlikeliest of training grounds. He grew up in Anaheim, where ska culture dominated and his dad played him OutKast's "B.O.B." and Chemical Brothers on repeat. The video game Rock Band got him hooked on rhythm. High school drumline taught him to hit things with precision. Then the student government needed someone to play music during lunch breaks.

He had never DJed. He bought a $150 controller and learned on YouTube. Soon he was mashing trap, moombahton, bass house, and pop in the school courtyard while kids ate lunch. House parties followed. Then he discovered how vast electronic music actually was.

Film school came next, briefly. He was studying cinematography but kept drifting back to music. In 2018 he signed to Zeds Dead's Deadbeats label under a different alias, making bass music and trap rooted in the hip-hop he loved. But it wasn't enough. He felt the urge to pivot, to find a sound that was actually his.

## Finding the Sound: GDB and the Bass House Sweet Spot

The AYYBO project officially launched in Spring 2021. He started with edits, clubbing up tracks like No Doubt's "Hella Good" and Ciara's "1,2 Step" with tech-house production and basslines that hit like a physical force. The formula worked: take familiar hooks, wrap them in thick, elastic low-end, and watch the crowd react.

By 2022 he was releasing original material at a furious pace, nearly a single every month. "GDB" and "Do This" came out on Zeds Dead's Altered States imprint, establishing that bassline-heavy, slightly ominous tech house sound that AYYBO describes as "good evil." Then "Hypnosis" happened.

The track, a collaboration with singer ero808 built around a swinging saxophone melody and a hook that burrows into your brain, dropped in July 2022. DJs rinsed it immediately. TikTok picked it up. Within months it had millions of streams and half a million video views. Then Pitbull's producer came calling. A week later, Pitbull had written a hook. The remix, "Right Or Wrong (Hypnosis)," dropped in January 2023.

AYYBO's response: "I would never predict that to happen ever. I think it's just the saxophone. It summoned him or something."

## The label: GDB Records

The founding of GDB Records gave AYYBO something every independent artist needs: control. The label's name comes from that same inside joke, "Goddamn, baby," which started in a dream and became a mantra. Anything good happens, you say "GDB." Fans say it back. It's a positivity loop, as weird as that sounds.

The label's output so far includes "RIZZ," "BASS GLIDE," and "NEW DANCE," all tracks that demonstrate what AYYBO does best: minimal, funky, bass-heavy house built for big systems. No major label interference. No A&R meetings. Just the sound he wants to make, released when he wants to release it.

Support has come from everywhere. The Martinez Brothers, Vintage Culture, John Summit, Pete Tong, Joseph Capriati, Dom Dolla, they've all played his records. He's performed at Electric Forest, Beyond Wonderland, Floyd Miami, and sold-out headline shows in Miami, Chicago, Denver, San Diego, Portland, and Austin.

## The Knockdown Center show

Gray Area is bringing AYYBO to Knockdown Center on March 13, and it's a booking that makes perfect sense. The venue's 15,000-square-foot main room and Danley sound system are built for exactly this kind of bass-heavy, forward-leaning house music. Support comes from Gabss, who knows her way around a tech-house groove.

Expect the hits: "Hypnosis," "RIZZ," "BASS GLIDE," "NEW DANCE." Expect unreleased material from GDB Records. Expect that specific AYYBO energy, the one that comes from someone who treats social media as a creative tool rather than a necessary evil, who plays characters in his videos (cowboys, 1920s gangsters, underwater DJs), who doesn't take himself too seriously but takes the music dead seriously.

He's hinted at new directions in recent interviews: "more minimal and funky. It's going to get deep." The Knockdown Center set might be where some of that new material gets its first airing.

## The Verdict

AYYBO represents something the American dance music scene needs more of: artists who build their own infrastructure. No major label backing. No industry connections from day one. Just a kid from Anaheim who learned to DJ on YouTube, found his sound through trial and error, and built a following one track, one show, one "GDB" at a time.

The bass house wave has produced plenty of copycats. AYYBO isn't one of them. His tracks have personality. His sets have momentum. His label has a point of view. And now he's headlining rooms like Knockdown Center on his own terms.

That's the blueprint. See you in Queens.
