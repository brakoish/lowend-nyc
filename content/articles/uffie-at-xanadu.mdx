# Uffie at Xanadu: the Ed Banger icon returns to remind NYC what electro-clash was

**Bushwick — March 2026**

You know that feeling when "Pop The Glock" comes on? That deadpan delivery, the minimal electro beat, some 17-year-old kid from Miami via Paris talking shit like she owned the room. Because she did. That track hit different in 2006 — sounded like it came from a future where hip-hop and French house got drunk and made a baby.

Everyone wrote Uffie off. MySpace flash in the pan. Bloghouse one-hit-wonder. Another internet casualty who'd disappear once the hype machine found its next snack. But here's the thing: Anna Hartley never went away. She's still here, still performing, still carrying that Ed Banger energy while the rest of us pretend we didn't spend years sleeping on her.

Now she's playing Xanadu. And yeah, I'm going.

## The venue: Bushwick's fever dream

Look, Xanadu shouldn't work. A roller rink in Bushwick that doubles as a nightclub? Hand-painted maple floors, d&b audiotechnik rig, the whole thing sounds like someone described their trip to you at 4am. Most venues this concept-heavy collapse under their own quirk.

But Xanadu doesn't collapse. It actually works.

Same crew behind Turk's Inn and Sultan Room — they get what NYC nightlife needs right now. We're done with black boxes. Done with rooms that are just a booth, a bar, and four walls painted black. We want places that feel like experiences, venues that transport you somewhere else the second you walk in.

Xanadu pulls it off. 7,000 square feet of rink, custom Riedell skates you can actually use, stage programming from the Sultan Room heads. When you're there, you're not just at a show — you're inside a whole thing. A vibe. An aesthetic universe that doesn't exist anywhere else in the city.

For Uffie? Couldn't be more perfect.

## Uffie: the accidental icon

You've heard this story before. Miami-born, Hong Kong-raised, living in Paris at 17. Recorded "Pop The Glock" basically as a joke — bored-rich-girl vocals over a Feadz beat, tossed up on MySpace because that's what you did in 2006.

Busy P heard it. Ed Banger signed her. The hype machine kicked in.

The late 2000s blog cycle chewed through artists fast. Most teenagers would've been destroyed by it. Uffie was somehow everywhere and nowhere: Coachella slots, that Justice track "Tthhee Ppaarrttyy," becoming the face of a sound no one had named yet.

Electro-clash. Bloghouse. Nu-rave. Call it whatever. Uffie was there before the thinkpieces, before the genre tags hardened. She made music that sounded like the internet felt — fragmented, global, detached, emotional all at once.

Then the machine turned. *Sex Dreams and Denim Jeans* took four years to come out. Internet eternity. By 2010 the blogs had moved on to dubstep, then trap, then the next thing. Uffie became a footnote, a "remember when," frozen in time as that MySpace-era curiosity.

Except she didn't actually disappear. She just stopped playing.

## The long game

Here's what everyone missed: Uffie was always smarter than the narrative. While Twitter was calling her a one-hit wonder, she was actually living. Had a kid. Moved around. Dropped music when she felt like it, not when the content calendar demanded it. Refused to become a nostalgia act even when the industry tried to shove her in that box.

Then something shifted. The kids who grew up on "Pop The Glock" grew up for real. Charli XCX. SOPHIE. The whole hyperpop wave. They all carried Uffie's DNA — that deadpan vocal delivery, rap over electro beats, treating artifice like authenticity. It all came back around.

When *Sex Dreams and Denim Jeans* turned 14, the reappraisals hit. Pitchfork, The FADER — same outlets that were lukewarm in 2010 — suddenly got it. The album wasn't a failed follow-up. It was a blueprint. A document of someone figuring themselves out in public, messy and honest and weird in ways that actually hold up.

Now it's 2026 and Uffie's performing again. Not as nostalgia. Not as a "remember the 2000s" throwback. As a real artist whose influence finally gets acknowledged by a mainstream she never fit into anyway.

## Why this show matters

Let's be real about what you're getting at Xanadu. Uffie's live shows have always been divisive — that's the point. She's not a polished pop act. No choreographed routines, no perfect vocals. What she brings is rarer: presence. Attitude. The sense that you're watching someone who genuinely doesn't care if you get it.

That Parisian cool mixed with American swagger made her stand out in 2006 and it hits even harder now. Every artist today is a brand, every show is content, every move is calculated for engagement. Uffie's detachment feels almost radical by comparison. She's not trying to be your friend. Not trying to go viral. Just performing. Being there. Existing in the moment with a mic and whatever she's feeling.

At Xanadu that energy hits different. The whole roller rink surrealism, stepping out of normal Brooklyn into some alternate dimension — it amplifies what Uffie does. This isn't a show you watch passively. It's something you step into, a world you inhabit for a few hours.

Music-wise? Yeah, you'll get "Pop The Glock." "MCs Can Kiss," "ADD SUV," the hits. But you'll also get deeper cuts, newer material, tracks that don't have a million plays but mean everything to the people who've actually been paying attention.

## The Ed Banger connection

Can't talk about Uffie without Ed Banger. Busy P founded the label in 2003 and defined a sound that still echoes through everything. Justice, SebastiAn, Mr. Oizo, Breakbot — the roster is basically a who's-who of French electronic music.

Uffie was always the weird one. The American. The rapper. Didn't fit the template, which is exactly why she mattered. Ed Banger wasn't just French house revival. It was attitude. Taking the past and warping it into something new. Looking good, sounding better, not giving a damn what anyone thought.

That energy is still here in 2026. French house revivalists are everywhere. Hyperpop kids sampling the same sounds Uffie used. This general sense that electronic music should be fun, stylish, slightly dangerous.

When Uffie hits that Xanadu stage, she's not just doing her own thing. She's carrying the torch for a whole era. An aesthetic. A way of thinking about music that valued attitude over polish, vibe over virality.

## The prediction

Not gonna tell you this will be perfect. Uffie doesn't do perfect. She does real. Messy. Memorable.

But Xanadu is the right room for her. That focus on experience over efficiency, vibe over volume, creating something actually unique in a city that's seen everything — it fits what Uffie's about.

This isn't a DJ set. Not karaoke over backing tracks. It's Uffie in a room built for spectacle, reminding everyone why her name still matters.

If you were there in 2006 — the MySpace days, bloghouse exploding, that feeling that the internet was about to change music forever — this is your shot at that energy. Not nostalgia. Living history.

If you weren't there, if you found Uffie through Charli XCX interviews or hyperpop playlists or TikToks using "Pop The Glock," this is your chance to see where it started. To understand why an entire generation still name-checks her.

Either way: get to Bushwick. Skate if you want. Don't if you don't. Just be there. Artists like Uffie don't come around often. Venues like Xanadu don't last forever. The two together? That's something.

**Rating: 4/5** — Essential for Ed Banger heads, electro-clash heads, anyone who appreciates artists who refuse to play by the rules. Xanadu turns what could be a standard show into something you'll actually remember.
