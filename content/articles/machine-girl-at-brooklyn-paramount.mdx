# Machine Girl at Brooklyn Paramount: Digital Hardcore Chaos Comes to Downtown Brooklyn

**Downtown Brooklyn — March 20, 2026**

There's a particular kind of show where you walk in knowing you're going to leave changed. Not in some vague, spiritual sense — I mean physically changed. Ears ringing. Clothes soaked. Sense of reality slightly rearranged. Machine Girl doesn't do polite. They don't do background music. What they do is closer to an exorcism than a concert, and on March 20th, they're bringing that energy to Brooklyn Paramount.

Matt Stephenson has been operating under the Machine Girl banner since 2012, though calling it a "solo project" feels misleading. Live, Machine Girl is a duo — Stephenson handling the electronics, Sean Kelly on drums — and the transformation from studio entity to live wrecking crew is part of what makes this essential. In a city increasingly dominated by safe, Instagram-friendly electronic music, Machine Girl represents something genuinely transgressive. They're not here to make you comfortable. They're here to make you feel alive.

## The Project: From Long Island Basements to Internet Cult Status

Machine Girl started in the suburbs of Long Island, which somehow makes perfect sense. There's a particular strain of American electronic music that emerges from the dead zones between cities — kids with internet connections, cheap software, and enough suburban alienation to fuel something genuinely unhinged. Stephenson came up making music that sounded like it was assembled from broken glass and dial-up modem sounds, releasing early EPs on Bandcamp that found their way to the right weirdos.

The breakthrough came with *WLFGIRL* in 2014, though "breakthrough" is relative when you're operating in the margins of breakcore and digital hardcore. That album established the template: breakbeats pushed to their absolute limit, distorted synths that sound like they're fighting for air, samples that feel like they were ripped from forgotten video games and late-night infomercials. It's music that shouldn't work — too chaotic, too abrasive, too willfully difficult — and yet it does, because buried under all that noise is genuine songwriting craft.

Then came *...BECAUSE I'M YOUNG ARROGANT AND HATE EVERYTHING YOU STAND FOR* in 2017, an album title that tells you everything about the attitude. This was Machine Girl finding their voice, refining the chaos into something almost anthemic. Tracks like "Ginger Claps" and "Krystle" became underground staples, the kind of songs that get dropped at 3 AM when the crowd is too far gone for subtlety. The sound was still brutal, still confrontational, but there was a pop sensibility creeping in — hooks buried under distortion, melodies struggling to emerge from the noise floor.

The *Neon White* soundtrack in 2022 changed things. Suddenly Machine Girl wasn't just a cult concern for Bandcamp obsessives and breakcore heads. They were reaching gamers, speedrunners, a whole new audience that discovered them through the kinetic energy of that first-person shooter. The soundtrack showcased a different side — still intense, still unmistakably Machine Girl, but with moments of genuine beauty, of cinematic scope. It proved they could do more than just punish. They could transport.

## The Sound: What You're Actually Getting

Describing Machine Girl's sound to the uninitiated is a fool's errand, but I'll try. Imagine if Atari Teenage Riot had grown up on SoundCloud rap instead of punk. Imagine if Venetian Snares learned to write hooks. Imagine the soundtrack to a cyberpunk anime where the protagonist is losing their mind in real-time. It's breakcore — those sped-up Amen breaks, the time signatures that seem to shift mid-measure — but it's also digital hardcore, industrial, noise, and something uniquely American.

Live, it's a different beast entirely. The recorded material is already dense, already overwhelming. Add Sean Kelly's live drumming — real physical percussion cutting through the electronic chaos — and the experience becomes something else. This isn't a laptop set. This isn't someone pressing play and nodding along. This is two people generating genuine danger in real-time, feeding off each other's energy and the crowd's response.

Expect the BPMs to be punishing. Expect the bass to be physical, the kind of low-end that you feel in your sternum before your ears register it. Expect moments of genuine beauty to emerge from the chaos — a melodic passage, a half-remembered sample, a breakdown that gives you just enough time to catch your breath before the next assault. Machine Girl understand dynamics. They know that brutality requires contrast to land properly.

And expect the unexpected. Stephenson has a reputation for improvisation, for reading the room and adjusting accordingly. No two Machine Girl sets are identical. The framework might be similar — certain tracks get played, certain motifs recur — but the execution is live, reactive, human.

## The Venue: Brooklyn Paramount's Strange History

Brooklyn Paramount is a weird choice for this show, and I mean that as a compliment. Built in 1928 as a movie palace, it's sat at the corner of Flatbush and DeKalb for nearly a century, hosting everything from vaudeville acts to punk shows to religious services. It was a theater, then a concert venue, then a gymnasium for LIU Brooklyn, then restored to its original purpose. The architecture is pure art deco excess — ornate details, a grand lobby, a sense of occasion that most modern venues can't replicate.

For years, it sat dormant or underutilized, a beautiful relic from a different era of Brooklyn entertainment. Recently, it's been reborn as a proper concert venue again, hosting everything from legacy rock acts to hip-hop shows to, apparently, experimental electronic artists who specialize in sonic violence. The juxtaposition is perfect. Machine Girl's cyberpunk aesthetic against the Paramount's old-world grandeur. Digital chaos in an analog space.

The sound system is the question mark. This isn't a warehouse built for bass music, isn't Silo or Brooklyn Storehouse with their carefully tuned rigs. But the Paramount has been investing in production, understanding that modern audiences expect more than nostalgia. For a show this intense, they'll need to bring their A-game. Machine Girl's sound demands volume, demands clarity in the high-end even when the low-end is trying to shake the foundations.

The seating situation matters too. Brooklyn Paramount has a mix of floor and seated areas, and for Machine Girl, you want to be on the floor. This isn't sit-down music. This is music that demands movement, demands participation. The balcony might offer a better view, but the view isn't the point. The point is being in it, being part of the chaos, letting the sound wash over you until you're not thinking anymore, just responding.

## Why This Show Matters

New York City's electronic music scene has been craving something like this. For all the techno and house that dominates our warehouses, for all the tasteful selectors playing tasteful records to tasteful crowds, there's been a void where the genuinely transgressive used to live. We used to have spaces for noise, for industrial, for the kind of electronic music that felt dangerous. Those spaces have been closing, been pushed to the margins, been replaced by something safer and more marketable.

Machine Girl represents a connection to that lineage. They're not retro, not nostalgic for some imagined golden age of underground music. They're contemporary, internet-native, making music that could only exist right now. But they carry the DNA of those earlier scenes — the willingness to alienate, the commitment to intensity, the understanding that electronic music can be confrontational, can be art that challenges rather than comforts.

There's also the local angle. Stephenson is from Long Island, cut their teeth in the tri-state area's weird corners before the internet turned Machine Girl into a global concern. This isn't just another tour stop. This is a homecoming of sorts, a return to the region that shaped them, playing a venue that represents the old Brooklyn they're both part of and apart from.

And then there's the timing. Machine Girl have been building momentum, reaching new audiences through the *Neon White* soundtrack and a relentless touring schedule. They're not underground in the way they were five years ago. They're breaking through, reaching that critical mass where cult concern becomes genuine cultural force. Seeing them at Brooklyn Paramount now means catching them at a specific moment — established enough to command a room this size, but still hungry enough to destroy it.

## The Prediction

This is going to be messy. Not in a bad way — messy in the way that matters. The crowd will be a mix of breakcore veterans who've been waiting years for a show like this, *Neon White* fans who discovered Machine Girl through gaming and are about to get educated, and curious locals who saw "Brooklyn Paramount" and "electronic music" and decided to take a chance. Those last ones are in for a revelation.

Expect the pit to be active. Machine Girl's music demands physical response, and the crowd that shows up for this will know the score. Expect to hear material spanning their catalog — early chaos, mid-period refinement, recent soundtrack work — woven together into something that makes sense only in the moment. Expect the live drums to add a dimension that recordings can't capture, a human element cutting through the digital noise.

By the end, the room will feel different. That's the promise of a show like this. Not entertainment, not distraction, but transformation. You don't walk out of a Machine Girl set the same person who walked in. You walk out with your nervous system rewired, your expectations recalibrated, your sense of what electronic music can do expanded.

## The Verdict

This is essential for anyone who thinks electronic music has gotten too safe, too predictable, too focused on vibe curation and algorithm-friendly playlists. Machine Girl represents something older and more vital — the idea that music can be dangerous, can challenge you, can leave marks.

For the breakcore-curious, this is your entry point. For the already-converted, this is a chance to see one of the most vital projects in experimental electronic music doing what they do best in a room that will amplify their intensity. For everyone else — the techno heads, the house kids, the casual club-goers — this is a chance to step outside your comfort zone and remember why you started caring about this music in the first place.

Brooklyn Paramount is about to host something genuinely unhinged. Don't miss it.

**Rating: 4.5/5** — A rare chance to see one of experimental electronic music's most vital projects in a storied Brooklyn venue. Machine Girl don't play by the rules, and that's exactly why you need to be there.
