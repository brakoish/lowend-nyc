# 7 Tracks That Moved NYC Floors This Month

**New York City — March 2026**

Four weekends. Four warehouses in Brooklyn. Three basements in Manhattan. That one Ridgewood spot that never starts on time and somehow still runs until 8 AM. I've been bouncing between them all, and here's what I've noticed: certain tracks keep showing up. Not because DJs are copying each other—because these records *work*. They solve problems. They create moments. They make a room full of strangers feel like a single organism for three to seven minutes at a time.

These are the seven tracks that have been killing it in NYC clubs this month.

---

## 1. **Prospa — "This Rhythm" (feat. RAHH)**
*Label: Polydor | Released: January 2026*

**The Vibe:** Euphoric UK house with gospel-tinged vocals and piano stabs that hit like serotonin injections.

**Why It Works in NYC:** This city has always had a weird relationship with UK house. We love the energy, but sometimes the references don't land the same way they do in Leeds or London. "This Rhythm" bridges that gap—it's classic enough for the heads who know their 90s piano house history, but immediate enough that a first-timer at Brooklyn Storehouse feels it in their chest without needing the context.

**Who's Playing It:** Prospa themselves dropped it during their B2B with Josh Baker at Brooklyn Storehouse, but I've heard it in sets from local selectors at Basement, Knockdown Center, and even that afterhours spot in Bushwick that I'm not supposed to name. It's become a peak-time staple—the kind of track that earns a hands-in-the-air moment without feeling cheap.

**The Energy:** Build, release, repeat. The vocal hook ("I need this rhythm") lands every single time.

---

## 2. **Oppidan — "hold tight" (with camoufly)**
*Label: Night Bass | Released: February 2026*

**The Vibe:** UK garage meets bass house—2-step rhythms with a low-end weight that rattles ribcages.

**Why It Works in NYC:** Oppidan has been converting American crowds one show at a time, and "hold tight" is her secret weapon. The UKG revival is having a real moment in Brooklyn right now—maybe it's the post-pandemic hunger for anything that feels communal and joyous, maybe it's just that the sound finally found its footing here. This track sits at the perfect intersection: garage swing for the purists, bass weight for the bass heads.

**Who's Playing It:** Oppidan obviously, but also the Night Bass residents, the 24 Hour Garage Girls crew, and a surprising number of techno DJs who use it as a palette cleanser between harder cuts. I heard it at both a SILO warehouse party and a more mainstream room in Manhattan within the same week.

**The Energy:** Bouncy, infectious, impossible not to move to. The kind of track that makes people who "don't dance" suddenly find themselves two-stepping.

---

## 3. **Taiki Nulight — "Stay in Your Lane"**
*Label: Night Bass | Released: Late 2024 (still burning in 2026)*

**The Vibe:** Grime-influenced bass music at 140 BPM—dubstep roots with modern production sheen.

**Why It Works in NYC:** NYC has always had a soft spot for the 140 spectrum, from the early dubstep days at Dub War to the current wave of bass music that's been creeping back into house and techno sets. Taiki's return to dubstep territory with this EP wasn't nostalgia—it was a reminder that he can operate in any lane he chooses. "Stay in Your Lane" works because it's *rude* in the best way. It demands attention.

**Who's Playing It:** The bass music faithful at spots like The Caverns and Bowery Ballroom (where Taiki played recently), but also adventurous techno selectors who aren't afraid to dip below 128 BPM for a moment of pure weight. I've heard it in sets at Basement from DJs who usually stay in the 130+ range.

**The Energy:** Dark, aggressive, physical. This is the track you play when you want to remind the room that bass music still has teeth.

---

## 4. **8ULENTINA — "Metal Clip"**
*Label: Club Chai | Released: 2025*

**The Vibe:** Middle Eastern club experiments—tempered metallic beats, muddled string melodies, ritualistic energy.

**Why It Works in NYC:** NYC's diasporic electronic scene has been growing steadily, and 8ULENTINA represents the vanguard. "Metal Clip" shouldn't work on a typical club system—it's too weird, too specific, too tied to traditions that most of the room didn't grow up with. But that's exactly why it lands. In a city this diverse, there's an appetite for music that doesn't pander, that brings its full self to the floor. The Nowadays crowd has been particularly receptive.

**Who's Playing It:** 8ULENTINA in their live sets, obviously, but also the more adventurous selectors at Nowadays, H0l0, and the experimental-leaning warehouse parties. It's not a mainstream weapon, but it's become a secret handshake among DJs who are looking for something that breaks the mold.

**The Energy:** Hypnotic, otherworldly, transportive. This is music for the heads who want to be taken somewhere unfamiliar.

---

## 5. **Steen — "Untitled 12" (SILO Dub)**
*Label: Self-Released | Released: March 2026*

**The Vibe:** Brooklyn techno—functional, dubby, built for SILO's Danley system.

**Why It Works in NYC:** Sometimes the best tracks are the ones you can't buy. Steen's been testing this dubplate at Open Decks and his own sets for weeks now, and it's become a calling card. It's not revolutionary on paper—just solid, driving techno with a dubby warmth that fills the room without battering it. But in context, at SILO, on that system, with that crowd? It hits different. This is what local knowledge sounds like.

**Who's Playing It:** Steen, primarily, though I've heard rumors of a few other locals getting their hands on it. That's the thing about dubs—they create scarcity, which creates demand. When this finally gets a proper release, every Brooklyn techno DJ is going to have it in their bag.

**The Energy:** Workmanlike, relentless, deeply satisfying. The kind of track that keeps a room moving at 3 AM without needing tricks.

---

## 6. **Josh Baker — "The One"**
*Label: Stress Records | Released: February 2026*

**The Vibe:** UK tech house with a groove that locks in and doesn't let go.

**Why It Works in NYC:** Josh Baker represents the deeper, more restrained side of UK house—the selector's selector approach. "The One" isn't trying to be the biggest moment of the night. It's trying to be the *right* moment. NYC crowds can be jaded (we've heard everything, or at least we think we have), so a track that earns its response through patience and precision stands out. This one builds slowly, rewards attention, and pays off exactly when it should.

**Who's Playing It:** Baker himself, during that Prospa B2B and his solo sets, but also the UK house contingent that's been making regular trips to Brooklyn. I've heard it at Good Room, at smaller spots in Manhattan, and at a particularly memorable sunrise set at a warehouse in East Williamsburg.

**The Energy:** Locked-in, hypnotic, confident. This is music for dancers who trust the DJ enough to go on a journey.

---

## 7. **Arushi Jain — "Zid" (Live Edit)**
*Label: Leaving Records | Released: 2024 (live versions circulating 2026)*

**The Vibe:** Hindustani classical meets modular synthesis—vocal drones processed into architecture.

**Why It Works in NYC:** This one's a curveball. "Zid" isn't a club track in the traditional sense. It's from Jain's album *Delight*, and in its studio form, it's beautiful but not exactly peak-time material. But the live edit—stretched out, processed through her modular system, allowed to evolve and surprise—has been showing up in the more adventurous rooms. NYC has always had space for the weird and wonderful, and this track proves that "experimental" doesn't have to mean "unapproachable."

**Who's Playing It:** Jain in her live sets, but also DJs who aren't afraid to take risks. I've heard it at Nowadays during ambient-focused nights, at H0l0's more experimental programming, and even as a closing track at a warehouse party that started hard and ended in something approaching transcendence.

**The Energy:** Contemplative, expansive, healing. The track you play when you want the room to remember why they came out in the first place.

---

## The Takeaway

Seven tracks, seven different approaches, all finding their place in NYC's ecosystem. That's the thing about this city—we're not a monoculture. A room that goes wild for Prospa's euphoria might be the same room that loses themselves in 8ULENTINA's ritual. The techno heads at SILO can appreciate Steen's functional precision while still making space for Arushi Jain's experimental drones.

The common thread? These tracks all bring something *specific*. They're not algorithm-friendly background noise. They're statements of intent, whether that intent is to make you dance, to make you think, or just to remind you that electronic music still has the capacity to surprise.

I'll be back next month with another roundup. Until then: keep digging, keep dancing, and if you hear something that moves you—whether it's on this list or not—tell someone about it. That's how scenes stay alive.
