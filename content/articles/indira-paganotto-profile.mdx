# Indira Paganotto Is the Hardest Working DJ in Acid Techno

**Gran Canaria — March 2026**

I first heard Indira Paganotto's name in a Brooklyn warehouse three years ago. Someone was playing a track with flamenco guitar samples layered over a punishing 303 line—completely wrong on paper, completely undeniable on the floor. I Shazamed it standing in a puddle of spilled beer and body heat. "Jambo." ARTCORE001. That was my introduction.

Since then, I've watched her climb from underground curiosity to DJ Mag Top 100 regular (#36 this year, up from #49 in 2024). But here's what separates her from the usual festival-circuit ascent: she built her own infrastructure. While most artists chase label deals, Paganotto founded ARTCORE in 2022—a label, event series, and merch line that operates entirely on her terms. No middlemen. No compromise.

That's not hustle. That's ownership. And in 2026, it's increasingly rare.

## The Goa Connection

Paganotto's father was a DJ in Goa during the '80s, spinning acid house and trance between shifts with Doctors Without Borders. Her mother preferred opera and classical. Growing up in Gran Canaria with that particular parental combination—Italian mother, Spanish father, Goa trance vinyl scattered everywhere—gave her a musical vocabulary that most techno producers simply don't have.

She started DJing at 16 at the Moon Club in Las Palmas. By her own account, she was a "macho girl" into surfing and skating, more interested in Detroit techno and Chicago house than her dad's "hippie music." She built her own vinyl collection, taught herself to produce, and spent over a decade releasing lean club cuts on labels like Second State, KNTXT, and Octopus before anyone outside the underground knew her name.

That decade matters. Most "overnight successes" in dance music are actually five-year stories. Paganotto's was fifteen. She's been producing since before SoundCloud algorithms, before Instagram followers determined booking fees, before techno became the default music for luxury brand campaigns. She earned her position the old way: by being better than everyone else for longer than anyone else could sustain.

## Psy-Techno: The Sound No One Asked For

Let's be honest: psy-techno was not a gap in the market. Before Paganotto, the intersection of psytrance and techno was mostly theoretical. Psytrance had its festivals, its dedicated following, its 145-BPM intensity. Techno had its warehouses, its stripped-back aesthetic, its suspicion of anything too melodic or too obvious. They were parallel universes.

Paganotto crashed them together. Her sound takes the rolling basslines and hypnotic repetition of techno and injects them with psytrance's melodic complexity and emotional openness. Flamenco guitars. Eastern scales. Vocals that sound like incantations. The 303 isn't just an accent. It's a lead instrument, screaming through tracks like "Guns & Horses" and "Lions of God."

The purists hated it at first. Too trancey for the techno heads, too techno for the psy crowd. But the dancefloor doesn't lie. I've seen her play at Amnesia Ibiza—arguably the most commercial room on the island—and watched her convert tech-house tourists into psy-techno believers in real-time. The residency she holds there with Joseph Capriati's Metamorfosi party series shouldn't work on paper. It works because she's relentless.

## ARTCORE: Building the Machine

Most artists start labels as vanity projects—somewhere to release their own music when no one else will. ARTCORE is different. From the beginning, Paganotto treated it as a platform: ARTCORE001 was her own "Guns & Horses" EP, but she immediately began signing other artists, building a roster that shares her aesthetic without cloning her sound.

The label's visual identity is unmistakable. Bold typography, mythological imagery, a color palette that suggests both ancient ritual and future technology. It's branding that understands its audience: people who want their techno to feel like an experience, not just a genre.

But ARTCORE isn't just a label. It's an event series that has toured globally—10 dates in 2024 alone. It's a merchandise line that actually sells (have you seen the ARTCORE jerseys? They're everywhere at festivals now). It's a universe that fans can inhabit, not just music they can stream.

That's the difference between an artist and a brand. Paganotto is both, but she's built the brand to serve the art, not the other way around.

## The Collaborations That Matter

In 2023, Paganotto released "White Horse" with Nina Kraviz on трип. The pairing made perfect sense: Kraviz has always operated in the darker corners of psy-influenced techno, while Paganotto brings a more open, progressive energy. The track splits the difference—creepy and ecstatic simultaneously.

Her 2024 collaboration with Joseph Capriati, "Mantra," was equally significant. Capriati is Italian techno royalty, a veteran with decades of experience and a sound that bridges traditional and contemporary. Their studio sessions at STMPD in Amsterdam reportedly involved actual instruments—guitars, vocals, the kind of creative exchange that most techno productions skip in favor of DAW efficiency.

And then there's the Depeche Mode remix. When you're asked to remix one of the most influential electronic acts in history, you've arrived. The fact that she reportedly leaned into the minimal, darker aspects of her sound for that project—rather than the more accessible psy-techno she plays at festivals—says something about her priorities. She's not chasing the biggest audience. She's following her instincts, even when they lead away from the obvious move.

## The Relentless Schedule

Here's what "hardest working" actually means: Paganotto's 2024 calendar included Tomorrowland, Awakenings, DC-10, fabric, Space Miami, Ushuaïa, and a nine-date Ibiza residency. That's not counting the ARTCORE tour dates, the studio sessions, the label management, the press obligations.

I've watched DJs burn out on half that schedule. The travel alone—Gran Canaria to Amsterdam to Ibiza to Miami to wherever the next festival is—would break most people. Paganotto treats it like a job. Not in the cynical sense, but in the professional sense: this is the work, and she does the work.

She's also maintained a relationship with the underground that many artists abandon once they reach festival-headliner status. In her DJ Mag interview last year, she said: "I always think, even if you are a big artist, you can't forget where you come from; the underground. It's the small clubs, the small promoters, this is the magic of what we are making."

That's not press-release speak. That's someone who remembers playing empty rooms, who understands that the pyramid only exists because of the base.

## The NYC Question

Paganotto hasn't played New York as much as she should have. Brooklyn's techno scene—particularly rooms like Basement, Nowadays, and the various warehouse spaces that operate in the gray areas of legality—should be her natural habitat. The city's appetite for acid-tinged, emotionally open techno is voracious. Artists like SPFDJ, Héctor Oaks, and KiNK have found second homes here.

Paganotto's played the Brooklyn Mirage, sure. But she hasn't done the residency circuit, hasn't built the kind of local following that turns casual fans into devoted believers. That needs to change. If any artist deserves a quarterly ARTCORE night at a venue like Knockdown Center or Elsewhere, it's her.

The infrastructure is there. The audience is there. The only question is whether she'll commit to the city the way she's committed to Ibiza, to Miami, to the European festival circuit.

## The Sound in 2026

Paganotto's recent releases show an artist still evolving. "Mantra" with Capriati has the collaborative energy of two veterans pushing each other. Her solo material continues to refine the psy-techno formula without simplifying it. The ARTCORE releases from other artists—she's signing records, not just distributing them—suggest a curatorial vision that's expanding beyond her own sound.

What's striking is the consistency. She hasn't diluted her aesthetic to chase bigger stages. If anything, she's doubled down on the elements that made her divisive in the first place: the melodic complexity, the cultural references, the refusal to treat techno as a purely functional genre.

That's the hardest thing to maintain in this industry: conviction. The pressure to conform increases with every booking, every chart position, every brand partnership. Paganotto has somehow managed to become more successful while becoming more herself. That's not just hard work. That's integrity.

## The Verdict

Indira Paganotto is not the future of techno. She's the present, and she's been the present for longer than most people realize. The psy-techno sound she pioneered is now a recognized subgenre. The ARTCORE model—artist-as-label-founder-as-event-promoter—is being copied by others who see the independence it enables.

But copying the model isn't the same as having the vision. Paganotto's advantage isn't structural. It's artistic. She hears connections that other producers miss. She trusts her instincts even when they lead to commercial risk. She works harder than artists with half her talent and twice her resources.

That's why the "hardest working" label sticks. Not because of the schedule, though the schedule is brutal. Because of the sustained creative effort, the refusal to coast, the continued hunger after fifteen years in the game.

I've seen her play twice. Both times, the room was transformed—not just energized, but altered. That's the Goa influence, the sound healing background she doesn't talk about much but clearly absorbed. The understanding that dance music can be more than entertainment. That it can be ritual.

In 2026, that's increasingly rare. Most festival techno is designed for playlist placement, for algorithmic discovery, for the 30-second TikTok clip. Paganotto's music demands more. It demands attention, endurance, surrender. It demands that you meet her where she is, not the other way around.

That's not just hard work. That's art.

See you at ARTCORE.
