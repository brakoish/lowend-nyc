# Kevin Saunderson at Paragon: The Belleville Three Come to Brooklyn

**Brooklyn — March 2026**

I almost didn't believe it when the Paragon flyer hit my feed. Kevin Saunderson. In a 270-cap room. For like, forty bucks.

Let me be clear: this isn't one of those "legend" bookings where some DJ who had a hit in 1995 plays the same four records to bored tourists. Saunderson actually invented this shit. Born in Brooklyn, moved to Belleville, Michigan at nine, met Juan Atkins and Derrick May in high school. The Belleville Three. Detroit techno. The whole thing starts with them.

When I saw the announcement, I felt that jolt. The real kind. The kind that doesn't happen much anymore because I've been doing this too long and I've seen too much.

## The man, not the myth

Here's what you need to know about Saunderson that the Wikipedia page won't tell you.

He could've stopped in 1988 and still be one of the most important figures in electronic music. That Reese bassline from "Just Want Another Chance"? Created on a Casio CZ-1000 with phase distortion synthesis. Most sampled bassline in jungle and DnB history. The "Amen break" of low-end. Every time your ribcage rattles at a bass night, that's Saunderson's ghost in the machine.

As Inner City, he had nine UK top 40 hits. "Big Fun." "Good Life." Six million records sold. But here's the thing: he didn't sell out to get there. Those tracks worked in warehouses at 4 AM and on daytime radio. That's nearly impossible to pull off. Most artists who try for the crossover end up making garbage that satisfies nobody. Saunderson threaded that needle.

Under aliases like E-Dancer, Tronik House, The Reese Project, he's spent four decades refusing to become a museum piece. His KMS label launched countless careers. His sets still go places. He's not playing the hits and cashing checks — he's still digging, still evolving, still showing up week after week.

I caught him a few years back at a festival. What stuck with me wasn't the technical skill, though he was flawless. It was the joy. Four decades in and he still clearly loves this. The way he reads a room, builds tension, releases it. That's not nostalgia. That's mastery.

## The room matters

Paragon sits at 990 Broadway, right where Bed-Stuy bleeds into Bushwick, under the J/M/Z tracks. 270 capacity. Soundsystem that hits harder than it should for the size. Safwat Riad designed the interior with church references — regal DJ booth hovering above the stacks, mirrored balcony throwing light everywhere. Dramatic but not pretentious. Intimate but not cramped.

I like Paragon because it knows what it is. It's not a Manhattan bottle service club pretending to care about underground music. It's not some cavernous warehouse where you lose all human connection. It's a dance club for people who actually want to dance, run by people who know the soundsystem matters more than the VIP list.

They've hosted everything from fashion shows to 6 AM techno sets. They respect the music. That's what you need for Saunderson. He doesn't need pyrotechnics or LED walls. He needs a good system, a dark room, and bodies ready to move.

Paragon gives him all three.

## Why I'm actually going

I see a lot of shows. I write about a lot of DJs. I try not to throw around "legend" because every press release calls someone a legend now. But Saunderson actually is one. Not because he's been around a long time. Because he changed the course of music history.

When he plays "Big Fun" or drops something with that Reese bassline, he's not just playing records. He's playing history. He's showing you where you came from. And he could easily coast — show up, play the hits, collect the check. Plenty from his era do exactly that. But Saunderson never became a nostalgia act. His sets are forward-looking, new music alongside classics. He understands techno is alive, still evolving, and he's still contributing to that evolution.

There's something profound about standing in a room with someone who was there at the beginning. Who helped define what this music could be. Who's still doing it with passion four decades later.

## What I'm expecting

A history lesson and a party, simultaneously. Saunderson can educate without lecturing, pay homage without getting stuck in nostalgia. He'll play the Inner City classics because they still work — "Good Life" still destroys rooms regardless of how many times you've heard it. But he'll also play the new cuts, the underground tracks, the stuff that reminds you techno isn't dead and buried.

The Paragon soundsystem should handle the low-end that defines his sound. That Reese bassline needs room to breathe. Based on my nights there, I'm confident they'll deliver.

The crowd will be a mix. The heads who know exactly who they're seeing. The younger ravers who might not know the history but know a good party. The Brooklyn regulars who trust Paragon's bookings. All of them in a room with one of the people who made all of this possible.

That's the thing about seeing a true originator. It's not just the music — though the music will be excellent. It's the connection to something larger than one night. When Saunderson plays, he's channeling four decades of innovation, struggle, joy, collective experience. He's a living bridge between techno's past and its future.

For one night in Brooklyn, we get to stand on that bridge with him.

**Rating: 5/5** — Essential. A rare chance to witness a genuine pioneer in an intimate room. This is why New York's club scene still matters.
