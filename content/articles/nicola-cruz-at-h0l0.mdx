# Nicola Cruz at H0L0: Andean Rhythms in a Ridgewood Warehouse

**Ridgewood, Queens — April 11, 2026**

I've been to enough electronic shows to know when someone's faking it. When the "world music" elements are just samples ripped from YouTube. When the "cultural fusion" is code for "I added a pan flute preset to a four-on-the-floor beat." Nicola Cruz isn't faking it. That's why I'm writing about this show.

Cruz is playing H0L0 on April 11th. It's a ReSolute party, which already tells you something. They don't book tourist acts. Cruz has been building his sound out of Quito's electronic scene since the early 2010s, back when everyone else in South America was trying to sound like Berlin. He went the other direction, deep into the Andean highlands, pulling from indigenous rhythms and folk traditions that most electronic producers wouldn't know existed.

## The sound: not your average "tropical bass"

Cruz broke through in 2015 with *Prender el Alma* on ZZK Records. If you missed it then, go back and listen. It's still ahead of most of what's coming out now. The guy was processing charango loops through analog filters, using bombo legüero drums to trigger 808 patterns, layering quena flutes over sub-bass that hits you in the chest. It didn't sound like anything else in 2015, and it still doesn't.

What separates Cruz from the pack is that he's not using Andean music as decoration. He's not dropping a "world music" sample over a techno beat and calling it fusion. His productions are built from the ground up with traditional instruments and electronics in actual conversation. Listen to "Colibria." That syncopated rhythm could've come from a pre-Columbian ceremony, but it's the backbone of a hypnotic techno track that works at 2 AM in a warehouse. "Cumbia del Olvido" takes the Colombian form and stretches it into something almost psychedelic, but it never loses the thread.

He followed up with *Siku* in 2019 and *Cantos de Visión* in 2022, and the sound got deeper, more complex. He started recording in remote villages, working with traditional musicians, building a sonic archive that most electronic producers wouldn't bother with. The music got more layered, less interested in fitting dancefloor expectations.

But here's what matters: it still works on dancefloors. Cruz has toured this live show through Fusion, MUTEK, Nuits Sonores. The kind of festivals where audiences expect to be challenged. He delivers.

## What to expect at H0L0

Cruz is DJing, not playing live, so expect something more floor-focused than his albums. But his DJ sets still have that Cruz DNA. Driving rhythms that don't settle into predictable patterns. Organic textures that sound like they were recorded in mountain villages because they were. Moments where a melody cuts through the percussion and the room stops breathing for a second.

The "Curates" billing matters too. He's picking the supporting acts, and Cruz has good taste. Expect artists who share his dedication to music that knows where it comes from while pushing forward. ReSolute's involvement is a good sign. They've been throwing parties in New York for years with a clear aesthetic, prioritizing vision over easy crowd-pleasing.

## Why H0L0 is the right room

H0L0 has been holding down the Ridgewood/Bushwick border since 2017. It's an 800-capacity warehouse on Wyckoff Avenue, and it's become essential by sticking to a simple formula. Keep it intimate. Keep the sound good. Don't turn it into a bottle-service nightmare.

For Cruz, the intimacy matters. His music relies on subtlety and detail. You need to be close enough to hear the textures, to feel the low-end properly, to catch the interactions between acoustic and electronic elements. At a bigger venue, that gets lost.

The sound system at H0L0 punches above its weight. I've heard everything from noise experiments to straight techno in that room, and it delivers clarity without harshness, weight without mud. For music as texturally rich as Cruz's, that matters. You don't want his carefully recorded charangos getting buried in a boomy mess.

The crowd at H0L0 tends to know what they're getting into. This isn't a bridge-and-tunnel spot for people who want a generic night out. The people who trek to Ridgewood are there for the music. They're willing to engage with something unfamiliar. That's the right audience for this.

## Why this show actually matters

Electronic music has spent the last decade consolidating around a handful of dominant sounds: the Berghain techno template, the polished house of major festival stages, the tech-house formulas that have colonized dancefloors everywhere. The cost is obvious. Regional differences are flattening out, clubs in Buenos Aires and Berlin and Brooklyn are starting to sound interchangeable.

Cruz pushes against that. His music insists on specificity, on the value of local traditions, on the idea that electronic music can be rooted in particular places without being parochial or nostalgic. He's not making museum pieces. He's making contemporary music that draws on centuries of Andean practice.

There's a political dimension too. In an era of resurgent nationalism, Cruz's work shows a different kind of cultural pride. One that invites instead of excludes. His music is deeply Ecuadorian without being defensive. It assumes audiences anywhere can appreciate what it offers, that difference doesn't need to be explained or diluted.

For New Yorkers, this is a rare shot. Cruz doesn't tour the US constantly, and when he does, it's often larger venues or festival slots that don't allow for the kind of intimate, extended set he'll play at H0L0. This is the kind of booking that justifies the rent, the noise, the general grind of living here. Because occasionally, on a Saturday night in Queens, you can walk into something that actually expands what electronic music can do.

## The call

I'm expecting a long set. Cruz doesn't rush. He'll take time establishing atmosphere, building energy gradually, letting the room settle into his frequency. Early hours will be deeper, more atmospheric. As the night goes, the rhythms will intensify, the percussion will get more insistent, but even at peak intensity, there will be that Cruz quality. The sense that every element was chosen deliberately, that nothing is disposable.

The crowd will start curious, maybe not knowing Cruz as well as the usual techno circuit. By the end, if it goes right, they'll be converted. That's how it works with artists outside the mainstream. One room at a time, one person at a time, until the influence becomes impossible to ignore.

H0L0's warehouse aesthetic, exposed brick and industrial fixtures, gives Cruz a neutral backdrop that won't fight the music. The sound system should handle his low-end without strain. And the crowd, that particular H0L0 mix of dedicated heads and curious explorers, should give him the energy he needs.

If you're unsure: go. Bring an open mind and comfortable shoes. Be ready for rhythms that might be unfamiliar, melodies that don't resolve the way you expect, structures that take time to develop. Let the music teach you how to listen. That's what Cruz offers. Not just a night out, but an actual expansion of what this music can be.

**Verdict:** Essential. One of the most interesting bookings H0L0 has made this year. If you care about where electronic music is headed, or you just want a dancefloor experience that wasn't designed by algorithm, be there.
