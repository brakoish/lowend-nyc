# Prospa B2B Josh Baker at Brooklyn Storehouse: UK House Meets Brooklyn's Warehouse Underground

**Brooklyn Navy Yard — March 20, 2026**

There's a particular thing that happens when two selectors who actually get each other step up together. Not those polite tag-team sets where each artist plays their biggest tracks and hands over with a fist bump. I mean the real back-to-back, the kind where the conversation happens live, where one person's record becomes the launchpad for the other's next move, where the line between "your set" and "my set" dissolves into something neither could make alone.

That's what Prospa and Josh Baker are promising at Brooklyn Storehouse this Friday. And honestly? It's the kind of booking that reminds me why Teksupport still matters in this city.

## The venue

Let's talk about the room first, because it matters. Brooklyn Storehouse sits in the Navy Yard, that sprawling industrial complex along the East River that's become ground zero for the kind of large-scale electronic events that used to happen in actual warehouses before developers converted them all to luxury condos. The Storehouse is exactly what the name suggests: a massive, raw space with high ceilings, concrete floors, and enough square footage to swallow a few thousand bodies without feeling tight.

Teksupport has made this their Brooklyn home base, and I get why. The production values are serious (Funktion-One or similar, proper lighting rigs, the kind of sound that separates a real rave from a glorified bar night), but the vibe stays gritty. No VIP bottle service nonsense. The crowd is there for the music, not the Instagram backdrop. In a city where the underground keeps getting pushed further to the margins, Brooklyn Storehouse is one of the few spots big enough to host serious talent while still feeling like an actual party.

## Prospa: Leeds' rave revivalists

If you haven't been paying attention to Prospa, you've been sleeping on one of the most consistent stories in UK house music the past few years. Harvey Blumler and Gosha Smith, the duo behind the name, have been climbing fast since their 2018 breakout single "Prayer" put Stress Records back on the map and announced them as serious players in the UK house revival.

Their trajectory makes sense when you know where they came from. Both cut their teeth in Leeds, that Northern city that's produced so much of the UK's best dance music. They met in the early 2010s, bonding over production before either had fully found their sound. Blumler was making dubstep-adjacent beats; Smith was exploring jazz-inflected hip-hop. When they converged, first on deep house and then on the classic rave sounds that would become their signature, something clicked.

What sets Prospa apart from the wave of producers mining the 90s for inspiration is that they understand revival isn't enough. Yeah, they reference the piano stabs, the breakbeats, the euphoric vocal chops of classic UK house and rave. But they're not making museum pieces. Tracks like "This Rhythm" (featuring RAHH), "If You Want My Loving," and the more recent "You Don't Own Me" feel immediate, built for today's dancefloors even as they nod to yesterday's.

Their 2024-2025 run has been especially strong. "This Rhythm" became a staple in sets across the UK and beyond, that combination of driving bassline and emotive vocal hitting the sweet spot between peak-time energy and actual feeling. They've been releasing on Polydor and Stress Records, balancing major-label reach with underground credibility. And their DJ sets, this is key, have developed a reputation for being relentless, joyous, and technically sharp.

Prospa gets something fundamental about UK house: it's not about minimalism or subtlety. It's about euphoria, about collective release, about building moments where the whole room feels like one thing. When they play, they play for that moment.

## Josh Baker: the selector's selector

Josh Baker is a different kind of artist, though one equally rooted in the UK tradition. Where Prospa came up as producers who learned to DJ, Baker feels like a DJ's DJ. Someone whose reputation was built on selection, on reading rooms, on the kind of encyclopedic knowledge of house music that only comes from years of digging and playing out.

His style sits somewhere between UK house, tech house, and the deeper shades of garage that never quite died in Britain. He's not chasing trends. While the wider world of electronic music swings between hyperpop and minimal techno, Baker has stayed focused on what works: tight grooves, well-chosen vocals, basslines that move bodies without beating them over the head.

The Beatport data tells part of the story. Baker's name appears alongside Prospa's on various releases and remixes, suggesting they've been orbiting the same circles for a while. But the real connection is harder to pin down. Both artists understand UK dance music as a living tradition, not a retro exercise. Both can play peak-time sets that never sacrifice musicality for cheap thrills. Both have that Northern sensibility: workmanlike, unpretentious, focused on the dancefloor above all else.

Baker's reputation has been growing steadily, particularly among those who pay attention to the deeper side of UK house. He's the kind of artist who might not have the massive streaming numbers or festival mainstage slots yet, but who other DJs consistently name-check as someone to watch. His productions, when he releases them, tend to be functional, well-crafted tools that work in sets without demanding attention. His DJ sets, by contrast, are where he shines: patient, knowledgeable, able to move between moods without losing the thread.

## Why this B2B matters

Prospa and Josh Baker don't play together often. As far as I can tell, this Brooklyn Storehouse date might be their first back-to-back. That's significant.

The UK house scene is tight. Everyone knows everyone, plays with everyone, remixes everyone. But there's a difference between the scene's regular circuit and these moments where two distinct voices get thrown together in a new context. When Prospa and Baker step up together at Brooklyn Storehouse, they're not just playing a gig. They're figuring something out live, in front of a few thousand New Yorkers who might not know either name as well as a London crowd would.

That's exciting for a few reasons. First, there's the unpredictability. A solo set gives you a pretty good idea of what you're going to get. A B2B, especially one between artists who haven't developed a routine together, could go anywhere. Will Prospa's euphoric, piano-heavy moments mesh with Baker's more restrained, groove-focused approach? Will they find common ground in the classics, or push each other into new territory? Nobody knows, probably including them. That's the point.

Second, there's the transatlantic angle. UK house has always had a complicated relationship with American audiences. The sounds that dominate British clubs, UK garage, bassline, the particular strain of piano-driven house that Prospa specializes in, have never quite broken through to the mainstream in the US the way they have at home. When UK artists come to Brooklyn, they're often playing to crowds who are hearing this stuff fresh, without the years of cultural context that UK audiences bring. That can be liberating. It can also be challenging. Prospa and Baker will have to translate, not water down, their sound for a room that might not know all the references.

Finally, there's the Teksupport factor. This promoter has become essential to New York's electronic music ecosystem, particularly for the kind of large-scale warehouse events that used to happen more organically. They bring serious talent, techno legends, UK garage revivalists, house mainstays, to spaces like Brooklyn Storehouse, Brooklyn Hangar, and their various other venues. The crowd they draw is knowledgeable, committed, there for the right reasons. It's not the easiest room to play, but it's one where real moments can happen.

## The prediction

I'm expecting a long night. Teksupport events tend to run late, and with Mink also on the bill, there's going to be plenty of music before Prospa and Baker even touch the decks. But when they do, when those two names appear together on the lineup, when they step up for their B2B, I think we're going to get something special.

Prospa will bring the energy, the euphoria, the big moments. Baker will bring the selection, the patience, the deep knowledge of how to build and release tension. Together, they might find a middle ground that neither would reach alone. A set that's both emotionally satisfying and technically impressive, that honors the UK tradition without being trapped by it, that works the Brooklyn Storehouse room into the kind of collective frenzy that justifies staying out until 4 AM on a Friday.

The UK house revival has been building for years now. Prospa is one of its most visible success stories. Baker represents its deeper, more underground current. Seeing them together, in a space like Brooklyn Storehouse, feels like a statement. Not just about where UK house is, but about where it might go next.

If you're on the fence: go. This is the kind of booking that doesn't happen often. Two artists at different points in their careers, with different strengths, coming together for one night in a city that's hungry for what they're selling. The warehouse is waiting. The sound system is primed. All that's missing is you.

**Rating: 4.5/5** — Essential for fans of UK house, tech house, and proper warehouse raves. The B2B format adds unpredictability to an already compelling pairing.
