# Nicola Cruz Curates at H0L0: Andean Rhythms Meet Ridgewood's Warehouse Grit

**Ridgewood, Queens — April 11, 2026**

There's a moment in certain electronic sets where the room suddenly feels bigger than itself. Not bigger in the sense of scale or volume, but in depth, in history, in the sense that the music reaching your ears has traveled through time and geography to get there. Nicola Cruz specializes in that moment.

When the Ecuadorian producer takes over H0L0 on April 11th for a curated night presented by ReSolute, he's bringing more than a DJ set. He's bringing a philosophy, a methodology, a deep cultural excavation that treats electronic music not as an escape from tradition but as its continuation.

## The artist: archaeology through sound

Cruz has been operating in his own lane since he first emerged from Quito's electronic scene in the early 2010s. While his contemporaries were looking to Berlin or London for inspiration, Cruz looked inward, to the Andean highlands, to the indigenous rhythms and folk traditions of his native Ecuador. The result is a sound that doesn't sample "world music" as decoration but integrates it as foundation.

His breakthrough came with 2015's *Prender el Alma*, released on ZZK Records, the Buenos Aires label that became ground zero for South America's electronic avant-garde. The album was a revelation: charango loops processed through analog filters, bombo legüero drums triggering 808 patterns, quena flutes floating over sub-bass that could rattle ribcages. It sounded like nothing else happening in electronic music at the time, and it still sounds fresh nearly a decade later.

What distinguishes Cruz from the broader "tropical bass" movement is his refusal to treat his source material as exotic seasoning. He's not dropping Andean samples over four-on-the-floor kicks and calling it fusion. His productions are genuinely hybrid, built from the ground up with traditional instruments and electronic gear in constant conversation. You can hear the difference in tracks like "Colibria," where a syncopated rhythm that might have come from a pre-Columbian ceremony becomes the backbone of a hypnotic techno workout, or "Cumbia del Olvido," which takes the Colombian cumbia form and stretches it into something almost psychedelic.

His follow-up albums, *Siku* (2019) and the more recent *Cantos de Visión* (2022), deepened this approach. Cruz began collaborating more extensively with traditional musicians, recording in remote villages, building a sonic archive that most electronic producers wouldn't think to access. The music became more complex, more layered, more committed to its own internal logic rather than the expectations of dancefloor utility.

But here's the thing: it still works on dancefloors. Cruz has spent years touring his live show across Europe, Latin America, and beyond, and he's developed a reputation for sets that satisfy both the head and the body. He's played Fusion Festival, MUTEK, Nuits Sonores, the kinds of events where audiences expect to be challenged. He's held his own.

## The sound: tradition as technology

Trying to describe Cruz's music with genre tags feels reductive, but let's try anyway. There's techno in the pulse, house in the swing, ambient in the spaces between beats. There's the rhythmic complexity of Afro-Latin traditions, the drone-based meditation of Andean folk, the precise sound design of contemporary electronic production. It all coexists without contradiction.

His DJ sets, which is what he'll be delivering at H0L0, tend to be more floor-focused than his live performances, but they retain that distinctive Cruz sensibility. Expect driving rhythms that never quite settle into predictable patterns. Expect organic textures that sound like they were recorded in mountain villages because they probably were. Expect moments of real beauty, when a melody breaks through the percussion and the room collectively catches its breath.

He's also a skilled curator, which is why this event is billed as "Nicola Cruz Curates" rather than just a headline set. The supporting acts will reflect his aesthetic, artists who share his commitment to music that honors its roots while pushing into new territory. ReSolute, the party collective presenting the night, has a track record of thoughtful bookings that prioritize artistic vision over easy crowd-pleasing. Their involvement is a good sign.

## The venue: why H0L0 makes sense

H0L0 has become one of Queens' essential venues by staying true to a specific vision: intimate, community-focused, sonically ambitious. The 800-capacity warehouse on Wyckoff Avenue has been holding down the Ridgewood/Bushwick border since 2017, offering an alternative to the bigger, more commercial rooms that dominate Brooklyn's electronic scene.

What H0L0 offers is proximity. There's no vast gap between artist and audience, no VIP section creating hierarchy, no massive stage distancing the performer from the crowd. For an artist like Cruz, whose music relies on subtlety and detail, that intimacy matters. You need to be close enough to hear the nuances, to feel the low-end properly, to catch the interactions between acoustic and electronic elements that make his sound distinctive.

The room's sound system punches above its weight. I've heard everything from noise experiments to rolling techno in that space, and it consistently delivers clarity without harshness, weight without muddiness. For music as texturally rich as Cruz's, that's essential. You don't want his carefully recorded charangos and flutes getting lost in a boomy mess.

H0L0's crowd tends to be knowledgeable and open-minded. This isn't a venue that draws the bridge-and-tunnel crowd looking for a generic night out. The people who make the trek to Ridgewood are there for the music, willing to engage with something unfamiliar, ready to be challenged. They're the right audience for what Cruz is offering.

## Why this show matters now

Electronic music has spent the last decade consolidating around a handful of dominant sounds: the streamlined techno of Berghain and its imitators, the polished house of major festival stages, the increasingly formulaic rhythms of tech-house that have colonized dancefloors worldwide. There's been a cost to this consolidation, a flattening of regional differences, a homogenization that makes clubs in Buenos Aires, Berlin, and Brooklyn increasingly interchangeable.

Artists like Cruz push back against this flattening. His music insists on specificity, on the value of local traditions, on the idea that electronic music can be rooted in particular places and histories without being parochial or nostalgic. He's not making museum pieces. He's making contemporary music that happens to draw on centuries of Andean musical practice.

This matters politically too. In an era of resurgent nationalism and xenophobia, Cruz's work represents a different kind of cultural pride, one that doesn't exclude or demonize but invites and shares. His music is deeply Ecuadorian without being defensive about it. It assumes that audiences anywhere can appreciate what it has to offer, that difference doesn't need to be explained or diluted to be understood.

For New York audiences, this show is a rare opportunity. Cruz doesn't tour the US constantly, and when he does, he often plays larger venues or festival slots that don't allow for the kind of intimate, extended set he'll be able to deliver at H0L0. This is the kind of booking that reminds you why you live in this city, why you put up with the rent and the noise and the general grind: because occasionally, on a Saturday night in Queens, you can stumble into something genuinely transformative.

## The prediction

I'm expecting a long set. Cruz doesn't do quick hits. He'll take his time establishing the atmosphere, building the energy gradually, allowing the room to settle into his particular frequency. The early hours will probably be deeper, more atmospheric, establishing the sonic world he's creating. As the night progresses, the rhythms will intensify, the percussion will become more insistent, but even at peak intensity, there will be that distinctive Cruz quality: the sense that every element has been carefully chosen, that nothing is arbitrary or disposable.

The crowd will be attentive at first, curious about this artist they might not know as well as the usual circuit of techno headliners. By the end, if everything goes right, they'll be converted, part of the global audience that Cruz has been building methodically over the past decade. This is how it works with artists who operate outside the mainstream: one room at a time, one convert at a time, until the influence becomes undeniable.

H0L0's physical space should suit him well. The warehouse aesthetic, the exposed brick and industrial fixtures, provides a neutral backdrop that won't compete with the music. The sound system should handle his low-end requirements without strain. And the crowd, that particular H0L0 crowd of dedicated heads and curious explorers, should give him the receptive energy that any performance needs to reach its potential.

If you're on the fence: go. Bring an open mind and comfortable shoes. Be prepared to hear rhythms that might be unfamiliar, melodies that don't resolve the way you expect, structures that take their time developing. Let the music teach you how to listen to it. That's the gift Cruz offers: not just entertainment but education, not just a night out but a genuine expansion of what electronic music can be and do.

**Rating: 4.5/5** — Essential for anyone interested in the future of electronic music, the intersection of tradition and technology, or just a really well-crafted dancefloor experience. One of the most important bookings H0L0 has made this year.
