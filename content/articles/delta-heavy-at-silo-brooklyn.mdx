# Delta Heavy at Silo Brooklyn: UK Bass Royalty Invades East Williamsburg

**East Williamsburg — March 28, 2026**

There's a particular kind of anticipation that builds when you know a room is about to get properly destroyed. Not the polite, chin-stroking appreciation of a tasteful house set. Not the gradual, hypnotic drift of a techno marathon. I'm talking about the moment when the subwoofers wake up and remind you that electronic music is supposed to be physical, confrontational, almost violent in its beauty. That's what Delta Heavy brings. And that's what's coming to Silo Brooklyn at the end of the month.

Ben Hall and Simon James have been operating as Delta Heavy since 2009, which in drum & bass years makes them elder statesmen. They've outlasted scenes, trends, and entire subgenres that came and went while they were busy refining their particular brand of cinematic bass music. When they touch down at Silo on March 28th, they'll be bringing fifteen years of accumulated knowledge about exactly how to make a soundsystem beg for mercy.

## The Duo: From "Get By" to Global Dominance

If you know one Delta Heavy track, it's probably "Get By." Released in 2012, that single did something rare: it crossed over without selling out. The track peaked at number 37 on the UK Dance Chart, which sounds modest until you realize how thoroughly it colonized the global bass music consciousness. The Gorgon City remix got radio play. The original got rinsed at festivals from Glastonbury to EDC. And the animated video, with its retro-gaming aesthetic and that unforgettable Clippit cameo, racked up millions of views on UKF's YouTube channel.

But here's the thing about Delta Heavy: they didn't stop there. So many artists who score an early hit spend the rest of their careers chasing that same formula, rehashing the same sounds, becoming their own tribute act. Hall and James went the other direction. Their debut album *Paradise Lost* (2016) was a statement of intent, sprawling across drum & bass, dubstep, and the various hybrid territories in between. Tracks like "Ghost" and "Punish My Love" showed they could do emotional depth without sacrificing the dancefloor impact that made their name.

Then came *Only in Dreams* (2019), which pushed even further into melodic territory while maintaining that signature Delta Heavy weight. By this point, they'd established a template: anthemic without being cheesy, heavy without being monotonous, technically precise without sounding clinical. It's a harder balance to strike than they make it look.

Their remix work tells its own story. Rita Ora, Avicii, DJ Fresh, Tinie Tempah — they've taken pop records and bent them to their will, always adding something distinctive rather than just slapping a DnB break over the top. That's the difference between producers who understand songcraft and those who just know how to use Serum.

## The Sound: What You're Actually Getting

Delta Heavy live is a different beast than Delta Heavy on record. In the studio, they can be meticulous, layering orchestral elements and cinematic textures, building tracks that reward headphone listening. In the booth, they're merciless.

Expect a journey through the full spectrum of bass music, anchored in drum & bass but never constrained by it. Their sets typically move between several distinct modes: the liquid moments where melody takes precedence and the crowd sways rather than moshes; the jump-up sections where the energy peaks and hands go up; the dubstep detours that remind you these guys came up in the UK bass scene, not just the DnB bubble; and the neurofunk passages where the production gets technical and the heads in the crowd start nodding with that particular appreciation of complexity.

What ties it together is their understanding of dynamics. Delta Heavy know that impact requires contrast. They'll give you a moment of relative calm, just long enough to miss the intensity, then drop something that rearranges your internal organs. The transitions are seamless — these are producers who understand that a DJ set should feel like a continuous experience, not a playlist.

And the sound design. God, the sound design. Delta Heavy's productions are characterized by enormous, meticulously crafted bass tones that seem to occupy physical space. When those sounds hit a proper system at proper volume, it's not just hearing — it's feeling. The air pressure changes. Your clothes vibrate. You become aware of your skeleton in a way that normal music doesn't require.

## The Venue: Silo's Warehouse Pedigree

Silo sits at 90 Scott Avenue in East Williamsburg, tucked into that industrial corridor that's become the last refuge for proper warehouse-scale events in Brooklyn. If you've been paying attention to NYC's electronic music scene, you already know the room. If you haven't, here's what you need to understand: this isn't a club in the traditional sense. It's a space that earned its license through years of underground operation, that carries the DNA of the Secret Loft parties that birthed it back in 2012.

The sound system is the key. Silo runs a custom Danley setup that's been refined over years of trial and error. We're talking serious wattage, carefully tuned for the room's acoustics, with enough low-end extension to handle whatever Delta Heavy throw at it. This matters more for drum & bass than almost any other genre. DnB lives and dies by sub-bass, by the physical impact of those 174 BPM breaks landing on top of frequencies that you feel more than hear. A weak system turns the music into a tinny approximation of itself. A strong system, like Silo's, makes it come alive.

The layout helps too. Unlike the cavernous main rooms at venues like Brooklyn Storehouse or Knockdown Center, Silo maintains a certain intimacy even when it's packed. You can see the DJ. You can read the crowd. The energy doesn't dissipate into empty space or get lost in VIP sections. It's a room built for focus, for the kind of shared experience that makes electronic music meaningful.

And there's history here. Silo has been hosting bass music consistently, building a crowd that understands what they're there for. This isn't a one-off rental where the staff are confused by the tempo. This is a venue that has invested in the scene, that has earned the trust of both artists and audiences. When Delta Heavy play here, they're playing to a room that knows how to receive them.

## Why This Show Matters

Drum & bass has always had a complicated relationship with New York City. The genre was born in the UK, refined in London and Bristol, and despite decades of effort from dedicated promoters and DJs, it's never quite broken through to mainstream American consciousness the way house or techno has. We get moments — a big festival slot, a viral track, a mainstream crossover — but the consistent infrastructure has been harder to maintain.

That's why nights like this feel significant. Delta Heavy aren't just any DnB act. They're a headliner-level duo with major label backing, festival pedigree, and actual chart success. When artists of their stature choose to play a warehouse in East Williamsburg rather than a marquee Manhattan club, it says something about where the energy is in this city. It validates the work that promoters and venues have been doing to build a sustainable bass music scene.

There's also the timing. Delta Heavy have been touring hard, hitting festivals and clubs across the US and Europe. They're tight, road-tested, playing with the confidence that comes from years of experience. This isn't a warm-up show or a throwaway date between bigger gigs. This is a proper headline appearance in a room that will let them do what they do best.

For the DnB-curious, this is an ideal entry point. Delta Heavy's sound is accessible without being dumbed down, heavy without being alienating. They play records you can sing along to, mixed with deeper cuts that reward closer attention. It's a masterclass in crowd-reading and energy management from two people who have been doing this at the highest level for over a decade.

## The Prediction

I'm expecting a full-scale assault. Delta Heavy don't really do subtle, and Silo's system is built for exactly the kind of punishment they're going to deliver. The room will be packed — DnB nights in this city always draw a dedicated crowd, and this is the biggest name Silo has hosted in the genre for a while.

The set will move through their catalog: "Get By" will get dropped (probably more than once, in various forms), "Ghost" will soundtrack some transcendent moment around 2 AM, and somewhere in the middle they'll play something new that nobody's heard yet, testing it on a crowd that will respond honestly. There will be moments of unexpected beauty, because Delta Heavy understand that contrast makes the heavy parts hit harder. And there will be moments of pure chaos, when the bass drops and the room becomes a single organism moving in the dark.

By the time they finish, the crowd will be different than when they started. That's the promise of a great drum & bass set, and Delta Heavy are very good at keeping that promise.

## The Verdict

This is essential for bass music heads, highly recommended for the DnB-curious, and a reminder for everyone else that there's more to electronic music than the four-on-the-floor rhythms that dominate most NYC clubs. Delta Heavy represent the UK bass tradition at its most polished and effective, and Silo is exactly the kind of room that lets that tradition flourish.

Fifteen years into their career, Hall and James are still refining their craft, still finding new ways to make soundsystems cry uncle, still building the kind of immersive experiences that turn casual listeners into lifelong converts. If you've never experienced proper drum & bass on a proper system, this is your chance. If you have, you already know why you need to be there.

**Rating: 4.5/5** — A rare chance to see UK bass royalty in an intimate warehouse setting. The combination of Delta Heavy's production pedigree and Silo's soundsystem should make this one of the DnB highlights of the year.
