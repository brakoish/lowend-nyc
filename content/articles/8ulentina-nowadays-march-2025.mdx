# 8ULENTINA and Arushi Jain Bring the Diasporic Future to Nowadays

**Ridgewood, Queens — March 18, 2026**

There's a particular frequency that Nowadays captures better than almost any room in the city. Out past the industrial stretch of Flushing Avenue, past the warehouses and auto shops, the venue sits like a concrete sanctuary—its indoor room dark and cavernous, its outdoor space sprawling and open to the Ridgewood sky. It's a place built for patience, for music that unfolds rather than drops. Tonight, that patience pays off.

The bill pairs two artists who couldn't be more geographically distant—8ULENTINA from Oakland, Arushi Jain from Delhi via San Francisco—and yet sonically, they feel like they're having the same conversation. Both are interrogating what it means to make electronic music from a diasporic position. Both are using modular systems and digital tools to reimagine traditions that were never meant to stay static.

## 8ULENTINA: The Club Chai Vision

I've been tracking 8ULENTINA since the early Club Chai days—that Oakland party series they co-founded with FOOZOOL that became a label, a radio show, and arguably one of the most important incubators for diasporic club music on the West Coast. The Pitchfork review of their *Eucalyptus* EP nailed it: "nimble movement between genres and the decentering of Western cultural narratives." But that doesn't quite capture what happens when you're standing in front of the speakers.

Their approach is sculptural. 8ULENTINA builds tracks the way they build their installation art—layering samples of darbuka and ney, those traditional Middle Eastern instruments, into structures that feel both ancient and immediate. There's a track they sometimes play out, "Metal Clip," where a tempered metallic beat anchors muddled string and flute melodies that spin off into space. It works on headphones, sure, but in a room like Nowadays—where the sound system has room to breathe—it becomes something else entirely. A ritual. A conversation between acoustic and electronic that doesn't privilege either side.

What sets 8ULENTINA apart from the wave of producers sampling "world music" (a term they would rightly dismiss) is their refusal to exoticize their own heritage. Their father's family is from Turkey, and they've spoken in interviews about hybridizing traditional Middle Eastern instruments with electronic percussion not as a gimmick, but as a way of accessing spirituality and ancestry through technology. The Alevi ritual instruments they sample aren't decoration—they're a direct line to something personal, something that exists in tension and distance for anyone in the diaspora.

Tonight, I'm expecting the ambient side of their catalog to surface more than the club tools. Tracks like "Wander Flute" with its dulled, pummeling heartbeat rhythm, or the more recent experimental work they've been teasing. But 8ULENTINA has never been predictable, and that's the point.

## Arushi Jain: The Modular Princess

If 8ULENTINA is working with Middle Eastern acoustic instruments fed through electronics, Arushi Jain approaches from the opposite direction—starting with the modular synthesizer and finding her way back to Hindustani classical tradition. She calls herself @modularprincess on socials, and the title fits: there's something regal and commanding about her command of the Eurorack system, the way she patches cables like she's conducting an orchestra of voltage.

Her 2021 album *Under the Lilac Sky* was a breakthrough—six tracks that proved you could make genuinely transportive ambient music while keeping one foot in the raga tradition. "Richer Than Blood" still gives me chills, those vocal drones processed through delay and reverb until they become architecture. But it's her live sets that really reveal her genius. I caught her opening for Suzanne Ciani a few years back at a Diva of the Diode event, and the way she held the room for forty minutes—building from near-silence to these cascading waves of harmonics—showed an artist who understands pacing as a spiritual practice.

Jain's background is in Indian classical music, but she's also deeply embedded in the experimental electronic world. Her releases on labels like Leaving Records and her collaborations with the broader modular synthesis community have positioned her as a bridge-builder between two worlds that don't always talk to each other. The Hindustani influence isn't pasted on top—it's structural, embedded in the way she thinks about time and timbre.

Her most recent album, *Delight* (2024), pushes further into song form while maintaining that drone-heavy, meditative quality. Tracks like "Zid" showcase her vocal work—layered, processed, but never losing the human breath at the center. In a live context, she tends to stretch things out, letting the modular system do what it does best: evolve, surprise, become something other than what you started with.

## Why This Bill Works

On paper, pairing a Turkish-Arab artist from Oakland with an Indian classical-trained modular synthesist from Delhi might look like a festival programmer's idea of "world electronic music." But that's not what's happening here. Both 8ULENTINA and Arushi Jain are doing something more specific and more interesting: they're using electronic music as a tool for diasporic world-building.

The Club Chai ethos that 8ULENTINA helped establish—"dismissing genres" while centering non-Western sounds and marginalized identities—finds a natural companion in Jain's practice. Both artists are skeptical of easy categorization. Both are interested in what happens when you refuse to separate "traditional" from "experimental," "acoustic" from "electronic." Both understand that the club can be a space for healing, for care, for practices that go deeper than the weekend's entertainment.

And Nowadays is the right room for this conversation. The venue has become essential to New York's underground in part because it refuses to be just one thing. Its Mister Sunday parties are legendary for their egalitarian, no-phones dancefloor ethos. Its ambient and experimental programming—often tucked into weeknights or the indoor room's quieter hours—has created space for artists who need time and attention rather than just a slot before the headliner. The sound system is tuned for detail. The crowd knows how to listen.

## The Prediction

I've seen enough bills at Nowadays to know when something special is brewing. This isn't a throw-together tour package—it's a curated pairing that actually makes sense. 8ULENTINA will likely lean into the more experimental, ambient side of their catalog, and Jain will probably stretch her modular system into territories that surprise even those familiar with her records.

The real magic, if we're lucky, might come from proximity—two artists sharing a green room, sharing ideas, maybe even sharing the stage for a moment. The diasporic electronic scene is smaller than it looks, and nights like this are where connections get made, where the next collaborations get seeded.

If you're on the fence about trekking out to Ridgewood on a Wednesday: go. This is the kind of bill that defines a venue's programming identity. It's the kind of night that reminds you why you moved to New York in the first place—or why you never left.

**Rating: 4.5/5** — Essential for fans of ambient, experimental, and diasporic electronic music. The pairing is thoughtful, the room is perfect, and both artists are operating at peak form.
