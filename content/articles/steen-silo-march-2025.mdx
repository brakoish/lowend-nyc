# Steen at SILO: The Open Decks Anchor Keeping Brooklyn's Underground Honest

East Williamsburg, March 18, 2026

I've been to enough "industry nights" in this city to know the difference between a venue that talks about community and one that actually builds it. SILO falls firmly in the second camp. If you want to understand why, look no further than tonight's Open Decks with Steen and MyCube.

This isn't a headliner drop. No $80 tickets, no radius clauses, no promoter frantically texting agents about set times. Just Steen, DJ, producer, and the steady hand behind SILO's longest-running weekly, holding space for whoever shows up with a USB and something to prove.

## Who is Steen?

If you don't know the name, you're probably not from here. That's fine. Nobody's keeping score at the door. But Steen has become a fixture in Brooklyn's techno ecosystem, less through social media theatrics and more through sheer consistency. The Open Decks series he hosts has been running for years now, a weekly ritual where the sign-up sheet fills fast and those 12-minute slots force DJs to make their point or get off the stage.

Steen's productions sit in that fertile zone between house and techno. Functional enough for the floor, weird enough to keep you listening. He's released on a handful of local imprints over the years, though you'd have to dig to find them. He's not the type to blast every Bandcamp link across Instagram. His SoundCloud tells the real story: mixes that move from dubby warm-ups to peak-time workouts without ever feeling like he's checking boxes on some "journey" template.

What sets Steen apart is his understanding of context. The guy knows SILO's room like he built it, which in a way he has. Week after week he's watched how the crowd moves, how the Danley system responds, which corners of the front room get weird when you drop the right track at the wrong time. That kind of institutional knowledge doesn't come from fly-in gigs. It comes from showing up.

## The venue: SILO's warehouse soul

SILO sits at 90 Scott Avenue in East Williamsburg, tucked into the industrial sprawl that still clings to this corner of Brooklyn despite the best efforts of real estate developers. The building's history, formerly Secret Loft, born from an underground party in a converted auto garage back in 2012, still hums through the walls. You can feel it when you walk in. This is a room that earned its license the hard way.

The front room, where Open Decks happens, runs on a custom Danley sound system that SILO's team has been tweaking since day one. The wattage numbers get thrown around, 10,000, 15,000, depending on the upgrade cycle, but what matters is how it translates. This is a system built for techno: tight low-end, clear mids, enough headroom that a producer can test their own unreleased material without wondering if the compression will translate.

The layout helps too. Unlike the cavernous main rooms at Basement or Knockdown Center, SILO's front room is intimate without being cramped. You can stand at the back and actually see the DJ's hands on the mixer. You can have a conversation without screaming, though you probably won't want to. The lighting is functional, not theatrical. Strobes when needed, darkness when not. It's a room that trusts the music to do the work.

And then there's the water station. Free, always, which shouldn't be notable but absolutely is in a city where venues treat hydration like a revenue stream. The crowd tends to skew local: Bushwick and Bed-Stuy regulars, the occasional Manhattan refugee, a healthy contingent of actual working DJs who come to support or scout. It's the kind of room where you see the same faces month after month, and that consistency builds something. Call it community if you want, though that word's been beaten to death. It's more like a shared understanding. We're here for this, specifically.

## Why tonight matters

Look, I get it. On paper, a Wednesday night Open Decks isn't the most exciting ticket in town. No Boiler Room crew filming, no Resident Advisor review coming, no afterparty hype building across group chats. But that's exactly why it matters.

NYC techno has been having a moment, again, and with that attention comes the usual suspects: the European imports playing the same five tracks, the TikTok-famous selectors who treat the booth like a content studio, the promoters stacking bills based on Spotify monthly listeners rather than actual chemistry. It's not all bad, but it does create a kind of sameness. A flattening.

Open Decks is the antidote. The format, sign up in person, 12 minutes to make your case, random selection after the first three slots, keeps things unpredictable. You might hear a first-time DJ playing their only finished track, nervous hands on the faders. You might hear a seasoned local testing something that won't see release for months. You might hear Steen himself, stepping up to show how it's done after hours of hosting.

Tonight's co-host MyCube adds another dimension. The project, equal parts DJ and live act, brings a hardware-focused approach that contrasts nicely with Steen's more traditional deck work. Expect modular bleeps, drum machine workouts, maybe some live processing of Steen's selections if the chemistry's right. These back-to-backs have a way of pushing both artists out of their comfort zones.

## The bigger picture

There's a narrative that gets pushed about NYC techno right now. That the scene is booming, that we're experiencing a golden age, that the rest of the world is finally paying attention. And sure, there's truth to that. But the boom has a way of obscuring the foundation. The locals who kept rooms alive during the lean years. The residents who learned their craft in half-empty warehouses. The promoters who lost money for years because they believed in something.

Steen is part of that foundation. Open Decks is part of that foundation. SILO itself, still independent, still focused, still committed to its weekly programming even when the big-ticket weekends could probably pay the bills alone, is part of that foundation.

Tonight won't make headlines. It won't break your Instagram feed. But if you're looking for the real thing, the unvarnished, un-hyped, actually-underground version of NYC techno, this is where you'll find it.

## Practical notes

Sign-up is in person only, starting at 8pm. First three slots are first-come-first-spin, then random selection. Each DJ gets 12 minutes, including any mic time for producers explaining their tracks. Cover is free with advance RSVP, check SILO's site. Arrive early if you want to play, by 10pm if you want to catch the best energy.

SILO is at 90 Scott Ave in East Williamsburg. Take the L train to Jefferson, then walk or grab a Lyft. The block's still industrial enough that you'll want company after midnight.

## The verdict

Should you go? If you're a working DJ looking for a room to test material, absolutely. This is one of the best systems in the city and a crowd that actually listens. If you're a dancer looking for something rawer than the polished club experience, yes. If you're a tourist checking boxes on a "Brooklyn nightlife" list, maybe sit this one out. The room doesn't need your content.

Rating: 4/5. Not a can't-miss spectacle, but exactly the kind of night that keeps the scene healthy. Steen's a reliable selector, MyCube brings live hardware energy, and the Open Decks format guarantees at least one moment you'll remember.
