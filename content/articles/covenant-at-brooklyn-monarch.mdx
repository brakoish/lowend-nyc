# Covenant at Brooklyn Monarch: Swedish Synth-Pop Royalty Descends on Williamsburg

There are bands you discover, and then there are bands that *discover you*. Covenant falls firmly in the latter camp. If you've spent any time in darkened warehouses, sweating through mesh shirts at 3 AM while a strobe light tries to give you a seizure, you've already met them—even if you didn't know their name.

Now the Swedish titans are bringing their three-decade legacy to **Brooklyn Monarch**, and I'm already clearing my calendar.

## Who They Are (For the Uninitiated)

Formed in 1986 in Helsingborg, Sweden, Covenant emerged from the same Nordic frost that gave us The Knife and The Knife's entire aesthetic bloodline. But while their countrymen went pop, Covenant went *deeper*—forging a sound that sits somewhere between EBM's industrial aggression, synth-pop's melodic sensibility, and whatever dark magic happens when you lock yourself in a studio with a modular rig and existential dread.

Their breakthrough came with 1996's *Dreams of Cryotank*, but it was 1998's *Europa* that cemented their status as synth-pop royalty. "Figurehead" still hits like a freight train of pure melancholy. 2000's *United States of Mind* gave us "Dead Stars"—a track that somehow sounds like both a nightclub anthem and a funeral dirge, which is exactly the duality that makes Covenant special.

Three decades in, they're still releasing vital music. Their 2023 album *The Blinding Dark* proved they haven't lost a step, and their live shows have only grown more refined, more *theatrical*.

## The Venue: Brooklyn Monarch

If you've never been to **Brooklyn Monarch**, let me paint you a picture. It's housed in a converted industrial space on the Williamsburg waterfront—exposed brick, soaring ceilings, a sound system that doesn't just play music but *occupies* space. The kind of venue where the concrete floor vibrates in sympathy with the kick drum.

It's become one of Brooklyn's premier spots for electronic and industrial acts precisely because it understands the assignment. This isn't a polished Manhattan club with bottle service and dress codes. This is a *temple*—a place where the music is allowed to be loud, dark, and unapologetically intense.

For a band like Covenant, whose aesthetic has always leaned into the industrial and the monumental, it's a perfect marriage. The venue's raw architecture will complement their synthetic grandeur. The lighting rigs will have room to breathe. The sound will have space to expand and engulf you.

## Why This Matters

Look, New York gets a lot of electronic acts. We get the techno tourists, the EDM festival refugees, the DJ-producers who press play on a USB stick and call it a performance. Covenant is different. Covenant is a *band*—three musicians who actually play their instruments, who craft songs with verses and choruses and emotional arcs.

Eskil Simonsson's vocals are a masterclass in controlled intensity. He doesn't scream; he *inhabits* the songs. Joakim Montelius's synth work creates these vast, cinematic soundscapes that feel like standing at the edge of something ancient and unknowable. And live, they bring a physicality to electronic music that most acts can't touch.

In a city that's increasingly dominated by algorithmic playlists and TikTok-optimized earworms, a Covenant show is a reminder of what electronic music can be when it's made by humans with something to say.

## The Setlist Question

Will they lean into the classics? "Dead Stars," "Figurehead," "Call the Ships to Port"—these are songs that have soundtracked countless late nights and broken hearts. Or will they challenge us with deeper cuts from *The Blinding Dark* and *Leaving Babylon*?

My bet: they'll thread the needle. Covenant understands their legacy. They know why we're there. But they're also artists who refuse to become a nostalgia act, and that tension—between honoring the past and pushing forward—is what keeps them vital.

## The Bottom Line

Covenant at Brooklyn Monarch isn't just a concert. It's a *convergence*—of Swedish darkness and Brooklyn grit, of three decades of electronic innovation and one perfect venue. If you've ever felt the pull of the synthesizer's cold embrace, if you've ever danced with your eyes closed in a room full of strangers who understand something the daylight world doesn't, this is your night.

The industrial cathedral awaits. Don't miss it.
