# Basement Turns Five: What Knockdown Center Built Underground

I still remember the first time I walked down that long concrete tunnel. May 2019. Word had been spreading through Brooklyn's techno circles about something brewing underneath the Knockdown Center in Maspeth. Another club promising to be "New York's Berghain." Another venue claiming to understand what the city actually needed. I'd heard it all before.

But then I descended those stairs. The heavy metal doors. Brick walls standing for over a century. A Funktion-One system being tuned in the corner. I knew immediately — this was different.

Five years later, Basement hasn't just survived. It's become the anchor of New York's underground. In a city where clubs open and close with the seasons, where bottle service and influencer culture have infected nightlife like a virus, Basement has stayed stubbornly, beautifully committed to one thing: the music.

## The Space: Industrial Cathedral

The physical space matters. Basement occupies what was once part of a glass and steel-frame door factory — the same industrial complex that gives Knockdown Center its name. The building dates back to 1903. You feel every one of those years when you're down there.

The main room holds about 500 people. Sweet spot. Big enough to feel like an event, small enough to feel intimate. Concrete pillars rise from the floor like ancient sentinels. Old factory doors frame the DJ booth. The brick walls are hand-laid, weathered, left raw. There's a warren of nooks and narrow alleyways that create this disorienting, labyrinthine quality — you can lose yourself in the space before you lose yourself in the music.

The lighting is minimal by design. Flickering strobes. Fog machines. That's it. No LED walls, no elaborate productions, no distractions. The dance floor is essentially the size of a large living room surrounded by concrete pillars and arched brick windows.

That Funktion-One system, custom-tuned for the room, delivers the kind of all-encompassing, physical sound you feel in your chest before you hear it in your ears. Clean. Powerful. Exactly what techno demands.

## The Door Policy: Know Your Headliner

Here's where Basement made its name — and where it still courts controversy. When Tea Abashidze and GeGa Japaridze opened the club, they made one thing clear: if you don't know who's playing, you might not get in.

"You have to do your homework," Japaridze told PAPER Magazine in 2019. "We won't be declining people because you're wearing red or purple instead of black, but if you don't know who is playing at all..."

The no-photo policy is just as strict. Masking tape over phone cameras. Staff who will actually stop you if they see that screen light up. In an era where every moment is content, where clubs are designed to be Instagram backdrops, Basement's refusal to participate feels almost radical.

Is it pretentious? Sometimes. Have I seen people turned away for not knowing the lineup? Absolutely. Does it create a better dance floor? Without question.

The people who make it through that door are there for the music. Not to be seen. Not for the 'gram. They care about who's behind the decks. That creates an energy you can't fake.

## Five Years of Notable Moments

Basement opened in May 2019. It barely had a year of normal operations before the pandemic shut everything down. But that 2019 run established the template: international heavyweights mixed with local talent, techno-focused but with room for the broader electronic spectrum.

The early bookings set the tone — European avant-gardists who might play EBM, industrial techno, Italo house. DJs who treat the booth like a laboratory, not a stage. Terence Fixmer, Volvox, Amanda Mussi. The queer B.D.S.M. party Bound: Blackout found a home there, creating space for fetish gear and alternative culture that felt genuinely welcoming rather than performative.

Post-pandemic, Basement came back stronger. The Studio — a second, more intimate room with a housier sound — opened in late 2021, giving the venue more programming flexibility. While the main room keeps its techno focus, The Studio became a space for rising DJs, underground collectives, and house music that doesn't quite fit the main room's harder edge.

The lineups have only gotten heavier. Jeff Mills has played there. Paco Osuna. Richy Ahmed. DJ Tennis. The club has become a regular stop for international touring acts who might otherwise skip New York entirely, and a proving ground for local talent who need a proper system to show what they can do.

## The Knockdown Center Connection

Basement doesn't exist in a vacuum. It's part of the larger Knockdown Center ecosystem — a 50,000-square-foot arts venue that's been one of Queens' most vital cultural spaces since 2012. The relationship matters.

Tyler Myers, Knockdown Center's director, spent two years building community trust before Basement could open. The neighborhood was wary — an industrial stretch of Maspeth isn't exactly accustomed to all-night techno raves. But KDC had already established itself through art exhibitions, book fairs, community events. By the time Basement launched, the venue had earned the political and social capital to make it work.

That infrastructure supports Basement in ways pure nightlife venues can't match. Relationships with local authorities. Understanding how to manage large crowds in an industrial space. The network of artists, performers, and cultural workers who flow between different rooms and events.

Basement feeds back into that ecosystem too. The club brings a different audience to Knockdown Center — younger, more international, more focused on electronic music. It diversifies the venue's programming and helps establish KDC as a genuine multi-disciplinary space, not just an art venue that sometimes throws parties.

## What Makes Basement Unique

In a city with no shortage of clubs, what makes Basement special?

**The location.** Maspeth is not convenient. It's not the Lower East Side. It's not even Bushwick. You have to want to go there, and that self-selection creates a different crowd. The industrial surroundings — warehouses, auto shops, the BQE humming in the distance — set a tone before you walk through the door.

**The aesthetic is uncompromising.** No bottle service. No tables. No VIP sections. The bar is basic — drinks in cans or plastic cups, strong pours, reasonable prices. The food truck outside serves Philly cheesesteaks and hoagies to exiting patrons at 5 AM. It's functional, not luxurious, and that functionality keeps the focus where it belongs.

**The programming has stayed consistent.** While other venues chase trends — hyperpop one month, amapiano the next — Basement has stayed committed to techno and its adjacent genres. That consistency builds trust. You know what you're getting when you go to Basement.

**The door policy, for all its controversy, works.** The dance floor is better for it. The crowd is more engaged. The energy is more focused. It's not for everyone. That's the point.

## Five Years In: The State of the Underground

Basement opened claiming to be New York's first club specifically dedicated to techno. That claim was debatable then — Output was still around, and plenty of venues were programming techno regularly. Five years later, the claim feels more accurate than ever.

Output closed. Other venues pivoted to more commercial sounds or shut down entirely. The DIY spaces that sustained underground electronic music for years have been systematically killed by rising rents and regulatory pressure. What's left is a landscape where legally sanctioned venues dominate, and within that landscape, Basement has become the anchor.

It's not perfect. The initial lineup controversy — the too-white, too-male first announcement — was a real stumble, one the venue had to publicly address. The door policy can feel exclusionary in ways that aren't always about musical knowledge. The Berghain comparisons, invited by the venue's own aesthetic choices, set expectations that are hard to meet.

But the club has evolved. Expanded lineups corrected early diversity issues. The Studio opened to create more entry points for different sounds. The programming has stayed adventurous while remaining true to the core mission.

## Looking Forward

At five years old, Basement is no longer the new thing. It's an institution. That's both its challenge and its opportunity.

Institutions can become complacent. They can coast on reputation rather than pushing forward. The danger for Basement is that it becomes so associated with a specific moment in New York nightlife — the post-Output, post-DIY, legally-sanctioned underground — that it stops evolving as the scene changes.

But the opportunity is bigger. In a city where cultural spaces are increasingly precarious, where every venue feels like it could become a condo development next year, Basement has established something durable. It's proven there's an audience for uncompromising techno in New York. It's proven a door policy focused on musical knowledge can work. It's proven you don't need bottle service to make the numbers work.

The next five years will tell us whether Basement can build on that foundation. Whether it can keep discovering and developing local talent. Whether it can maintain its identity as the scene around it shifts. Whether it can avoid the pitfalls that have claimed so many other venues.

I'm optimistic. Every time I walk down that concrete tunnel, past those heavy metal doors, into that subterranean room with its century-old brick walls and perfect sound system, I'm reminded why this matters. New York needs spaces like Basement. Not just for the music — though the music is essential. But for the proof that another kind of nightlife is possible. One that values substance over style, community over clout, the shared experience of a dark room and a loud system over everything else.

Five years in, Basement has earned its place. Here's to the next five.
