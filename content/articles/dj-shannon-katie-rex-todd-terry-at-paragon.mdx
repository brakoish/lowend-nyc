# DJ Shannon, Katie Rex, Todd Terry at Paragon: A Triple Threat of Techno, Queer Rage, and House History

**Lower East Side — March 2026**

There are club nights that feel like a party, and then there are club nights that feel like a statement. When Paragon announced this lineup, DJ Shannon, Katie Rex, and Todd Terry sharing a bill, I knew immediately which category we were in.

This is the kind of stacked booking that makes you stop scrolling. Three artists who couldn't be more different on paper, united by the fact that they all absolutely destroy dancefloors. A Texas techno assassin, a Philadelphia-bred queer firebrand, and a Bronx house legend who helped invent the sound we're all still chasing. If you can't get excited about this bill, I genuinely don't know what to tell you.

## The venue

Paragon sits on the Lower East Side in that sweet spot where the neighborhood's grit hasn't been completely polished away. It's not trying to be Brooklyn. It's not trying to be the next Output or the next Basement. It's just a proper room with a proper soundsystem and a crowd that shows up ready to work.

The LES has always been where New York's nightlife goes to get real. While Manhattan clubs chase bottle service clients and Brooklyn warehouses chase the techno tourists, the Lower East Side has maintained this stubborn, unglamorous commitment to actual dancing. Paragon fits right into that lineage. The space is intimate enough that you can feel the bass in your chest, big enough that you can lose yourself in the crowd. No gimmicks. No theme nights. Just music, properly loud, properly late.

I've had some of my best nights at Paragon precisely because it doesn't try too hard. The door staff isn't performing exclusivity. The drinks aren't priced like rent. The sound system punches above its weight class. When a promoter brings real talent through those doors, the room responds. And this lineup? This is real talent.

## DJ Shannon: Texas techno, unfiltered

Let's start with DJ Shannon because she's the artist on this bill who most purely represents where techno is right now, at least the kind of techno that actually moves bodies instead of just impressing other DJs.

Coming out of Texas, Shannon's sound is exactly what you'd hope from that pedigree: hard, unrelenting, and completely devoid of pretension. She's not making techno for the Berghain crowd. She's making techno for people who want to sweat, who want to feel something physical, who don't need their dance music to come with a dissertation attached.

Her breakthrough came through her association with the Night Slugs and Fade to Mind extended universe, though she's always maintained a distinct voice separate from those London-centric sounds. What Shannon understood early, and what separates her from the pack of hard techno producers that have flooded the market in recent years, is that aggression alone isn't enough. You need groove. You need space. You need those moments where the kick drops out and the synths breathe before everything crashes back in.

Her DJ sets are relentless in the best way. She plays fast, she plays hard, but she never loses the thread. There's a narrative quality to her selections, a sense that each track is answering the one before it, building toward something. I've seen her clear rooms and then rebuild them from scratch, that confidence that only comes from years of playing tough rooms and learning what actually works.

What I respect most about Shannon is that she's stayed true to her sound even as techno has blown up globally. She could have softened her edges, chased the melodic techno dollar, tried to fit into the more palatable boxes that open up when you start getting festival bookings. Instead, she's doubled down on the hard stuff. The industrial-tinged kicks, the distorted synths, the tracks that feel like they're trying to break the soundsystem. That's the Shannon you're getting at Paragon, and thank god for that.

## Katie Rex: queer techno, loud and proud

If DJ Shannon represents techno's present, Katie Rex represents its future, or at least one possible future that I'm deeply invested in seeing realized.

Katie Rex is a DJ, producer, and founder of the queer collective and label Club Carry. She's based in New York now but came up in Philadelphia, and you can hear that Philly warehouse DNA in everything she does. This isn't polite, chin-stroking techno for people who want to discuss subgenres. This is techno as liberation, as community, as a space where queer bodies can take up room and demand to be seen.

Her sound sits somewhere between hard techno, EBM, and the kind of industrial-tinged dance music that's been bubbling up from queer undergrounds for years. She plays fast, often pushing 140 BPM and beyond, but there's always a musicality to it. The synths scream. The drums pound. The vocals, when they appear, are distorted, processed, made strange. It's music that refuses to be background noise.

What makes Katie Rex essential isn't just her DJ skills, though those are considerable. It's her vision for what dance music can be. Club Carry isn't just a party series or a label. It's a statement of intent, a commitment to centering queer and trans artists in a scene that's historically been hostile to them. When Katie plays, she's not just playing tracks. She's building a world, track by track, where people like her get to be the main characters.

I've seen her sets go completely off the rails in the best way. The crowd losing their minds, the energy building to these fever pitches where it feels like the room might actually lift off. She has that rare ability to make a club feel like a sanctuary and a battleground at the same time. This is music for dancing and for surviving, for joy and for rage, for the moments when you need to feel completely, overwhelmingly alive.

At Paragon, with this crowd, on this bill? I expect something special. Katie Rex has been on a tear lately, and playing alongside artists like Shannon and Terry is going to push her to bring her absolute best. Don't sleep on this set.

## Todd Terry: the legend, still teaching

And then there's Todd Terry.

If you don't know why Todd Terry matters, we need to have a longer conversation than I have space for here. But the short version: this man is house music royalty, a true pioneer who helped define the sound of New York dance music in the late 80s and early 90s and whose influence still echoes through every kick drum and chopped vocal you hear today.

Todd Terry is the Bronx-born producer and DJ who took the raw materials of Chicago house, hip-hop's breakbeat culture, and the burgeoning New York club scene and forged something entirely new. His "Todd Terry Project" releases, his work as Royal House, his remixes for everyone from Björk to Michael Jackson to The Rolling Stones, these are the foundations that everything else is built on. When people talk about "New York house," they're talking, in large part, about what Todd Terry invented.

The "Todd Terry sound" is instantly recognizable: those chopped-up vocal samples, the hard-hitting drum programming, the way he could take a soulful house groove and make it hit like hip-hop. Tracks like "Can You Party," "Weekend," and "Bango (To the Batmobile)" aren't just classics. They're blueprints. You can draw a straight line from Todd Terry's sampler to the entire UK hardcore and jungle scenes, to modern bass music, to the way contemporary producers still approach vocal manipulation.

But here's what matters for this Paragon date: Todd Terry isn't a museum piece. He's still out here, week after week, playing clubs and festivals around the world, showing the younger generation how it's done. And when I say "showing them how it's done," I mean it literally. A Todd Terry set in 2026 is a masterclass in selection, in reading a room, in understanding that the DJ's job is to make people dance, full stop.

He'll play the classics, of course. You don't go see Todd Terry and not hear "Can You Party" or "Jumpin'." But he'll also play records that came out last month, last week, digging deep into the current house landscape and finding the tracks that deserve to be heard. His ear is still sharp. His taste is still impeccable. And his technical skills, the way he mixes, the way he builds energy, the way he knows exactly when to drop the next record, these are things you can't fake and can't teach. You can only learn them by doing what Todd Terry has been doing for nearly four decades.

Seeing him on a bill with DJ Shannon and Katie Rex feels significant. It's a passing of the torch, or maybe a recognition that the torch never needed to be passed because Todd Terry is still holding it, still lighting the way. When he steps up at Paragon, he's not just playing a set. He's representing the entire history of this music, the New York tradition that made nights like this possible in the first place.

## Why this bill works

On paper, these three artists shouldn't work together. DJ Shannon's hard techno, Katie Rex's queer industrialism, and Todd Terry's classic house seem like different worlds. Different BPMs, different aesthetics, different crowds.

But that's exactly why this booking is genius. Paragon isn't trying to give you a seamless, homogeneous night where every DJ sounds like the last one. They're giving you a journey. They're trusting the audience to be sophisticated enough to handle variety, to appreciate contrast, to understand that the best nights are the ones that take you places you didn't expect to go.

Start with Todd Terry laying down the foundation, those classic house grooves that remind you where this all came from. Then Katie Rex takes over and blows the roof off, pushing the energy into raw, queer, unapologetic territory. And DJ Shannon brings it home with techno that doesn't let up, that demands everything you have left and then asks for more.

Or maybe they go in a different order. Maybe Todd Terry closes, showing the young guns how a legend commands a room. Maybe Katie Rex goes on first and sets the tone, declares the territory, makes it clear that this is a space where queer voices lead. The beauty of a bill this strong is that any configuration works. You're getting three distinct visions, three different approaches to making people move, and the tension between them is going to make each set hit harder.

## The prediction

I'm expecting a long night and a sore neck the next morning. I'm expecting to hear records I've never heard before and classics I can't believe I'm hearing on a soundsystem this good. I'm expecting to see a crowd that knows exactly what they're getting into, that shows up ready to work, that understands how rare it is to get three artists this compelling in a room this intimate.

Paragon doesn't do this kind of bill often. When they do, it's because they've found something special. DJ Shannon, Katie Rex, and Todd Terry together isn't just a good night out. It's a statement about what dance music can be when you stop worrying about genre boundaries and start focusing on what actually matters: the music, the community, the collective experience of losing yourself on a dancefloor.

New York has been through a lot with its nightlife. We've lost venues, lost scenes, lost whole eras of this city's dance music history. But nights like this remind me that it's not over. The spirit that made New York the birthplace of house music, that made this city essential to the global dance music conversation, that spirit is still here. You just have to know where to look.

Look at Paragon. Look at this bill. Show up early, stay late, and prepare to have your expectations exceeded.

**Rating: 5/5** — Essential. Three artists at the top of their respective games, in a room that knows how to host them properly. This is why we go out.
