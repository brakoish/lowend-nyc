# Friction at The Meadows Brooklyn: DnB Royalty Descends on Bushwick

**Bushwick — March 2026**

There's a particular frustration that comes with loving drum & bass in New York City. You'll be somewhere else, London or Bristol or even a random Tuesday in Berlin, and the energy in the room will shift. The tempo will jump to 174 BPM, the breakbeats will start chopping, the bass will drop an octave and suddenly everyone in the room is moving with a synchronized urgency that you just don't see at house or techno tempos. And you'll think: why can't we have this at home?

We can. Sometimes. And when Friction touches down at The Meadows this month, we're getting one of those rare nights where the UK's finest export comes to us with their A-game.

## Who Friction Actually Is

Ed Keeley has been Friction for over two decades now, and if you know drum & bass, you already know why that matters. This isn't some journeyman DJ riding the circuit. This is a foundational artist, someone whose fingerprints are all over the genre's evolution from the late 90s to right now.

He started as a teenager in Brighton, cutting his teeth on the local rave scene when drum & bass was still figuring out what it wanted to be. By the early 2000s, he was producing tracks that mattered: "Light Speed," "Believe," "Jazzfreak," records that balanced the genre's darker neurofunk impulses with something more musical, more accessible, without ever sacrificing the dancefloor impact.

But the real story is Shogun Audio. Friction founded the label in 2004, and over the next two decades, he turned it into one of the most respected imprints in drum & bass. We're talking about the launchpad for Rockwell, Icicle, SpectraSoul, Technimatic — artists who defined the sound of 2010s DnB. Shogun wasn't just releasing records; it was curating a vision of what drum & bass could be. Technical, forward-thinking, unafraid of melody but never cheesy, rooted in the underground but reaching for something bigger.

Friction's own productions followed that arc. His 2012 album *Led Astray* remains a high-water mark for vocal DnB, tracks like "Someone" featuring Arlissa and "Long Gone Memory" with Banks showing that the genre could do emotional depth without losing its edge. His remix work — for Chase & Status, for London Grammar, for everyone in between — demonstrated a versatility that kept him relevant even as the scene splintered into a thousand subgenres.

And then there's the radio show. Friction's BBC Radio 1 residency, which ran for years, was essential listening for anyone trying to keep up with DnB. He used that platform the right way: breaking new records, championing underground producers, connecting the dots between the genre's history and its future. When Radio 1 started cutting back on specialist shows, losing Friction felt like losing a vital artery for the scene.

## The Sound: What You're Actually Getting

If you've never seen Friction DJ, here's what to expect. He's a technical DJ in an era where that's increasingly rare. Three decks, sometimes four. Rapid-fire mixing that never feels rushed. The ability to drop from liquid funk sweetness into neurofunk aggression without losing the thread. A crate that spans decades but never feels like a history lesson.

Friction's sets typically move through the full spectrum of drum & bass. You'll get the liquid moments — soulful vocals, warm pads, that particular melancholy that the genre does better than anything else. You'll get the jump-up sections, the party-starting anthems that get hands in the air. You'll get the techy, stripped-back rollers that work the floor with precision rather than force. And you'll get the neurofunk, the dark, complex stuff that sounds like the future even when it's been around for years.

What ties it together is selection. Friction has been digging and playing this music for twenty-plus years. He knows what works, what doesn't, and crucially, he knows when. His timing is impeccable. The drops land when they should. The breakdowns breathe. The energy builds and releases in waves that feel inevitable even when they're surprising you.

## The Venue: The Meadows

Let's talk about where this is happening, because The Meadows matters. Bushwick has become the default home for bass music in NYC, and The Meadows is one of the reasons why. It's not the biggest room — capacity somewhere in the mid-hundreds — but it's become the most reliable spot for drum & bass, dubstep, and the various UK-derived sounds that struggle to find consistent homes elsewhere in the city.

The sound system is the key. The Meadows invested early in proper bass weight, and it shows. This is a room built for low-end, for the physical impact that these genres demand. The layout helps too: a long, narrow space that keeps the crowd close to the DJ, that creates intimacy even when it's packed, that channels energy toward the floor rather than dispersing it.

I've seen DnB nights struggle in bigger venues. There's something about the genre that works better when the room is full but not overwhelming, when you can see the DJ's hands on the decks, when the bass hits your chest directly rather than diffusing through thousands of bodies. The Meadows gets this. They've been booking consistent DnB for years now, building a crowd that knows what they're there for.

Bushwick itself matters too. This isn't Manhattan clubland or the bottle-service districts of Williamsburg. It's a neighborhood that still feels like Brooklyn, that hasn't fully surrendered to the forces of gentrification and homogenization. The crowd that comes to The Meadows is mixed — expat Brits who miss the London scene, American converts who discovered DnB through gaming soundtracks or YouTube algorithms, old-school ravers who've been following this music since the 90s. It's one of the few places in the city where drum & bass feels like a community rather than a curiosity.

## Why This Show Matters

Drum & bass has always had a complicated relationship with America. The genre was born in the UK, refined in London and Bristol, and despite decades of effort, it's never quite broken through to mainstream American consciousness the way house or techno has. You'll hear DnB influences in pop production, in movie trailers, in video games, but the actual scene remains stubbornly underground, concentrated in pockets of true believers.

NYC's DnB history is particularly spotty. We had moments — the Direct Drive parties of the early 2000s, various one-offs and touring acts over the years — but consistent infrastructure has been hard to maintain. Venues close. Promoters move on. The crowd that exists is passionate but fragmented.

That's why nights like this feel important. When someone of Friction's stature comes through, it's not just a gig. It's validation. It's a reminder that the genre has history, has weight, has artists who've dedicated their lives to this sound and who can still command a room anywhere in the world. It's a rallying point for a community that doesn't always get to feel like a community.

Friction specifically matters because he bridges eras. He's not a legacy act playing old hits for nostalgia's sake. He's still releasing music, still running Shogun (though in a different form than the peak years), still engaged with where the genre is going. His sets connect the dots between the classic records that defined DnB and the new productions that are pushing it forward. For a scene that sometimes feels trapped between reverence for the past and anxiety about the future, that synthesis is valuable.

## The Prediction

I'm expecting a masterclass. Friction doesn't play New York often, and when he does, he brings his full arsenal. The technical skill will be on display — the multi-deck mixing, the precise EQ work, the ability to weave between subgenres without jarring transitions. But more than that, I'm expecting the intangible thing that separates good DJs from great ones: the sense that someone in the room actually cares about where this night is going.

The Meadows will be packed. It always is for DnB nights, and Friction is the biggest name they've booked in a while. The sound system will be pushed to its limits. The crowd will be knowledgeable, responsive, ready for whatever he throws at them. And somewhere around 2 AM, probably during a particularly devastating drop or a perfectly timed vocal, there will be one of those moments where the whole room feels like a single thing. That's why we go out. That's why we put up with the inconsistent infrastructure and the late nights and the fact that our favorite genre will never get the mainstream recognition it deserves.

Because when it works — when the DJ is right, the room is right, the sound is right — drum & bass delivers something no other genre can. A particular combination of speed, weight, and musicality that creates genuine euphoria. Friction has been creating those moments for twenty years. He's very good at it.

If you're even slightly curious about drum & bass, this is the night to show up. If you're already a convert, you don't need me to tell you. See you in the dance.

**Rating: 4.5/5** — Essential for DnB heads, highly recommended for the DnB-curious. One of the genre's foundational artists in a room that understands what he's bringing.
