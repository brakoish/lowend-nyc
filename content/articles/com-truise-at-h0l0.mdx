# Com Truise at H0L0: The synthwave guy who actually matters

**Ridgewood, Queens — April 3, 2026**

Most synthwave sucks. Let's just say it. For every producer doing something interesting with 80s textures, there's fifty more cranking out the same neon-grid album art and arpeggiated basslines that sound like they were generated by an AI trained on *Drive* fan fiction. It's a genre that collapsed under its own nostalgia almost as fast as it emerged.

Com Truise is the exception. Seth Haley has been at this since 2010, and he's still finding new wrinkles in a sound that should've gone stale years ago. When he plays H0L0 on April 3rd, it'll be a reminder of why Ghostly International bet on him in the first place—and why, fourteen years later, people still care.

## From art director to full-time synth obsessive

Before Com Truise, Haley was designing packaging and promo materials for other people's music. He made tracks at night under names like Sarin Sunday and Airliner—side projects that hinted at where he was heading but never quite got there. In 2010 he quit the day job, dropped the *Cyanide Sisters* EP, and never looked back.

The name is a spoonerism of Tom Cruise, which tells you most of what you need to know about his relationship to 80s culture: half sincere love, half ironic distance. Haley calls his sound "mid-fi synthwave slow-motion funk," and yeah, that's accurate. Thick warbling bass, arpeggios that spiral upward like smoke, drums that hit with a deliberate, almost sluggish precision. This isn't gym music. It's 2 AM driving music, music for when you want to feel like you're inside a movie montage even though you're just taking the BQE home.

The Ghostly International association matters. That label has a specific aesthetic—Tycho, Matthew Dear, artists who treat electronic music as a whole experience where the visual identity carries as much weight as the kick drum. Haley fit right in. But what separated him from the pack was the narrative ambition. Every Com Truise album from *Cyanide Sisters* through 2017's *Iteration* follows the same character: a "synthetic astronaut" fleeing some oppressed society on a distant world. Concept albums for instrumental electronic music shouldn't work, but Haley commits hard enough that it does. You feel like there's a plot even when nobody's singing.

## What to expect from this show

Haley's billed for a DJ set, not a live PA. Some fans will be disappointed by that. I get it—you want to see the hardware, the OB-6 he added to his setup in 2017, the actual machines making the sounds. But DJ sets have their own appeal. Haley's tend to roam wider than his productions, touching on the 80s synth-pop, Italo disco, and early Detroit techno that shaped his sound in the first place. You'll hear influences that don't show up on his albums, tracks that explain where Com Truise came from.

If you're unfamiliar, start with "VHS Sex" or "Brokendate" from 2011's *Galactic Melt*. Those tracks established the template. *In Decay* (2012) collected unreleased material and deep cuts. *Iteration* (2017) was supposed to be the end of the synthetic astronaut story—a clean conclusion that felt final. Then *Persuasion System* arrived in 2019, looser and less conceptually burdened. The through-line across all of it is Haley's ear for texture. He knows how to make digital synths sound warm, how to make analog gear sound precise, how to layer pads until they create a sense of vast space.

## Why H0L0 makes sense

H0L0 has been holding down the Ridgewood/Bushwick corridor since 2017. 800-capacity warehouse on Wyckoff, no VIP section, no bottle service, no LED wall trying to distract you from the actual music. Just proximity: to the artist, to the sound system, to the other people who bothered to take the L train past Myrtle-Wyckoff for something specific.

I've heard everything from noise experiments to rolling techno in that room. The system punches above its weight—clear without being harsh, which matters for music as texturally dense as Haley's. You want to hear the details. The way a filter opens over sixteen bars. The pitch drift on an oscillator. The layers that create the atmosphere.

The crowd at H0L0 knows what they're getting into. This isn't a venue that draws the bridge-and-tunnel bottle-service crowd. The people who make the trek to Ridgewood are there for the music, willing to engage with something unfamiliar, ready to be transported. They're the right audience for this.

## The context

Synthwave had a weird trajectory. It started as a genuine underground thing in the late 2000s—producers rediscovering the emotional possibilities of 80s synth sounds. The *Drive* soundtrack blew it up in 2011. Subgenres splintered off: darksynth, outrun, vaporwave. For a minute it was everywhere. Then the backlash hit, and suddenly "synthwave" was almost embarrassing, shorthand for lazy nostalgia and algorithmic playlist filler.

Haley survived that backlash because he was never just a synthwave producer. His music has too much personality, too much craft, too much of a point of view to be dismissed as retro fetishism. The Ghostly association helped position him as an artist first, genre practitioner second. But mostly the music just holds up. Put on *Galactic Melt* today and it still sounds like something only Seth Haley could've made.

He's been relatively quiet on releases lately, though you might've heard his work without realizing it—he produced the music for Coinbase's Super Bowl LVI ad in 2022, sampling the Beatles' cover of "Money (That's What I Want)." That's the kind of high-profile sync that introduces your sound to millions of people who'll never know your name. He's been touring consistently, hitting festivals and clubs across the US and Europe. There's a sense that something new might be brewing, that this quiet period could be leading somewhere.

## Bottom line

If you're on the fence: go. Expect mid-tempo territory, 110-120 BPM, that slow-motion funk zone where Haley's most comfortable. Expect moments of pure dancefloor utility and moments of pure atmosphere. Expect to hear music that treats the 80s as a genuine source of inspiration, not a joke or a marketing gimmick—a period when synthesizers were new and full of possibility, when the future seemed bright and strange and a little dangerous.

That's the world Com Truise invites you into. It's worth visiting.

**Go if:** You like synthwave that doesn't suck, you respect the Ghostly International catalog, or you want to hear electronic music that prioritizes atmosphere and narrative over immediate gratification.

**Skip if:** You need 140 BPM and a drop every sixteen bars. This isn't that.
