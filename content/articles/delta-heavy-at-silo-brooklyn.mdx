# Delta Heavy at Silo Brooklyn

**East Williamsburg — March 28, 2026**

I've been to enough shows at Silo to know the exact moment the Danleys wake up. It's not gradual. One second you're chatting with someone about their terrible day job, next second your chest cavity is getting a massage from a frequency you didn't know existed. That's the Silo experience. And on March 28th, Delta Heavy are going to absolutely punish that soundsystem.

Ben Hall and Simon James have been at this since 2009. Think about that — fifteen years of making drum & bass in a scene where most producers flame out after two albums and a bad drug habit. They've survived dubstep's commercial peak, the EDM bubble, the pandemic, and whatever the hell we're calling the current era of algorithm-driven playlist music. They're still here. That means something.

## The Track That Refuses to Die

You know "Get By." Everyone knows "Get By." Released in 2012, that track did what almost nothing in DnB manages to do — it escaped the genre's gravity well without turning into embarrassing crossover trash. It peaked at number 37 on the UK Dance Chart, which sounds like nothing until you remember how thoroughly it colonized every bass music playlist, festival set, and YouTube recommendation algorithm for the next decade.

The Gorgon City remix got radio play. The original got rinsed at Glastonbury, EDC, Boiler Room, and every warehouse from London to Los Angeles. And that animated video — the one with the Clippit cameo and the retro gaming aesthetic — is sitting on UKF with millions of views, still getting comments from kids who weren't even born when it dropped.

Here's what impresses me: they didn't spend the next ten years remaking it. So many artists get one hit and immediately become their own cover band, chasing that same formula until the crowd gets bored and moves on. Hall and James went the opposite direction. Their debut album *Paradise Lost* (2016) sprawled across DnB, dubstep, and all the weird hybrid territory in between. "Ghost" and "Punish My Love" proved they could do emotional without doing cheesy. That's a harder line to walk than most people realize.

*Only in Dreams* (2019) pushed even further into melody while keeping that signature Delta Heavy weight. By then they'd figured out the formula: anthemic but not corny, heavy but not monotonous, technical but not sterile. Easy to describe. Hard as hell to actually pull off.

Their remix discography tells its own story. Rita Ora, Avicii, DJ Fresh, Tinie Tempah — they've taken pop records and bent them into shapes the original artists never imagined. The difference between producers who understand songcraft and producers who just know which Serum preset to load.

## What You're Actually Getting

Delta Heavy in the studio: meticulous, layered, orchestral, headphone music. Delta Heavy in the booth: absolutely merciless.

Their sets move through the whole spectrum. Liquid moments where the crowd sways instead of moshes. Jump-up sections where the energy peaks and hands go up. Dubstep detours that remind you these guys came up in the UK bass scene, not just the DnB bubble. Neurofunk passages where the production gets technical and you see the heads in the front row nodding with that specific appreciation of complexity.

The dynamics are what matter. They'll give you just enough calm to miss the intensity, then drop something that rearranges your internal organs. The transitions are seamless because these guys actually know how to DJ, not just trigger a playlist.

And the sound design — Jesus. Delta Heavy's bass tones are enormous, physical, like they occupy actual space in the room. When those sounds hit Silo's Danleys at proper volume, it's not hearing anymore. It's feeling. The air pressure changes. Your clothes vibrate. You become aware of your skeleton.

## Why Silo Matters

Silo sits at 90 Scott Avenue in East Williamsburg, tucked into that industrial corridor that's become the last real warehouse district in Brooklyn. This isn't a club. It's a space that earned its license through years of underground operation, that still carries DNA from the Secret Loft parties that birthed it in 2012.

The soundsystem is the whole point. Silo runs custom Danley rigs that have been tuned over years of trial and error. Serious wattage, careful acoustic treatment, enough low-end extension to handle whatever Delta Heavy throw at it. This matters more for drum & bass than almost any other genre. DnB lives and dies by sub-bass. A weak system turns the music into a tinny approximation of itself. A strong system makes it come alive.

The layout helps too. Unlike the cavernous rooms at Brooklyn Storehouse or Knockdown Center, Silo stays intimate even when it's packed. You can see the DJ. You can read the crowd. The energy doesn't get lost in VIP sections or dissipate into empty space. It's built for focus.

Silo has been hosting bass music consistently, building a crowd that knows what they're there for. When Delta Heavy play here, they're playing to a room that understands how to receive them.

## The Context

Drum & bass has always had a weird relationship with New York. Born in the UK, refined in London and Bristol, it's never quite broken through to mainstream American consciousness the way house or techno has. We get moments — a festival slot, a viral TikTok track — but the infrastructure has been harder to maintain.

That's why this show feels significant. Delta Heavy aren't just another DnB act. They're headliner-level, major label backed, with actual chart success and festival pedigree. When artists at their level choose a warehouse in East Williamsburg over a Manhattan club, it says something about where the energy is in this city. It validates the work promoters and venues have been doing to build a sustainable scene.

They're road-tested right now — been touring hard across the US and Europe. This isn't a warm-up show or a throwaway date between bigger gigs. This is a proper headline appearance in a room built for exactly what they do.

For the DnB-curious, this is the entry point. Delta Heavy's sound is accessible without being dumbed down, heavy without being alienating. They play records you can sing along to, mixed with deeper cuts that reward attention. It's a masterclass in crowd-reading from two people who have been doing this at the highest level for fifteen years.

## The Prediction

Full-scale assault. Delta Heavy don't do subtle, and Silo's system is built for the punishment they're going to deliver. The room will be packed — DnB nights in this city always draw a dedicated crowd, and this is the biggest name Silo has hosted in the genre for a minute.

"Get By" will get dropped, probably in multiple forms. "Ghost" will soundtrack some transcendent moment around 2 AM. Somewhere in the middle they'll test something new on a crowd that will respond honestly. There will be moments of unexpected beauty, because they understand that contrast makes the heavy parts hit harder. And there will be moments of pure chaos, when the bass drops and the room becomes a single organism moving in the dark.

By the time they finish, the crowd will be different than when they started. That's the promise of a great drum & bass set. Delta Heavy are very good at keeping promises.

## The Verdict

Essential for bass music heads. Highly recommended for the DnB-curious. A reminder for everyone else that there's more to electronic music than the four-on-the-floor rhythms that dominate most NYC clubs.

Fifteen years in, Hall and James are still refining their craft, still finding new ways to make soundsystems cry uncle, still building the kind of immersive experiences that turn casual listeners into lifers. If you've never experienced proper drum & bass on a proper system, this is your chance. If you have, you already know why you need to be there.

**Rating: 4.5/5** — UK bass royalty in an intimate warehouse setting. Delta Heavy's production pedigree plus Silo's soundsystem should make this one of the DnB highlights of the year.
