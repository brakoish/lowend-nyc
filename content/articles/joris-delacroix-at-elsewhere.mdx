# Joris Delacroix at Elsewhere: French Melodic Techno Lands in Bushwick

**Bushwick, Brooklyn — March 18, 2026**

There's this thing happening in French electronic music right now that doesn't sound like anything else. Not the hard Paris warehouse stuff. Not the glossy Daft Punk nostalgia trip that's been running on fumes since 2013. Something quieter, more inward. Music that builds rooms inside your head instead of blowing them up.

Joris Delacroix has been doing this since 2010, and he's finally bringing it to Elsewhere's Zone One tonight.

## The backstory: Montpellier to Brooklyn

Delacroix came up in Montpellier, that Mediterranean city that punches above its weight for electronic music. He started releasing tracks in 2010—right when EDM was about to swallow American dance music whole and spit out something unrecognizable. While everyone else was chasing bigger drops and louder synths, he went the other way. Deeper. More patient. Actually interested in what happens between the notes.

His breakout was "Air France," this gorgeous floating thing that somehow managed to be immediately catchy without being stupid. That track got him noticed, but the albums proved he wasn't a one-off: *Night Visions* (2017), *Going Back* (2020), *Room With View* (2021). Each one showed more range than the last.

Here's what separates Delacroix from the melodic techno flood: he actually writes songs. Not loops that get hammered until your ears go numb. His tracks have structure—verses, choruses, bridges, breakdowns that earn their payoff. You can listen close or you can dance. Both work.

Take "Epoque," his biggest track. Seven minutes built around a simple piano line that keeps adding layers: pulsing bass, shimmering pads, drums that know when to push and when to hold back. Nothing revolutionary on paper. But the timing is perfect. Every element hits exactly where it should. This is what happens when someone understands that emotional impact comes from restraint, not piling on more.

Or "Start The Engine" with Ornette—that vocal is soulful without being corny, the groove drives without being aggressive. The breakdown actually builds toward something instead of just dropping out for cheap suspense. These are the marks of a producer who respects his audience enough to do the work.

## The live thing: hardware and risk

Delacroix isn't just a studio guy. Watch his live sets from T7 in Paris, the Arc de Triompis in Montpellier, ADE in Amsterdam, or those marathon all-nighters at Rex Club—he's actually up there with gear. Synthesizers, drum machines, controllers. Building the tracks in real time, not triggering pre-made arrangements.

This matters. In 2026, "live" electronic music usually means a DJ set with some extra lights. Delacroix is doing something else. You can see him twisting filters, firing sequences, adjusting parameters on the fly. There's risk in it. The possibility of mistakes. The human element that separates live music from hitting play on a playlist.

His sets run long. That Rex Club video is six hours. The T7 set from last year is nearly two hours straight. This isn't the festival slot, carefully calibrated for maximum Instagram impact. It's the long-form thing—the chance to actually explore a sound, to take people through valleys and peaks instead of just peaks.

The style sits somewhere between melodic techno, progressive house, and that old "electronica" tag from before it became embarrassing. You can hear Booka Shade in the precision, Stephan Bodzin in the emotional directness, the wider German and French traditions that care more about atmosphere than brute force. But Delacroix has his own voice. You know it's him within a few bars.

## Why Elsewhere works

Elsewhere gets it. The multi-room complex in Bushwick understands that "underground" doesn't have to mean "uncomfortable." Different spaces for different energies: The Hall for the big productions, Zone One for intimacy, The Rooftop for open air, The Loft for the weird stuff.

Delacroix in Zone One is the right call. His music doesn't need a massive room. If anything, it works better up close, where you can actually hear the details—the subtle shifts, the careful sound design. Zone One has the sound system and the crowd that listens. Both matter for this.

Bushwick knows electronic music. The neighborhood has absorbed a decade of techno, house, experimental sounds washing through Brooklyn. The Elsewhere crowd tends to be there for the music, not the scene. They're the right audience.

## Why tonight matters

French electronic music has a weird relationship with American audiences. Daft Punk's massive success created a template that everyone since has struggled to escape: the cool, detached, slightly ironic French Touch thing. But there's this whole other tradition—deeper, more emotional—that never broke through the same way.

Delacroix is that tradition. His music isn't afraid to be beautiful, or sad, or hopeful. In a moment when so much electronic music is either aggressively bleak or cynically commercial, that commitment to actual feeling is almost radical.

The timing works too. He's been releasing new material—"Turpentine," "Lonely2," "Make A Move"—that shows he's still evolving. His T7 set last year had "a bunch of new tracks," per his own posts. He's in a productive phase, working through ideas in public.

For New York, this is rare. Delacroix doesn't tour the US constantly. When he comes through, it's worth catching. He's the reminder of why you got into this in the first place—not for the drops or the drugs or the social media posts, but for that moment when sound becomes feeling, when a room of strangers becomes something else for a few hours.

## The guess

I'm betting on a long set. Delacroix doesn't do quick hits. He'll take his time, build the atmosphere, let things breathe. Early night will be deeper, more restrained, setting the mood. As the room fills and the energy rises, he'll bring in the driving stuff—the pulsing basslines, the propulsive rhythms. But even at peak, there will be melody, emotion, the human thing that separates his work from the mechanical churn.

Zone One's system should handle his productions, let those frequencies hit with the weight they need. The crowd will be attentive early, drawn in by the novelty of a European name they might not know as well as the local regulars. By the end, if it goes right, they'll be converted. Part of the global audience he's been building for fifteen years.

This is the kind of show that defines a venue. Elsewhere isn't just booking crowd-pleasers. They're bringing in artists who challenge their audience, who offer something different from the usual circuit. Delacroix fits: accessible enough for newcomers, sophisticated enough for veterans, distinctive enough to stick with you.

If you're unsure: go. Bring an open mind and comfortable shoes. Stay late. Let the music work. You might find your new favorite artist, or at least remember why you started caring about any of this.

**Rating: 4/5** — Highly recommended for melodic techno heads, progressive house fans, anyone who wants electronic music with actual emotional weight. Rare chance to catch a European veteran in an intimate Brooklyn room.
