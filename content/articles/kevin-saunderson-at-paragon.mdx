# Kevin Saunderson at Paragon: The Belleville Three Come to Brooklyn

**Brooklyn — March 2026**

Some artists you see because they're having a moment. Others you see because they *are* the moment, have always been the moment, and will continue to be the moment long after the hype cycle moves on. Kevin Saunderson falls firmly into the second category.

When Paragon announced that the Detroit techno originator would be taking over their soundsystem, I felt that rare jolt of genuine excitement that gets harder to come by the longer you spend in this city's clubs. This isn't just another DJ booking. This is a chance to stand in the same room as one of the architects of electronic dance music itself.

## The architect

Kevin Saunderson doesn't need an introduction, but he deserves one anyway. Born in Brooklyn in 1964, he moved to Belleville, Michigan at age nine, where he met Juan Atkins and Derrick May at Belleville High School. Together, they would become the Belleville Three, the trio credited with inventing Detroit techno and, by extension, laying the foundation for the entire global electronic music industry.

Think about that for a second. The music you're listening to right now, the clubs you go to, the festivals, the culture, the entire ecosystem that employs millions and moves billions, it all traces back to three kids in a Detroit suburb experimenting with synthesizers and trying to make something that didn't exist yet.

Saunderson's contributions go beyond just being in the right place at the right time. As Inner City, he gave the world "Big Fun" and "Good Life," tracks that crossed over from underground clubs to mainstream charts without losing their soul. Nine UK top 40 hits. Six million records sold. But the commercial success is almost beside the point. What matters is that Saunderson proved techno could be populist without being pedestrian, accessible without being diluted.

Then there's the Reese bassline. If you've ever felt your ribcage rattle at a drum and bass night, if you've ever lost your mind to a sub-bass drop in a techno set, you've experienced the ripple effects of Saunderson's 1988 track "Just Want Another Chance." That bass sound, created on a Casio CZ-1000 using phase distortion synthesis, became the most sampled bassline in jungle and drum and bass history. It's the "Amen break" of low-end, the foundation that an entire genre was built upon.

Under aliases like E-Dancer, Tronik House, and The Reese Project, Saunderson has spent four decades refusing to sit still. His KMS label has been a launchpad for countless artists. His sets remain propulsive, creative, and utterly joyous. This is a man who has done everything there is to do in electronic music, and he's still out here, week after week, showing everyone how it's done.

## The room

Paragon sits at 990 Broadway in Brooklyn, right on the border of Bed-Stuy and Bushwick, beneath the elevated J/M/Z tracks. It's a 270-capacity room with a soundsystem that punches well above its weight class and an interior designed by Safwat Riad that references churches and spiritual architecture. The regal DJ booth hovers above enormous speakers. A mirrored balcony refracts the lights. It's dramatic without being pretentious, intimate without being cramped.

What I love about Paragon is that it doesn't try to be anything it's not. It's not a Manhattan bottle service club pretending to care about underground music. It's not a cavernous warehouse where you lose all sense of connection. It's a proper dance club, built for people who want to actually dance, run by people who understand that the soundsystem matters more than the VIP list.

The venue has become a Brooklyn staple over the past few years, hosting everything from fashion shows to wild late-night DJ sets. It's a space that respects the music, which is exactly what you want when Kevin Saunderson comes to town. This isn't an artist who needs elaborate production or pyrotechnics. He needs a good soundsystem, a dark room, and a crowd that's ready to move.

Paragon gives him all three.

## Why this matters

Look, I see a lot of shows. I write about a lot of DJs. And I try not to get too precious about the whole "legend" thing, because sometimes it feels like every other press release is calling someone a legend these days. But Kevin Saunderson actually is one. He's not a legend because he's been around a long time. He's a legend because he changed the course of music history.

There's something profound about witnessing an artist who was there at the beginning, who helped define what this music could be, who is still out here doing it with passion and skill four decades later. When Saunderson plays "Big Fun" or drops a track that samples that iconic Reese bassline, he's not just playing records. He's playing history. He's reminding us where we came from.

And the thing is, he could easily coast. He could show up, play the hits, collect the check, and go home. Plenty of artists from his era do exactly that. But Saunderson has never been interested in being a museum piece. His sets are forward-looking, filled with new music alongside the classics. He understands that techno is a living, evolving form, and he's still contributing to that evolution.

I saw him a few years ago at a festival, and what struck me wasn't just the technical skill (though that was impeccable). It was the joy. This man has been doing this for nearly forty years, and he still clearly loves it. The way he reads a crowd, the way he builds tension and releases it, the way he moves between eras and styles without ever losing the thread, it's the work of someone who has mastered their craft and still finds new things to discover within it.

## The prediction

I'm expecting a history lesson and a party, simultaneously. Saunderson has the rare ability to educate without lecturing, to pay homage without getting stuck in nostalgia. He'll play the Inner City classics because they still work, because "Good Life" still makes people lose their minds regardless of how many times they've heard it. But he'll also play the new stuff, the underground cuts, the tracks that remind you techno is still a vital, evolving art form.

The Paragon soundsystem should do justice to the low-end that defines Saunderson's sound. That Reese bassline, those Inner City sub frequencies, they need room to breathe. They need a system that can handle the weight. Based on my experiences at Paragon, I'm confident they'll deliver.

The crowd will be a mix, I suspect. The heads who know exactly who they're seeing and what it means. The younger ravers who might not know the full history but know a good party when they find one. The Brooklyn regulars who trust Paragon's bookings. All of them, together, in a room with one of the people who made all of this possible.

That's the thing about seeing a true originator. It's not just about the music, though the music will be excellent. It's about the connection to something larger than any single night. When Kevin Saunderson plays, he's channeling four decades of innovation, of struggle, of joy, of collective experience. He's a living bridge between techno's past and its future.

And for one night in Brooklyn, we get to stand on that bridge with him.

**Rating: 5/5** — Essential. A rare chance to witness a genuine pioneer in an intimate setting. This is the kind of booking that reminds you why New York's club scene still matters.
