# Carl Cox at Brooklyn Storehouse

**Brooklyn Navy Yard — March 21, 2026**

About two hours into a Carl Cox set, something clicks. You're not thinking about the week you had or the emails waiting for you. You're just there — sweat on the back of your neck, bass in your chest, surrounded by strangers who feel like family for a few hours. That's the thing about Cox: he still makes you remember why you fell for this music in the first place.

He's back in Brooklyn on March 21st. And yeah, we need this.

## Why Carl Cox Still Matters

Let me be clear about something — Carl Cox doesn't need me to validate him. The man started DJing in the '80s. He was there for acid house's birth in Britain. He held down Space Ibiza's terrace for fifteen years and turned it into the most famous room in dance music. He's played every festival, every legendary club, every scene-defining party you can name.

But here's what keeps him relevant while other veterans coast on nostalgia: he never stopped caring. His recent stuff — "Pure," his remixes for the new generation — still pushes forward. He's not a museum piece. He's still in the game, still hungry, still playing like he's got something to prove.

30+ years. Hundreds of thousands of records. Millions of fans across generations. But the real test is simpler: when he plays, does the room still go off? With Cox, the answer is always yes. Not because people feel obligated to cheer. Because he's that good.

## The Room: Brooklyn Storehouse

Let's talk about the space, because it matters. Brooklyn Storehouse sits in the Navy Yard, that industrial stretch along the East River that's become the last real home for warehouse techno in this city. Concrete floors. High ceilings. Room for thousands without the crush.

Teksupport has made this their Brooklyn spot, and they've earned it. In a city where the underground keeps getting pushed to the margins — rezoned, renovated, priced out — Teksupport has held down legitimate warehouse events with real production. Funktion-One sound. Proper lighting. The difference between a rave and a glorified bar night.

But what makes Brooklyn Storehouse work is the crowd. Teksupport draws people who know why they're there. No bottle service. No Instagram backdrop hunters. Just people who came for the music, who understand that 2 AM in a warehouse hits different than midnight at a club. When Cox steps up here, he's not playing to tourists. He's playing to people who get it.

## The Support

The lineup doesn't start and end with Cox. Nicole Moudaber b2b Ilario Alicante could headline their own night. Moudaber's been a force for years — her MOOD brand and radio show are essential infrastructure for the global scene. She's got that rare mix of technical precision and emotional intuition. Her sets feel relentless but human.

Ilario Alicante represents the Italian techno that's been dominating European warehouses for a decade. Drumcode releases. Cocoon connections. Top-tier peak-time stuff. Put him back-to-back with Moudaber and you've got a warm-up that most nights would kill for as a main event.

VIOT holds down opening duties. On a stacked night like this, the opener matters more than people admit. VIOT will read the room, build the energy without peaking early. Thankless work when it's done right.

## Why This One Counts

New York's relationship with techno is complicated. We were there at the start — Paradise Garage, the Loft, the '90s rave scene. But we fell behind. Berlin, Detroit, even London built better infrastructure while our clubs closed or changed hands. The warehouse scene kept getting pushed further out, further underground.

Something's shifting. Teksupport's rise lines up with a broader renaissance. Basement, H0l0, Nowadays — the threads are weaving into something that feels sustainable. We're not back to the glory days, but we're not in the wilderness anymore either.

Carl Cox at Brooklyn Storehouse is a milestone in that shift. It's not just a big name in a big room. It's validation. When someone like Cox chooses a Brooklyn warehouse over a Manhattan marquee club, it says something about where the energy is. The warehouse scene isn't just surviving. It's where things are actually happening.

The timing helps. March 21st puts this right before Ultra Miami, where Cox plays his annual set. He's coming to us first, fresh off whatever prep he's doing for that festival stage, but playing a room that's intimate by comparison. Festival Cox is big, bombastic, tens of thousands. Warehouse Cox is closer, more dangerous, more connected to what this music was supposed to be.

## What You're In For

If you've never seen Carl Cox live, here's the deal. Three hours minimum, probably more. A journey: dubby and atmospheric early, building through tech house grooves, peak-time weapons when the room's ready, moments of beauty in the chaos. Cox knows pacing. He knows when to push, when to pull back, when to drop something familiar, when to challenge you with something new.

His mixing is technically sharp but never cold. Classic Cox selections next to records from producers half his age. Working the EQs, riding the faders, treating the mixer like an instrument. This is craft, not just curation.

And the energy — Cox plays like he means it. Every transition, every drop, every build and release feels considered. By the time he's done, the room's transformed. That's the promise. That's why people keep coming back.

## Bottom Line

Don't skip this. If you care about techno, if you care about New York's electronic music culture, if you just want to see what three decades of mastery looks like — be at Brooklyn Storehouse on March 21st.

The lineup's right. The room's right. The timing's right, with Cox heading to Ultra after. Everything lines up.

Teksupport has been building toward nights like this. They've earned the trust of serious artists and serious crowds. Carl Cox doesn't play just anywhere. His presence here is a co-sign — recognition that what's happening in Brooklyn right now matters globally.

Three decades in, Carl Cox is still the standard. Still the king. Still the reason half of us got into this. See you in the warehouse.

**Rating: 5/5** — Unmissable. A generational talent in a room that understands what he's bringing. This is why we still go out.
