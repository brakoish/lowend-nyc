# Joris Delacroix at Elsewhere: French Melodic Techno Lands in Bushwick

**Bushwick, Brooklyn — March 18, 2026**

There's a particular strain of electronic music coming out of France right now that feels like it's operating on its own frequency. Not the brutalist techno of the Parisian warehouse scene, not the glossy French Touch revival that's been trying to recapture Daft Punk's magic for two decades. Something else. Something more introspective, more melodic, more concerned with building emotional landscapes than destroying dancefloors.

Joris Delacroix has been one of the most consistent practitioners of this sound since he emerged from Montpellier in 2010. And now he's bringing it to Elsewhere's Zone One, which feels like exactly the right room for what he does.

## The artist: a decade of melodic precision

Delacroix's story starts in the south of France, in Montpellier, that sun-drenched city on the Mediterranean that's produced more good electronic music than its size would suggest. He began releasing music in 2010, right when the EDM wave was about to crash over America and change everything about how dance music was consumed and understood. While that scene was building toward bigger drops and louder synths, Delacroix was moving in a different direction: deeper, more nuanced, more interested in the spaces between the notes.

His breakthrough came with tracks like "Air France," a gorgeous, floating piece of melodic house that managed to be both immediately accessible and genuinely sophisticated. The track put him on the map, but it was the albums that followed, "Night Visions" (2017), "Going Back" (2020), "Room With View" (2021), that established him as an artist with real range and vision.

What distinguishes Delacroix from the flood of melodic techno producers is his commitment to actual musicality. This isn't loop-based production where a 16-bar phrase gets repeated until the crowd goes numb. His tracks breathe. They develop. They have verses and choruses, bridges and breakdowns, the kind of structural complexity that rewards close listening while still working on a dancefloor.

Take "Epoque," his most-streamed track with nearly a million YouTube views. It's seven minutes long, built around a simple piano motif that gradually accumulates layers: a pulsing bassline, shimmering synth pads, a restrained but effective drum pattern. Nothing revolutionary on paper. But the execution is flawless. Every element arrives exactly when it should, builds exactly as much as it needs to, resolves exactly where you hope it will. It's the work of someone who understands that emotional impact in electronic music comes from restraint, not excess.

Or "Start The Engine," featuring Ornette, which became a staple in melodic house sets for years after its release. The vocal is soulful without being cheesy, the groove is driving without being aggressive, the breakdown actually earns its euphoria through careful construction rather than cheap manipulation. These are the marks of a producer who respects his audience enough to do the work.

## The live show: hardware and humanity

Delacroix isn't just a studio artist. His live performances, documented in videos from T7 in Paris, the Arc de Triomphe in Montpellier, ADE in Amsterdam, and his legendary all-night-long sets at Rex Club, reveal a performer deeply engaged with the physical reality of making music. He plays live, actual hardware, synthesizers and drum machines and controllers, building his tracks in real time rather than triggering pre-made arrangements.

This matters. In an era where "live" electronic performances often mean little more than a DJ set with some extra bells and whistles, Delacroix's approach feels almost radical. You can see him manipulating filters, triggering sequences, adjusting parameters. The performance has risk in it, the possibility of mistakes, the human element that separates live music from playback.

His sets tend toward the marathon. The video of his all-night-long DJ set at Rex Club runs over six hours. His live set at T7, posted last year, is nearly two hours of continuous performance. This isn't the 90-minute festival slot, carefully calibrated for maximum impact. It's the long-form experience, the opportunity to really explore a sound, to take the audience on a journey that has time for valleys as well as peaks.

The style sits somewhere between melodic techno, progressive house, and what used to be called "electronica" before that term became unfashionable. There are echoes of Booka Shade in his precision, of Stephan Bodzin in his emotional directness, of the wider German and French traditions of electronic music that prioritize atmosphere and texture over brute force. But Delacroix has his own voice, recognizable within a few bars, consistent without being repetitive.

## The venue: why Elsewhere makes sense

Elsewhere has become one of Brooklyn's essential venues by understanding that "underground" doesn't have to mean "uncomfortable." The multi-room complex in Bushwick offers different spaces for different energies: The Hall for bigger productions, Zone One for more intimate experiences, The Rooftop for open-air moments, The Loft for experimental sounds.

Delacroix is playing Zone One, and that's the right choice. His music doesn't need a massive room to work. In fact, it might work better in a space where the audience can feel close to the performance, where the details of his sound design, the subtle shifts in his arrangements, can actually be heard and appreciated. Zone One has a reputation for excellent sound and a crowd that listens, two things that matter enormously for an artist like this.

The Bushwick location matters too. This is a neighborhood that understands electronic music, that has absorbed the waves of techno, house, and experimental sounds that have washed through Brooklyn over the past decade. The crowd at Elsewhere tends to be knowledgeable, open-minded, there for the music rather than the scene. They're the right audience for what Delacroix is offering.

## Why this show matters now

French electronic music has had a complicated relationship with American audiences. The massive success of Daft Punk created a template that subsequent French artists have struggled to escape: the cool, detached, slightly ironic approach to dance music that defined French Touch. But there's a whole other tradition, deeper and more emotional, that has never quite broken through in the same way.

Delacroix represents that tradition. His music wears its heart on its sleeve. It's not afraid to be beautiful, to be sad, to be hopeful. In a moment when so much electronic music feels either aggressively bleak or cynically commercial, his commitment to genuine emotion feels almost radical.

The timing of this show is significant too. Delacroix has been releasing new music consistently, tracks like "Turpentine," "Lonely2," "Make A Move," that suggest he's still evolving, still finding new angles on his sound. His recent live set at T7 featured "a bunch of new tracks," according to his own social media, suggesting that he's in a productive phase, working through new ideas in real time.

For New York audiences, this is a rare opportunity. Delacroix doesn't tour the US constantly. When he comes through, it's worth paying attention. He's the kind of artist who reminds you why you fell in love with electronic music in the first place: not for the drops or the drugs or the social scene, but for that moment when sound becomes feeling, when a room full of strangers becomes a temporary community united by something they can't quite name.

## The prediction

I'm expecting a long set. Delacroix doesn't do quick hits. He'll take his time, build his atmosphere, let the music breathe. The early part of the night will probably be deeper, more restrained, establishing the mood. As the room fills and the energy builds, he'll gradually introduce more driving elements, the pulsing basslines and propulsive rhythms that get bodies moving. But even at peak intensity, there will be melody, emotion, the human element that distinguishes his work from the mechanical churn of lesser techno.

Zone One's sound system should do justice to his productions, letting those carefully crafted frequencies hit with the weight they deserve. The crowd will be attentive, at least at first, drawn in by the novelty of a European artist they might not know as well as the local regulars. By the end, if everything goes right, they'll be converted, part of the global audience that Delacroix has been building methodically over the past fifteen years.

This is the kind of show that defines a venue's reputation. Elsewhere isn't just booking crowd-pleasers or safe names. They're bringing in artists who challenge their audience, who offer something different from the usual circuit of American techno and house DJs. Delacroix fits that mandate perfectly: accessible enough for newcomers, sophisticated enough for veterans, distinctive enough to leave an impression.

If you're on the fence: go. Bring an open mind and comfortable shoes. Be prepared to stay late. Let the music do its work. You might discover your new favorite artist, or at least remember why you started caring about this scene in the first place.

**Rating: 4/5** — Highly recommended for fans of melodic techno, progressive house, and emotionally resonant electronic music. A rare chance to catch a European veteran in an intimate Brooklyn setting.
