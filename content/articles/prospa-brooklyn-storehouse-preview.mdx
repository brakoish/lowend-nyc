# Prospa B2B Josh Baker at Brooklyn Storehouse: UK House Meets Brooklyn's Warehouse Underground

**Brooklyn Navy Yard — March 20, 2026**

There's a particular alchemy that happens when two selectors who truly understand each other get behind the decks together. Not the polite tag-team sets where each artist plays their biggest tracks and hands over with a fist bump—I'm talking about the real back-to-back, the kind where the conversation happens in real time, where one person's record becomes the jumping-off point for the other's next move, where the boundary between "your set" and "my set" dissolves into something neither could create alone.

That's what Prospa and Josh Baker are promising at Brooklyn Storehouse this Friday—and honestly, it's the kind of booking that makes me grateful Teksupport still exists in this city.

## The Venue: Brooklyn Storehouse

Let's start with the room, because it matters. Brooklyn Storehouse sits in the Navy Yard, that sprawling industrial complex along the East River that's become ground zero for the kind of large-scale electronic events that used to happen in actual warehouses before developers turned them all into luxury lofts. The Storehouse itself is exactly what the name suggests: a massive, raw space with high ceilings, concrete floors, and enough square footage to swallow a few thousand bodies without feeling cramped.

Teksupport has made this their Brooklyn home base, and for good reason. The production values are high—Funktion-One or similar, proper lighting rigs, the kind of attention to sound that separates a real rave from a glorified bar night—but the vibe stays gritty. There's no VIP bottle service nonsense. The crowd is there for the music, not the Instagram backdrop. In a city where the underground keeps getting pushed further and further to the margins, Brooklyn Storehouse represents something essential: a space big enough to host serious talent, raw enough to feel like a proper party.

## Prospa: Leeds' Rave Revivalists

If you haven't been paying attention to Prospa, you've been missing one of the most consistent stories in UK house music over the past few years. Harvey Blumler and Gosha Smith—the duo behind the name—have been on a rapid ascent since their 2018 breakthrough single "Prayer" helped revive Stress Records and announced them as serious contenders in the UK house revival.

Their trajectory makes sense when you know their background. Both came up through Leeds, that Northern city that's produced so much of the UK's best dance music. They met in the early 2010s, bonding over production before either had fully found their sound—Blumler was making dubstep-adjacent beats, Smith was exploring jazz-inflected hip-hop. When they converged, first on deep house and then on the classic rave sounds that would become their signature, something clicked.

What sets Prospa apart from the wave of producers mining the 90s for inspiration is their understanding that revival isn't enough. Yes, they reference the piano stabs, the breakbeats, the euphoric vocal chops of classic UK house and rave. But they're not making museum pieces. Tracks like "This Rhythm" (featuring RAHH on vocals), "If You Want My Loving," and the more recent "You Don't Own Me" feel immediate, designed for today's dancefloors even as they nod to yesterday's.

Their 2024-2025 run has been particularly strong. "This Rhythm" became a staple in sets across the UK and beyond, that combination of driving bassline and emotive vocal hitting the sweet spot between peak-time energy and genuine feeling. They've been releasing on Polydor and Stress Records, balancing major-label reach with underground credibility. And their DJ sets—this is crucial—have developed a reputation for being relentless, joyous, and technically sharp.

Prospa understands something fundamental about UK house: it's not about minimalism or subtlety. It's about euphoria, about collective release, about building moments where the whole room feels like one organism. When they play, they play for that moment.

## Josh Baker: The Selector's Selector

Josh Baker is a different kind of artist, though one equally rooted in the UK tradition. Where Prospa came up as producers who learned to DJ, Baker feels like a DJ's DJ—someone whose reputation was built on selection, on reading rooms, on the kind of encyclopedic knowledge of house music that only comes from years of digging and playing out.

His style sits somewhere between UK house, tech house, and the deeper shades of garage that have never quite gone away in Britain. He's not chasing trends. While the wider world of electronic music swings between hyperpop and minimal techno, Baker has stayed focused on what works: tight grooves, well-chosen vocals, basslines that move bodies without battering them.

The Beatport data tells part of the story—Baker's name appears alongside Prospa's on various releases and remixes, suggesting they've been orbiting the same circles for a while. But the real connection is harder to quantify. Both artists understand UK dance music as a living tradition, not a retro exercise. Both can play peak-time sets that never sacrifice musicality for cheap thrills. Both have that Northern sensibility—workmanlike, unpretentious, focused on the dancefloor above all else.

Baker's reputation has been growing steadily, particularly among those who pay attention to the deeper side of UK house. He's the kind of artist who might not have the massive streaming numbers or festival mainstage slots yet, but who other DJs consistently name-check as someone to watch. His productions—when he releases them—tend to be functional, well-crafted tools that work in sets without demanding attention. His DJ sets, by contrast, are where he shines: patient, knowledgeable, able to move between moods without losing the thread.

## Why This B2B Matters

Here's the thing: Prospa and Josh Baker don't play together often. In fact, as far as I can tell, this Brooklyn Storehouse date represents a rare—or possibly first—back-to-back pairing between the two. That's significant.

The UK house scene is tight-knit. Everyone knows everyone, plays with everyone, remixes everyone. But there's a difference between the scene's regular circuit and these cross-pollinating moments where two distinct voices get thrown together in a new context. When Prospa and Baker step up together at Brooklyn Storehouse, they're not just playing a gig—they're figuring something out in real time, in front of a few thousand New Yorkers who might not know either name as well as a London crowd would.

That's exciting for a few reasons. First, there's the unpredictability. A solo set gives you a pretty good idea of what you're going to get. A B2B—especially one between artists who haven't developed a routine together—could go anywhere. Will Prospa's euphoric, piano-heavy moments mesh with Baker's more restrained, groove-focused approach? Will they find common ground in the classics, or push each other into new territory? Nobody knows, probably including them. That's the point.

Second, there's the transatlantic dimension. UK house has always had a complicated relationship with American audiences. The sounds that dominate British clubs—UK garage, bassline, the particular strain of piano-driven house that Prospa specializes in—have never quite broken through to the mainstream in the US the way they have at home. When UK artists come to Brooklyn, they're often playing to crowds who are hearing this stuff fresh, without the years of cultural context that UK audiences bring. That can be liberating. It can also be challenging. Prospa and Baker will have to translate—not water down, but translate—their sound for a room that might not know all the references.

Finally, there's the Teksupport factor. This promoter has become essential to New York's electronic music ecosystem, particularly for the kind of large-scale warehouse events that used to happen more organically. They bring serious talent—techno legends, UK garage revivalists, house mainstays—to spaces like Brooklyn Storehouse, Brooklyn Hangar, and their various other venues. The crowd they draw is knowledgeable, committed, there for the right reasons. It's not the easiest room to play, but it's one where real moments can happen.

## The Prediction

I'm expecting a long night. Teksupport events tend to run late, and with Mink also on the bill, there's going to be plenty of music before Prospa and Baker even touch the decks. But when they do—when those two names appear together on the lineup, when they step up for their B2B—I think we're going to get something special.

Prospa will bring the energy, the euphoria, the big moments. Baker will bring the selection, the patience, the deep knowledge of how to build and release tension. Together, they might find a middle ground that neither would reach alone: a set that's both emotionally satisfying and technically impressive, that honors the UK tradition without being beholden to it, that works the Brooklyn Storehouse room into the kind of collective frenzy that justifies staying out until 4 AM on a Friday.

The UK house revival has been building for years now. Prospa is one of its most visible success stories. Baker represents its deeper, more underground current. Seeing them together, in a space like Brooklyn Storehouse, feels like a statement—not just about where UK house is, but about where it might go next.

If you're on the fence: go. This is the kind of booking that doesn't happen often. Two artists at different points in their careers, with different strengths, coming together for one night in a city that's hungry for what they're selling. The warehouse is waiting. The sound system is primed. All that's missing is you.

**Rating: 4.5/5** — Essential for fans of UK house, tech house, and proper warehouse raves. The B2B format adds unpredictability to an already compelling pairing.
