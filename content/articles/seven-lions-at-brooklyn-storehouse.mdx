# Seven Lions at Brooklyn Storehouse: melodic bass royalty descends on the Navy Yard

**Brooklyn Navy Yard — March 2026**

There's a particular alchemy that happens when an artist built for festival mainstages steps into a warehouse. The scale changes. The stakes change. What worked for fifty thousand bodies under open sky has to be recalibrated for a few thousand in concrete darkness. Some artists shrink in that transition. Others find something new, something sharper, something more dangerous.

Seven Lions has always operated in that tension. Jeff Montalvo's project was never just about the drop, though God knows he knows how to build one. It was about the journey: the way a track could start in ethereal ambience, mutate through trance arpeggios, detour into dubstep's gut-punch low end, and resolve into something that felt like catharsis. That's the formula that's kept him at the top of American bass music for over a decade. And now he's bringing it to Brooklyn Storehouse.

## The artist: from metalhead to melodic mastermind

Jeff Montalvo's origin story is almost too perfect. Santa Barbara kid, grew up playing guitar in metal bands, discovered electronic music through the gateway drug of UK drum & bass. The name Seven Lions comes from a fantasy novel he was reading at the time, something about a character who could transform into seven different lions. It's a fitting metaphor for what he's become: an artist with multiple modes, multiple faces, all of them genuinely him.

He broke through in 2012 with the "Days to Come" EP on OWSLA, Skrillex's label that was then defining the sound of American bass music. That release announced Seven Lions as something different. While the dubstep mainstream was chasing ever more aggressive drops, Montalvo was weaving in influences from trance, from progressive house, from the melodic sensibilities that would become his signature. "Days to Come" and its title track weren't just bangers. They were emotional experiences, built for the kind of transcendent moments that happen at 3 AM when the drugs are working and the crowd is unified.

The years since have been a masterclass in consistency. Albums like *Worlds Apart* (2014), *The Throes of Winter* (2015), and *Beyond the Veil* (2023) have refined and expanded his sound without losing its core identity. He's collaborated with everyone from Illenium to Above & Beyond, straddling the worlds of bass music and trance with an ease that few can match. His Ophelia Records, launched in 2018, has become a platform for the next generation of melodic bass producers: Wooli, Trivecta, Gem & Tauri, artists who share his vision of heavy music that doesn't sacrifice emotion for impact.

What separates Seven Lions from the pack is his understanding of dynamics. This is someone who actually knows how to build a set, not just string together bangers. He'll take you through ambient passages that feel like floating, hit you with trance breakdowns that trigger every euphoria receptor in your brain, then drop into dubstep territory that rattles your ribcage. It's not ADHD genre-hopping. It's architecture.

## The venue: Brooklyn Storehouse

Let's talk about where this is happening, because it matters. Brooklyn Storehouse sits in the Navy Yard, that sprawling industrial complex along the East River that's become the last real stronghold for warehouse-scale electronic music in this city. It's not pretty. It's not curated. It's a massive concrete box with high ceilings, industrial infrastructure, and enough square footage to hold thousands without feeling like a festival crowd.

Teksupport has made this their Brooklyn home base, and they've earned it. In a city where every interesting space seems to get converted to condos or co-working offices, they've managed to hold down legitimate warehouse events with serious production values. We're talking proper sound systems (Funktion-One or equivalent), lighting rigs that actually transform the space, and the kind of infrastructure that separates a real rave from a glorified club night.

But the real magic of Brooklyn Storehouse is the crowd. Teksupport has built a following that knows what they're there for. This isn't the bottle-service Instagram crowd. This isn't tourists looking for a night out. This is people who came for the music, who understand that a warehouse party hits different than a club set, who are willing to travel to the Navy Yard and stand on concrete for hours because the experience is worth it.

For Seven Lions, this is a different context than he's used to. His natural habitat is the festival mainstage: EDC, Lost Lands, his own Chronicles events that draw tens of thousands. Those are incredible experiences, but they're a different beast. The warehouse demands something more intimate, more responsive, more connected. You can't hide behind production value in a space like Brooklyn Storehouse. The sound system will expose you. The crowd will read you. The room will tell the truth.

## The sound: what to expect

If you've never experienced Seven Lions live, here's what you're in for. It's not a dubstep set, though there will be dubstep. It's not a trance set, though there will be trance. It's something that moves between territories, that refuses to stay in one genre lane because Montalvo's vision has always been bigger than any single style.

Expect to hear the classics: "Days to Come," "Worlds Apart," "Freesol," "Rush Over Me." These are the tracks that built his reputation, the ones that trigger instant recognition and collective release when they drop. But also expect the deeper cuts, the newer material, the Ophelia releases that show where his sound is heading now.

The tempo will vary. He'll take you through the 128 BPM territory of progressive house and trance, drop down into the 70-75 BPM half-time of dubstep and trap, find moments of ambient drift that let the crowd breathe before the next peak. It's a rollercoaster by design, calibrated to maximize emotional impact.

And the production value will be serious, even in a warehouse context. Seven Lions shows are known for their visual elements: the lighting, the lasers, the way the production enhances rather than distracts from the music. Teksupport will deliver on that front. This won't be a stripped-down DJ set. This will be a full Seven Lions experience, just in a more intimate container than usual.

## Why this show matters

New York City has always had a complicated relationship with bass music. While cities like Denver and Los Angeles became hubs for the dubstep and trap explosion, NYC stayed more focused on techno and house, the four-on-the-floor traditions that have deeper roots here. That's been shifting lately, the rise of artists like ISOxo, Knock2, and the broader wave of American bass music has found more footholds in the city. But it's still not the dominant sound.

Seven Lions at Brooklyn Storehouse feels like a statement. It's a major artist in the bass music world choosing to play a warehouse in Brooklyn rather than a bigger, more conventional venue. It's recognition that the warehouse scene in this city has become essential, that the crowd at Teksupport events is worth playing for, that there's something happening in these industrial spaces that matters on a national level.

There's also the timing. Seven Lions has been on a run lately, with the *Beyond the Veil* album in 2023 and steady releases on Ophelia since. He's not resting on legacy. He's actively pushing his sound forward, incorporating new influences, working with new artists, staying relevant in a scene that moves fast. This show comes at a moment when he's still peaking, still hungry, still proving why he's lasted over ten years in American electronic music.

For the crowd, this is a chance to see an artist who normally plays to massive festival audiences in a setting that's actually human-scale. The connection between artist and audience in a warehouse is different than on a mainstage. You can see the DJ. You can feel the sweat. The sound hits your body differently when you're not a hundred yards from the speakers. This is Seven Lions up close, and that's a rare opportunity.

## The prediction

I'm expecting a long night. Teksupport events tend to run late, and Seven Lions is known for extended sets when the vibe is right. He'll have time to stretch out, to take the crowd through the full range of his sound, to build and release tension over hours rather than minutes.

The opener will matter. Whoever Teksupport has booked to warm up the room will set the tone, build the energy, prepare the crowd for what's coming. Based on their track record, it'll be someone who understands the assignment.

And when Seven Lions finally takes over, I expect something special. This is an artist who knows how to read a room, how to adjust his set to the energy in front of him. He won't just play his festival bangers on autopilot. He'll craft something for this specific night, this specific crowd, this specific warehouse. That's what separates the greats from the good.

The bass will be heavy. The melodies will soar. There will be moments of genuine beauty in the chaos, those transcendent passages where the whole room feels like one thing. And there will be drops that hit like a truck, because Seven Lions has never been afraid of impact when the moment calls for it.

**Rating: 4.5/5** — Essential for fans of melodic bass, dubstep, and trance. A rare chance to see a festival headliner in an intimate warehouse setting.
