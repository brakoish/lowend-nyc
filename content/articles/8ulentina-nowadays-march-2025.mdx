# 8ULENTINA and Arushi Jain Bring the Diasporic Future to Nowadays

**Ridgewood, Queens — March 18, 2026**

There's a frequency Nowadays captures better than almost any room in the city. Past the warehouses on Flushing Avenue, past the auto shops and scrap yards, the venue sits there like a concrete bunker—indoor room dark and cavernous, outdoor space open to the Ridgewood sky. It's built for patience. For music that unfolds instead of drops.

Tonight's bill pairs two artists who couldn't be more geographically different—8ULENTINA from Oakland, Arushi Jain from Delhi via San Francisco—and yet sonically, they're having the same conversation. Both are figuring out what electronic music means from a diasporic position. Both use modular systems and digital tools to work with traditions that were never supposed to stay static.

## 8ULENTINA: Club Chai and What Comes After

I've been following 8ULENTINA since the early Club Chai days—that Oakland party series they ran with FOOZOOL that became a label, a radio show, and probably the most important incubator for diasporic club music on the West Coast. Pitchfork called their *Eucalyptus* EP "nimble movement between genres and the decentering of Western cultural narratives." Fine. But that doesn't tell you what happens when you're actually standing in front of the speakers.

Their approach is sculptural. 8ULENTINA builds tracks the way they build their installation art—layering darbuka and ney samples into structures that feel ancient and immediate at the same time. "Metal Clip" is the one that gets me: a tempered metallic beat anchoring muddled string and flute melodies that spiral off into space. It works on headphones, sure. But in a room like Nowadays—where the system has room to breathe—it becomes something else. A ritual. A conversation between acoustic and electronic that doesn't pick sides.

What separates 8ULENTINA from every producer sampling "world music" (a term they'd laugh at) is their refusal to exoticize their own heritage. Their father's family is from Turkey, and they've talked in interviews about hybridizing traditional Middle Eastern instruments with electronic percussion not as a gimmick, but as a way to access spirituality and ancestry through technology. The Alevi ritual instruments they sample aren't decoration—they're a direct line to something personal, something that exists in tension and distance for anyone in the diaspora.

Tonight I'm expecting the ambient side of their catalog more than the club tools. "Wander Flute" with its dulled, pummeling heartbeat rhythm. The newer experimental work they've been teasing. But 8ULENTINA has never been predictable, and that's the point.

## Arushi Jain: The Modular Princess

If 8ULENTINA works with Middle Eastern acoustic instruments fed through electronics, Arushi Jain comes from the opposite direction—starting with the modular synthesizer and finding her way back to Hindustani classical tradition. She calls herself @modularprincess on socials, and it fits: there's something regal about her command of the Eurorack system, the way she patches cables like she's conducting an orchestra of voltage.

Her 2021 album *Under the Lilac Sky* was the breakthrough—six tracks that proved you could make genuinely transportive ambient music while keeping one foot in the raga tradition. "Richer Than Blood" still gives me chills, those vocal drones processed through delay and reverb until they become architecture. But her live sets are where the real thing happens. I caught her opening for Suzanne Ciani a few years back at Diva of the Diode, and the way she held the room for forty minutes—building from near-silence to cascading waves of harmonics—showed an artist who understands pacing as a spiritual practice.

Jain's background is in Indian classical music, but she's embedded in the experimental electronic world too. Her releases on Leaving Records and her collaborations with the modular synthesis community have put her in a position as a bridge between two worlds that don't always talk. The Hindustani influence isn't pasted on top—it's structural, embedded in how she thinks about time and timbre.

Her most recent album, *Delight* (2024), pushes further into song form while keeping that drone-heavy, meditative quality. "Zid" showcases her vocal work—layered, processed, but never losing the human breath at the center. Live, she tends to stretch things out, letting the modular system do what it does best: evolve, surprise, become something other than what you started with.

## Why This Bill Works

On paper, pairing a Turkish-Arab artist from Oakland with an Indian classical-trained modular synthesist from Delhi might look like a festival programmer's idea of "world electronic music." But that's not what's happening here. Both 8ULENTINA and Arushi Jain are doing something more specific and more interesting: they're using electronic music as a tool for diasporic world-building.

The Club Chai ethos that 8ULENTINA helped establish—refusing genres while centering non-Western sounds and marginalized identities—fits naturally with Jain's practice. Both artists are skeptical of easy categorization. Both are interested in what happens when you refuse to separate "traditional" from "experimental," "acoustic" from "electronic." Both understand that the club can be a space for healing, for care, for practices that go deeper than weekend entertainment.

And Nowadays is the right room for this conversation. The venue has become essential to New York's underground partly because it refuses to be just one thing. The Mister Sunday parties are legendary for their egalitarian, no-phones dancefloor ethos. The ambient and experimental programming—often tucked into weeknights or the indoor room's quieter hours—has created space for artists who need time and attention rather than just a slot before the headliner. The sound system is tuned for detail. The crowd knows how to listen.

## The Prediction

I've seen enough bills at Nowadays to know when something special is brewing. This isn't a throw-together tour package—it's a curated pairing that actually makes sense. 8ULENTINA will likely lean into the more experimental, ambient side of their catalog, and Jain will probably stretch her modular system into territories that surprise even people who know her records.

The real magic, if we're lucky, might come from proximity—two artists sharing a green room, sharing ideas, maybe even sharing the stage for a moment. The diasporic electronic scene is smaller than it looks, and nights like this are where connections get made, where the next collaborations get seeded.

If you're on the fence about trekking out to Ridgewood on a Wednesday: go. This is the kind of bill that defines a venue's programming identity. It's the kind of night that reminds you why you moved to New York in the first place—or why you never left.

**Rating: 4.5/5** — Essential for fans of ambient, experimental, and diasporic electronic music. The pairing is thoughtful, the room is perfect, and both artists are operating at peak form.
