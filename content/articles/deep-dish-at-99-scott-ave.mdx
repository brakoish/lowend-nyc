# Deep Dish at 99 Scott Ave: The return of house music's most underrated dynasty

**East Williamsburg — March 28, 2026**

I didn't think I'd see this again.

When the announcement hit my feed, I stared at it for a solid minute. Deep Dish. Together. At 99 Scott Ave. Not a festival slot, not some Vegas residency cash grab. An actual warehouse in Brooklyn, the kind of room where this duo built their legend in the first place.

Ali Shirazinia and Sharam Tayebi. Two Iranian kids who met in DC in the early 90s, changed the shape of American house music, dominated the global scene for a decade, then went their separate ways in 2006. Solo careers happened. Sharam became a mainstay of the big-room circuit. Ali dove deeper into the underground, producing as Dubfire and launching SCI+TEC. Both succeeded. Neither was Deep Dish.

Now they're back. And they picked Brooklyn.

## The dynasty nobody talks about enough

Let me be clear about something. Before there was Disclosure, before Duke Dumont, before any of the UK house acts that Americans now treat like they invented the genre, there was Deep Dish. Two Iranian-American producers who took the blueprint of Chicago house and Detroit techno, ran it through their own weird filter, and created something that dominated dancefloors from Ibiza to Miami to, yeah, Brooklyn warehouses.

"Flashdance" still hits. That 2004 rework of the Irene Cara classic, all stuttering vocals and massive breakdowns, was everywhere. But it wasn't even their best work. Go back to *Junk Science* from 1998. Listen to what they were doing with breaks and progressive textures while most American producers were still trying to figure out how to program a 909. They were ahead. Way ahead.

The Grammy in 2002 for remastering Dido's "Thank You"? That's the mainstream credential. But the real credential is the discography. Remixes for Janet Jackson, Depeche Mode, The Rolling Stones. Their Yoshitoshi label, which became a cornerstone of progressive house. The way they made American dance music credible to European audiences at a time when that crossover barely existed.

And then they split. 2006. Creative differences, the usual story. Sharam kept the big-room energy, the festival slots, the accessible hooks. Ali became Dubfire, went minimal, went techno, went deeper than Deep Dish ever went. Both had hits. Both maintained careers most producers would kill for. But the magic? That specific alchemy of Ali's darkness and Sharam's melodic instinct? Gone.

Until now.

## 99 Scott Ave: The right room for the right return

They could have picked Output, back when it existed. They could have picked Avant Gardner, or Mirage, or any of the mega-venues that throw around "warehouse" in their marketing but feel more like airports with sound systems.

They picked 99 Scott Ave.

This matters. 99 Scott is an actual warehouse in East Williamsburg, the kind of industrial space that Brooklyn developers haven't managed to condo-ize yet. Concrete floors, exposed brick, a ceiling that disappears into darkness. The booking philosophy here has always leaned toward the credible over the commercial. They've hosted everyone from Four Tet to Bonobo to the kind of techno acts that don't bother with American tours because the room options are usually so bad.

The sound system punches. The crowd knows why they're there. This isn't a place you stumble into on a Saturday night looking for bottle service. This is a place you seek out because you read the lineup, you know the history, and you understand that some shows only happen in rooms like this.

For Deep Dish's reunion, there is no better choice. They came up in warehouses. DC's early rave scene, then the global circuit of illegal parties and after-hours that defined the 90s. They understand that house music breathes differently in a room with history, with grit, with walls that have absorbed decades of bass.

99 Scott has that. And on March 28th, it gets the duo that helped define what this music could be.

## What this reunion actually means

Let's not be naive. Reunions are tricky. Sometimes they're about money. Sometimes they're about ego. Sometimes the magic is just gone, and what you get is two famous people going through the motions for a check.

I don't think that's what this is.

Ali and Sharam have both been successful enough that they don't need to do this. Dubfire's SCI+TEC label and his hybrid live/DJ sets have kept him relevant in the techno underground. Sharam's solo output and his Wildcast brand have maintained his presence in the progressive and melodic spaces. They could keep doing their separate things indefinitely.

The fact that they're coming back together, and the fact that they're doing it in a Brooklyn warehouse rather than some EDC mainstage, suggests something else. It suggests they miss it. It suggests that whatever creative differences drove them apart have either been resolved or become irrelevant in the face of what they built together.

Deep Dish wasn't just a successful act. They were a sound. That specific blend of progressive structure, house groove, and unexpected left turns. The way Ali's techno instincts collided with Sharam's ear for melody. You can't fake that. You can't hire session musicians to recreate it. It's either there or it isn't.

March 28th is the test. Either the spark is still alive, or we're all going to witness a very expensive nostalgia trip.

I'm betting on the spark.

## The context: Why 2026 feels right

House music is in a weird place right now. On one hand, it's bigger than ever. Streaming numbers through the roof, festival lineups dominated by four-on-the-floor, every pop star suddenly discovering 124 BPM. On the other hand, the soul of it feels scattered. The underground is healthy but fragmented. The mainstream is profitable but often hollow.

Deep Dish coming back now feels like a statement. They represent a bridge between eras. The 90s warehouse roots, the 2000s progressive explosion, the modern world where those distinctions barely matter anymore. They can play to kids who discovered house music through Fred Again and to heads who were buying Yoshitoshi vinyl in 1999.

More than that, they represent craft. In an era of playlist-friendly drops and algorithm-optimized song structures, Deep Dish built their reputation on sets that took you somewhere. Long blends, unexpected selections, patience with the build. They treated DJing as an art form at a time when most American producers were still figuring out that DJing and producing were different skills.

If they bring that approach to 99 Scott Ave, if they treat this reunion not as a victory lap but as a continuation, then we're in for something special.

## What to expect (and what not to)

Don't expect "Flashdance" on repeat. Don't expect a greatest-hits set designed for casual fans who recognize one song. That's not who these guys are, and it's definitely not who they've become in their solo years.

Ali's Dubfire sets are legendary for their intensity, their willingness to go dark and stay there. Sharam's recent output has leaned into the melodic and emotional, that progressive house heart that never quite left him. Put them back in the same booth, and what emerges will be something neither could create alone.

I'm expecting journey sets. The kind that start deep, build slow, peak hard, and resolve in ways you don't see coming. I'm expecting tracks from their solo careers reinterpreted through the Deep Dish lens. I'm expecting selections that span decades and genres, held together by that specific taste level that defined their best work.

And I'm expecting a crowd that understands what they're witnessing. 99 Scott doesn't draw the casual tourist crowd. The people in that room will know the history. They'll know why this matters. That energy feeds back to the DJs. It creates the conditions for something memorable.

## The verdict

This is the most important house music event in NYC this year. Not the biggest. Not the most hyped. The most important.

Deep Dish defined an era of American dance music that too many people have forgotten or never knew. They proved that house could be complex, emotional, globally competitive. They built a sound that influenced everyone who came after, even if those influencers don't always know the source.

Their return, in a proper Brooklyn warehouse, is a reminder of what this music can be when it's treated with respect. When the artists care more about the craft than the check. When the room matters as much as the name on the flyer.

Ali and Sharam are back. 99 Scott Ave is waiting. And for one night in March, we get to see if the magic survived.

I'll be there. You should be too.

**Rating: 5/5** — Historic. Unmissable. The reunion we didn't know we needed until it was announced.
